# The Equity Equation

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-the-equity-equation`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: July 2007
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-the-equity-equation

## Summary

Paul Graham introduces a mathematical framework, the 'Equity Equation' (1/(1-n)), to help startup founders evaluate whether trading equity for capital or talent is financially beneficial. The formula determines if the value added by an investor or employee sufficiently increases the company's total worth to offset the dilution of the founders' shares.

## Key Ideas

- The decision to trade equity should be based on whether the trade increases the value of the remaining shares.
- The formula 1/(1-n) calculates the minimum improvement in company outcome required to break even on an equity trade.
- Top-tier VC firms like Sequoia are often a bargain because their brand value improves a startup's prospects by more than the equity they take.
- Hiring decisions can be modeled by calculating the equity equivalent of salary and overhead costs.
- Early employees should ideally accept lower salaries to retain more equity, as salary costs directly compete with stock grants.

## Notable Claims

- Trading equity is a good deal if the trade improves the company's average outcome by more than 1/(1-n). Evidence: Mathematical derivation showing that the remaining (100-n)% of the company must be worth more than the original 100%.
- Top-tier VC funding is often a bargain for the average startup. Evidence: The brand value and network effects of firms like Sequoia often improve a startup's prospects by significantly more than the 30% equity they typically take.

## Quotes

> You should give up n% of your company if what you trade it for improves your average outcome enough that the (100 - n)% you have left is worth more than the whole company was before.
> You should always feel richer after trading equity.
> I'm not claiming that stock grants can now be reduced to a formula. Ultimately you always have to guess. But at least know what you're guessing.
