# An Alternative Theory of Unions

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that the mid-twentieth-century 'golden age' of high-paying union manufacturing jobs was not the result of heroic labor organization, but rather a temporary economic aberration caused by rapid industrial growth, where companies overpaid for labor as a form of infrastructure to ensure speed and reliability.

## Key Ideas

- High-paying union jobs in the mid-20th century were a byproduct of rapid industrial growth, similar to the inflated salaries seen during the late 90s Internet Bubble.
- Companies in growth phases prioritize speed and reliability over cost efficiency, leading them to overpay for resources like labor or servers.
- The decline of unions is not due to a lack of moral courage in modern workers, but a shift in economic conditions where companies no longer need to overpay for labor to maintain growth.
- Unions are compared to web consulting firms like Razorfish during the dot-com era: temporary beneficiaries of an inefficient, high-growth market.

## Topics

- [[topics/economic-aberrations|Economic Aberrations]]: The concept that certain historical periods of high wages are temporary anomalies caused by specific market conditions rather than permanent structural shifts.

## Notable Claims

- Mid-twentieth-century manufacturing unions were not the primary drivers of high wages, but rather beneficiaries of a high-growth market. Evidence: Companies in that era prioritized rapid expansion and reliability over cost-efficiency, similar to how Internet startups prioritize speed over server costs.
- The decline of unions is not due to a decline in moral character or courage among workers. Evidence: It is a rational market response to the end of a specific era of rapid industrial consolidation and growth.

## Quotes
