# A New Approach To AI Models

## Summary

Karan Gaul, CEO of Cartisia, discusses his company's mission to advance AI architectures beyond the limitations of Transformers, focusing on 'spacebased models' (SSMs) inspired by human intelligence. Cartisia, a YC company founded by Stanford PhDs, aims to build models for voice applications, viewing multimodality as a core challenge. Gaul explains that intelligence involves compression and abstraction, which SSMs handle better than retrieval-oriented Transformers. He emphasizes that solving audio-text problems provides a foundational 'recipe' for other multimodal domains like robotics and video. The company's product-driven approach, exemplified by developing AI call center agents, ensures research is grounded in real-world impact and intellectual honesty, contrasting with academic research's broader exploratory nature.

## Key Ideas

- Cartisia is commercializing architecture research from Stanford, focusing on voice applications.
- Traditional machine learning architectures, like Transformers, are limited in achieving human-like intelligence due to challenges with long context, multimodality, and interaction.
- Spacebased Models (SSMs) offer a different approach, emphasizing 'compression' and 'abstraction' as fundamental to intelligence, contrasting with Transformers' 'retrieval-oriented' nature.
- Multimodality is defined broadly as mapping signals to discrete symbols (e.g., audio to text), and solving this for audio-text can provide a transferable 'recipe' for other multimodal domains like video and robotics.
- Cartisia aims to 'get rid of tokens' and enable models to learn raw signal representations internally, end-to-end.
- Product development, such as building AI call center agents, drives discipline and intellectual honesty in research by forcing validation against real-world user needs rather than just novelty.
- Research-driven companies, like all startups, should adhere to 'laws of startup gravity' and maintain a focused vision, unlike the broader exploration common in academic labs.

## Notable Claims

- The Transformer paradigm has fundamental limitations for building AI closer to human intelligence, especially concerning long context and multimodality. Evidence: Karan Gaul states that 'the transformer paradigm because of you know limitations in in the actual architecture itself would not be the right one to build something that's closer to human intelligence.'
- Intelligence is fundamentally about compression and abstraction of information. Evidence: Gaul states, 'one thing that we think a lot about is the this idea of compression as being sort of a very fundamental primitive for intelligence.'
- Transformers are 'context window machines' and 'retrieval oriented machines' that struggle with true compression and abstraction. Evidence: Gaul describes Transformers as being like 'a raw text file' compared to a 'zipped version' for SSMs, and that they are 'fundamentally limited by their inability to model and compress compress representations in this way.'
- Solving the audio-text multimodal problem in the right way provides a 'recipe' for solving all other signal-meets-text problems (e.g., video, robotics). Evidence: Gaul asserts, 'if you can solve one in the right way you can solve all of them. So that's one of the core beliefs we have around how we do our research.'
- Product development drives intellectual honesty and discipline in research. Evidence: Gaul explains that customers 'don't care about the architecture' but 'expect the best version of the product,' which 'drives a lot of honesty into research because it means that like you are going to run experiments to prove that your product or your approach is better for the end user.'
