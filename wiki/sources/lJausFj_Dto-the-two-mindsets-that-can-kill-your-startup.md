# The Two Mindsets That Can KILL Your Startup

## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell of Y Combinator discuss the two dangerous extremes of startup mindsets: excessive pessimism and excessive optimism. They argue that successful founders must maintain a balance, holding the cognitive dissonance of acknowledging harsh realities while maintaining the belief necessary to execute and lead.

## Key Ideas

- Founders must balance optimism (to lead and persist) with pessimism (to remain grounded in reality and identify risks).
- Excessive pessimism is characterized by overreacting to single negative events, such as a bad customer call or a missed goal, leading to a desire to quit.
- Excessive optimism, or 'magical thinking,' involves ignoring reality, believing in non-causal success, and expecting external factors or hires to solve fundamental business problems.
- Employees lose trust in leaders who are untethered from reality, as they feel like passengers in a car driven by someone insane.
- Cognitive dissonance is a necessary skill for founders; they must be able to hold the reality of a crisis while simultaneously executing with high energy.
- Pessimism is often curable through expectation setting, whereas excessive optimism is harder to correct because it involves a fundamental misunderstanding of how the world works.

## Topics

- [[topics/founder-psychology|Founder Psychology]]: The mental states and emotional regulation required to navigate the challenges of building a startup.
- [[topics/magical-thinking|Magical Thinking]]: A cognitive bias where founders believe success will come from non-causal, dramatic, or 'movie-like' events rather than hard work and reality-based execution.
- [[topics/cognitive-dissonance|Cognitive Dissonance]]: The ability to hold two conflicting thoughts—such as 'my company is in crisis' and 'I will succeed anyway'—simultaneously without losing focus.

## Notable Claims

- One negative signal (like a bad investor meeting) does not mean a startup idea is invalid. Evidence: Founders often ascribe god-like predictive powers to individuals who may not be the right customer or may not understand the business.
- Hiring a salesperson or marketer will not solve fundamental product-market fit issues. Evidence: Founders often use hiring as a form of magical thinking to avoid doing the hard work of finding customers themselves.
