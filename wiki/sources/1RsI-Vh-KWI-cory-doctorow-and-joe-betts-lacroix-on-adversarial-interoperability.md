# Cory Doctorow and Joe Betts-Lacroix on Adversarial Interoperability

## Summary

Cory Doctorow and Joe Betts-Lacroix discuss the concept of 'adversarial interoperability'—the practice of building products that plug into existing platforms without the incumbent's permission—as a vital mechanism for innovation, market competition, and breaking up digital monopolies.

## Key Ideas

- Adversarial interoperability is a historical driver of technological progress, allowing new entrants to challenge dominant incumbents.
- Modern legal 'thickets' like the DMCA, CFAA, and software patents are used by monopolies to create 'kill zones' that prevent competition.
- The 'peak indifference' model suggests that as problems (like surveillance or monopoly abuse) worsen, public demand for solutions will naturally increase.
- Conspiracy theories are rising because the 'truth-seeking' institutions (regulators, experts) have been suborned by corporate interests, leading to an epistemological crisis.
- Founders should view science fiction as a menu of choices for the future, helping them identify and build toward more resilient, decentralized systems.
- The Ninth Circuit's 'hiQ vs. LinkedIn' decision provides a potential legal opening for companies to use scraping to challenge platform monopolies.

## Topics

- [[topics/adversarial-interoperability|Adversarial Interoperability]]: The practice of creating products that interoperate with existing systems without the permission of the system owner.
- [[topics/regulatory-capture|Regulatory Capture]]: The process by which regulatory agencies become dominated by the industries they are supposed to regulate.
- [[topics/copyright-reform|Copyright Reform]]: The argument that copyright law should be restricted to industrial contexts and not applied to everyday user behavior.
- [[topics/epistemological-crisis|Epistemological Crisis]]: The breakdown of shared truth and trust in institutions due to corruption and market concentration.

## Notable Claims

- The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) is being misused to criminalize Terms of Service violations. Evidence: Legal actions by companies like Blizzard and Facebook to classify scraping as hacking.
- Large tech monopolies are using 'state-like' duties to justify their size and prevent competition. Evidence: Facebook's argument that it needs to be massive to police content effectively.
- The rise of conspiracy theories is a symptom of institutional corruption. Evidence: When regulatory processes are suborned by industry, people lose faith in official truth-seeking mechanisms.
