# After the Ladder

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the shift from the traditional 'corporate ladder' career model to the modern startup-centric approach, arguing that while the transition appears to increase economic inequality, it is actually a shift in how compensation and risk are structured.

## Key Ideas

- The traditional corporate ladder provided job security and seniority-based rewards that functioned like an annuity.
- Economic statistics are misleading because they fail to account for the monetary value of job security and 'sinecures'.
- The 1980s corporate takeover era exposed the hidden debt companies owed to employees, leading to the decay of the ladder.
- The modern startup model is more liquid and efficient, though it shifts the burden of risk from the corporation to the individual.
- Promoting employees to management as a reward for past service often results in poor management and corporate inefficiency.

## Notable Claims

- Economic statistics are misleading because they ignore the value of safe jobs. Evidence: Safe jobs function as annuities, which are not captured in standard income or wealth metrics.
- The corporate ladder was a form of implicit debt. Evidence: Companies were profitable to carve up in the 1980s because they had not formally acknowledged their obligation to reward long-term employees with high-paying executive roles.
