## Summary

Morgan DeBaun, founder of Blavity, discusses her journey from a corporate product manager at Intuit to building the largest media company and lifestyle brand for Black millennials. She shares insights on bootstrapping, the importance of community-driven content, the necessity of 'killing your darlings' to iterate quickly, and the challenges of fundraising as a Black female founder.

## Key Ideas

- Focus on community needs rather than personal editorial vision to drive engagement.
- Fail fast and be agnostic about the medium (e.g., switching from video to blog) to find product-market fit.
- Bootstrap until you have undeniable data that forces investors to take notice.
- Build a 'tribe' of supporters to overcome the lack of representation and validation in the startup ecosystem.
- Empower creators by providing resources and platforms, which in turn scales the company's own growth.

## Notable Claims

- Black people and people of color over-index in television consumption and are major content creators. Evidence: DeBaun cites this as a core insight for the initial Blavity content strategy.
- The traditional media landscape was failing to serve Black millennials. Evidence: DeBaun notes that existing outlets were not mobile-first or digital-first and ignored the specific interests of the demographic.
- Bootstrapping allows for more control and forces a focus on real metrics. Evidence: DeBaun describes bootstrapping for a year, which forced her to prove the business model before seeking outside investment.
