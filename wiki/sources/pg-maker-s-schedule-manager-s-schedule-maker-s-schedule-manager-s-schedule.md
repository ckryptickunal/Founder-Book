# Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the fundamental conflict between two distinct ways of organizing time: the 'manager's schedule,' which operates in one-hour increments, and the 'maker's schedule,' which requires long, uninterrupted blocks of time for creative work. He argues that meetings are disproportionately costly for makers and suggests strategies for managing this friction.

## Key Ideas

- The manager's schedule is designed for command and coordination, utilizing one-hour slots.
- The maker's schedule is designed for deep, creative work, requiring half-day or longer blocks.
- Meetings act as 'exceptions' for makers, breaking their workflow and destroying the morale needed for ambitious projects.
- Investors and executives typically operate on the manager's schedule, often failing to realize the cost their meeting requests impose on makers.
- Strategies like 'office hours' or splitting the day into distinct 'maker' and 'manager' blocks can mitigate scheduling conflicts.

## Notable Claims

- A single meeting can destroy an entire afternoon for a maker. Evidence: Meetings break up time into chunks too small for complex tasks and require a mental mode switch that disrupts deep work.
- Speculative meetings are effectively free for managers but extremely costly for makers. Evidence: Managers have empty slots in their hourly schedules, whereas makers lose the momentum required for ambitious projects.
