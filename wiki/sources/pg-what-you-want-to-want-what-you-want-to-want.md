# What You (Want to)* Want

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the philosophical tension between physical determinism and the human experience of free will, proposing that while we can influence our desires, we eventually reach a level of 'want' that is outside our control.

## Key Ideas

- The conflict between being a physical machine and the feeling of agency.
- The concept of higher-order desires (wanting to want).
- The recursive nature of desires: we can influence our desires, but the chain of control eventually terminates.
- The use of mathematical limits to describe the boundary of human self-control.

## Topics

- [[topics/free-will|Free Will]]: The philosophical debate regarding whether human actions are determined by physical laws or autonomous choice.
- [[topics/higher-order-desires|Higher-Order Desires]]: The psychological and philosophical concept of having desires about one's own desires.
- [[topics/determinism|Determinism]]: The view that all events, including human action, are ultimately determined by causes external to the will.

## Notable Claims

- Randomness does not solve the problem of free will. Evidence: Graham argues that randomness destroys the 'ghost in the machine' just as effectively as determinism.
- Human control over desires is limited by a recursive boundary. Evidence: By adding 'want to' layers, one eventually reaches a base desire that is not under conscious control.

## Quotes

> You can do what you want, but you can't want what you want.
> Ultimately you get back to a want that you don't control.
> There is some n such that you don't control your nth-order desires.
