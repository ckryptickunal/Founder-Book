# What Microsoft Is this the Altair Basic of?

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-what-microsoft-is-this-the-altair-basic-of`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: February 2015
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-what-microsoft-is-this-the-altair-basic-of

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the counterintuitive nature of successful startups, noting that many industry giants began as ideas that seemed trivial, 'lame,' or even ridiculous to observers at the time.

## Key Ideas

- Successful startups often appear insignificant or foolish at their inception.
- Founders are often driven by instinct rather than a fully articulated long-term vision.
- The 'What Microsoft is this the Altair Basic of?' framework helps investors and founders identify the hidden potential in seemingly small or niche ideas.
- Startups may have multiple potential growth paths, and founders should prioritize the one that aligns with their current excitement.

## Entities

- [[entities/microsoft|Microsoft]] (company): A global technology giant that began with a Basic interpreter for the Altair.
- [[entities/altair|Altair]] (product): An early microcomputer kit.
- [[entities/paul-graham|Paul Graham]] (person): Venture capitalist and essayist.

## Topics

- [[topics/startup-evaluation|Startup Evaluation]]: The process of looking past initial impressions to find latent potential in early-stage business ideas.
- [[topics/founder-instinct|Founder Instinct]]: The role of intuition in identifying market gaps before they are obvious to the general public.

## Notable Claims

- Most successful companies appear 'lame' or ridiculous when they first launch. Evidence: Historical examples like Microsoft (Altair Basic), Airbnb (airbeds), and Facebook (stalking site) were initially dismissed.
- Founders often do not know the full potential of their own ideas at the start. Evidence: Graham notes that founders are often attracted to ideas by instinct rather than a clear, articulated roadmap.

## Quotes

> One of the most valuable exercises you can try if you want to understand startups is to look at the most successful companies and explain why they were not as lame as they seemed when they first launched.
> When I encounter a startup with a lame-sounding idea, I ask 'What Microsoft is this the Altair Basic of?'
