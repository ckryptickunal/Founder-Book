## Summary

Tim Brady, a Y Combinator partner and early Yahoo employee, explains that company culture is essentially the set of behaviors that guide employees when explicit instructions are absent. He argues that founders should define these behaviors early, as the first 20 employees establish the 'cultural DNA' that will scale with the company.

## Key Ideas

- Culture is defined as the set of behaviors that inform employees how to act when not explicitly told.
- The first 20 employees are critical because they become the primary recruiters and trainers for future hires.
- Founders should be proud of the problem they are solving to maintain energy during difficult times.
- A long-term, inspiring vision should focus on the purpose of the work rather than the specific product.
- Company values should be a short, informal list used as a filter during the hiring process.
- Culture should be externally focused (customer-centric) rather than just internally focused.
- Diversity of opinion is essential for creativity and problem-solving and must be prioritized from the start.

## Notable Claims

- Culture is behavior, not just a set of written values. Evidence: The speaker defines culture as the implicit set of behaviors that inform employees how to act when not explicitly directed.
- Internally focused cultures can lead to negative outcomes. Evidence: The speaker cites Facebook's 'move fast and break things' as a culture that lacked the external guardrails to prevent privacy violations.
- Diversity programs cannot be successfully retrofitted at scale. Evidence: The speaker argues that if a company waits until it has 100 employees to start a diversity program, it is already too late.
