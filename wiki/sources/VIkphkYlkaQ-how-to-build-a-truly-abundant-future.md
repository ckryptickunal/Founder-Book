# How To Build A Truly Abundant Future

## Summary

Derek Thompson, staff writer at The Atlantic and co-author of 'Abundance', discusses his book's vision for a future defined by technological and scientific prosperity. He argues that modern bureaucracy and a shift toward 'anti-growth' sentiment since the 1960s have stifled innovation, and advocates for a return to an 'invention agenda' that prioritizes outcomes over process, utilizing mechanisms like pull funding and institutional reform.

## Key Ideas

- The 'Abundance Agenda' focuses on overcoming bureaucratic inertia to build housing, clean energy, and transformative technologies.
- Modern society suffers from a 'vibe shift' that occurred in the 1960s and 70s, turning a growth-oriented culture into an anti-growth, litigation-heavy environment.
- Bureaucracy often decentralizes care to the point where focus on the actual outcome is lost, as seen in the failure of rural broadband and high-speed rail projects.
- Institutional renewal is a responsibility of each generation; we must update systems built 50 years ago to solve 21st-century problems.
- Push funding (subsidies) should be supplemented by pull funding (prizes/advanced market commitments) to encourage competition and innovation.
- Science policy should be experimental, moving away from rigid, paperwork-heavy grant processes toward models like 'golden tickets' and long-term funding for high-risk research.

- [[topics/bureaucratic-reform|Bureaucratic Reform]]: The need to modernize 80-year-old institutions like the NIH to prioritize outcomes and speed over process.
- [[topics/metascience|Metascience]]: The study of how scientific discovery works and the importance of running experiments on the scientific process itself.
- [[topics/pull-funding|Pull Funding]]: A policy mechanism that rewards successful outcomes rather than subsidizing inputs, increasing competition and efficiency.

## Notable Claims

- The US has shifted from a growth-oriented machine to an anti-growth machine since the 1960s. Evidence: The rise of environmental legislation (NEPA, Clean Air Act) and adversarial legalism that makes physical infrastructure projects nearly impossible to complete.
- Scientists currently spend 40% of their time on paperwork. Evidence: Cited as a major inefficiency in the current NIH grant-based funding model.
- Advanced Market Commitments (AMCs) accelerate innovation. Evidence: Operation Warp Speed's success in delivering vaccines in 10 months compared to the typical 9-10 year timeline.
