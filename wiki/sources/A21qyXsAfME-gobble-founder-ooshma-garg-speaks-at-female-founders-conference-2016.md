## Summary

Ooshma Garg, founder and CEO of Gobble, shares the challenging five-year journey of building her company, detailing the 'trough of sorrow' and the iterative process of finding product-market fit for their 10-minute dinner kits.

## Key Ideas

- Startups often experience a 'trough of sorrow' between the initial launch and achieving long-term success.
- Product-market fit is often found through iterative experimentation and listening to customer feedback rather than just following investor advice.
- The 'Mise en place' concept from professional kitchens was the key innovation that allowed Gobble to succeed as a dinner kit service.
- Grit is defined as an internal core strength that keeps a founder going when others lose faith.
- A company's mission should remain constant even when the product or business model needs to pivot.
- Rejection from investors is not permanent; maintaining grace allows for future partnerships once the business proves its value.

## Notable Claims

- The startup journey is rarely a straight line and often involves a 'trough of sorrow' after the initial launch. Evidence: Comparison of Gobble's revenue graph to Airbnb's early growth trajectory.
- The 'Mise en place' concept was the breakthrough that made the product viable for families. Evidence: Customer feedback and growth metrics after shifting to pre-prepped ingredients.
- Joining Y Combinator saved the company from bankruptcy. Evidence: The company was at the point of being unable to meet payroll before receiving YC funding.
