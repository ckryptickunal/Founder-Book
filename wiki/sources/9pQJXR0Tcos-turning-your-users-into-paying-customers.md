# Turning Your Users Into Paying Customers

## Summary

The partners at Y Combinator discuss the common tendency for startup founders to avoid charging for their products, often due to fear, perfectionism, or a misunderstanding of market dynamics. They argue that charging is essential for validating product-market fit and that pricing is a dynamic, iterative process rather than a permanent decision.

## Key Ideas

- Founders often avoid charging to optimize for user feedback, but this delays critical validation.
- Pricing is not permanent; it can be changed, tested, and iterated upon just like product features.
- Charging for a product is a crucial signal of value; free pilots with large companies often fail to prove that a customer is willing to spend capital or political effort.
- The best feedback on a product often occurs in the three seconds after a customer hears the price.
- Freemium and open-core models are valid strategies only if they are intentional, tracked, and have a clear path to monetization.
- Founders should not fear 'getting it wrong' with pricing; they can always grandfather early users into older plans.

## Notable Claims

- The best feedback you get about your product is in the three seconds after you tell them the price. Evidence: The immediate facial expression and reaction of a potential customer provides the most honest assessment of perceived value.
- You can change your pricing every week if you want to. Evidence: Successful startups frequently test and adjust pricing, similar to how they A/B test product features.
- Not charging for a product can act as an anti-signal to enterprise customers. Evidence: Large companies may doubt the legitimacy or support capabilities of a vendor that does not charge for their software.
