## Summary

The founders of RevenueCat, Jacob and Miguel, discuss their journey from Y Combinator in 2018 to building a successful developer-focused SaaS company that simplifies in-app purchases and subscription management across mobile platforms.

## Key Ideas

- Solving a specific, painful problem for developers (in-app purchase infrastructure) is a strong foundation for a startup.
- Product-market fit is often signaled by organic growth and the inability to keep up with demand, rather than just survey results.
- Selling to developers requires a technical, bottom-up approach rather than traditional top-down sales.
- Transparency and consistent metrics are crucial for maintaining discipline and alignment in a distributed team.
- Remote work allows for a broader talent pool and provides operational advantages, though it requires intentional communication systems.
- Co-founder relationships require mutual respect, shared philosophy, and a commitment to not quitting first.

## Notable Claims

- In-app purchases are notoriously difficult to implement and maintain across different platforms. Evidence: The founders spent significant engineering resources on this at previous companies before building RevenueCat.
- Developer-focused products are best sold by making them easy to adopt rather than using traditional top-down sales. Evidence: Developers prefer technical solutions that they can pull into their projects independently.
- The Apple vs. Epic legal ruling had a minimal impact on the actual economics of in-app purchases. Evidence: RevenueCat's internal measurements show only a slightly positive, minimal change in market dynamics.
