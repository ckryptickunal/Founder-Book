# How to Disagree

## Summary

Paul Graham proposes a 'disagreement hierarchy' to categorize the quality of arguments, ranging from low-level name-calling to high-level refutation of central points, arguing that better disagreement leads to more productive and civil discourse.

## Key Ideas

- The internet has increased the frequency of disagreement, which can lead to increased hostility.
- Disagreement can be categorized into a seven-level hierarchy (DH0 to DH6).
- Higher levels of the hierarchy (refutation) are more convincing and less mean-spirited than lower levels (name-calling, ad hominem).
- Classifying arguments helps readers identify intellectual dishonesty and encourages writers to improve the quality of their discourse.
- Moving up the hierarchy makes conversations more productive and improves the happiness of participants.

## Topics

- [[topics/disagreement-hierarchy|Disagreement Hierarchy]]: A framework for evaluating the quality of arguments, moving from DH0 (name-calling) to DH6 (refuting the central point).
- [[topics/intellectual-honesty|Intellectual Honesty]]: The practice of engaging with the substance of an argument rather than attacking the writer or tone.
- [[topics/online-discourse|Online Discourse]]: The structural changes in communication brought by the web and the resulting increase in public disagreement.

## Notable Claims

- Higher levels of the disagreement hierarchy are less mean-spirited than lower levels. Evidence: Observation of conversational patterns where substantive arguments require less hostility than ad hominem attacks.
- Criticizing tone is a weak form of disagreement. Evidence: Tone is subjective and secondary to the factual accuracy of an argument.
- Refutation is the most effective form of disagreement. Evidence: It requires engaging directly with the author's central point using evidence and reasoning.
