# Startup = Growth

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-startup-growth`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: September 2012
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-startup-growth

## Summary

Paul Graham defines a startup as a company specifically designed for rapid growth, distinguishing it from traditional small businesses. He argues that growth should serve as a 'compass' for all decision-making, transforming the startup process into an optimization problem that helps founders navigate uncertainty and discover valuable, scalable ideas.

## Key Ideas

- A startup is defined by its intent and capacity for rapid growth, not by its age, industry, or funding status.
- Growth acts as a compass; if a decision contributes to growth, it is generally the right one.
- Successful startups often emerge from 'blind spots' where founders solve problems that seem niche but are actually precursors to larger market needs.
- Startups follow an S-curve: slow initial exploration, rapid ascent, and eventual stabilization.
- Weekly growth rate (e.g., 5-7%) is the most critical metric for early-stage startups to measure and optimize.
- The high failure rate of startups is a rational outcome of their high-risk, high-reward nature, similar to other 'high-beta' vocations like acting.
- Investors prefer startups because capital gains are easier to oversee than dividend-based returns from private companies.

## Notable Claims

- A startup is defined solely by its design for rapid growth. Evidence: Everything else associated with startups, such as venture funding or technology, follows from the requirement for growth.
- Weekly growth rate is the best metric for early-stage startups. Evidence: It provides frequent feedback, allows for rapid iteration, and serves as a clear indicator of whether the company is on the right path.
- Startups are 'economic research scientists'. Evidence: Founders are committing to solve problems that are not yet fully understood or proven to be soluble, similar to scientific discovery.
