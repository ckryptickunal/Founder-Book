# Six Principles for Making New Things

## Summary

Paul Graham outlines a design philosophy for innovation based on six principles: finding simple solutions to overlooked problems, focusing on actual needs, delivering informally, and iterating rapidly from a crude initial version. He argues that this approach often triggers initial contempt from critics because it defies conventional expectations of complexity and professionalism, yet it is highly effective for long-term success.

## Key Ideas

- Innovation often looks like a failure or a joke at the beginning because it ignores conventional markers of 'seriousness'.
- Simple solutions to overlooked problems are more valuable than complex solutions to popular problems.
- Informal delivery saves effort and prevents the creator from being fooled by their own marketing.
- Launching a crude version 1 allows for rapid iteration and learning from real-world usage.
- Contrarian approaches are necessary because they avoid competition and allow for the discovery of new, non-obvious truths.

## Topics

- [[topics/design-philosophy|Design Philosophy]]: A set of principles for creating new things that prioritizes simplicity, utility, and rapid iteration over superficial complexity.
- [[topics/contrarianism|Contrarianism]]: The strategy of doing things that seem wrong to the average person to gain a competitive advantage.
- [[topics/startup-methodology|Startup Methodology]]: The practice of launching early, ignoring conventional business norms, and focusing on solving actual user problems.

## Notable Claims

- The most successful innovations often appear 'flimsy' or 'laughable' to experts when first introduced. Evidence: Historical reactions to Viaweb, Y Combinator, and Reddit.
- Working on overlooked problems provides a competitive advantage. Evidence: Less competition exists in areas that most people deem unimportant.
- Nature's feedback is more powerful than a creator's initial vision. Evidence: Rapid iteration based on user feedback allows products to evolve in ways the creator could not have predicted.

## Quotes

> Don't worry about trying to look corporate; the product is what wins in the long term.
> Ideas like that are all around you. But you ignore them because they look wrong.
