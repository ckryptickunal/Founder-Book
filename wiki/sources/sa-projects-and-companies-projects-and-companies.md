# Projects and Companies

## Summary

Sam Altman argues that startups should maintain a 'project' mindset for as long as possible to avoid the stifling expectations and bureaucratic distractions that come with being labeled a 'company'.

## Key Ideas

- Treating a startup as a company too early leads to 'pretend work' like legal and administrative tasks instead of product development.
- Projects allow for experimentation and the pursuit of 'crazy' ideas that would be dismissed if presented as a formal company.
- High initial funding and press coverage often create expectations that kill promising, unconventional ideas.
- Many successful companies like Google, Facebook, and Airbnb began as low-stakes side projects.
- The primary risk of the 'project' mindset is the potential for founders to use it as an excuse for lack of discipline or effort.

## Notable Claims

- Thinking of yourself as a company leads to 'pretend work' that kills product development. Evidence: Founders focus on lawyers, conferences, and finance rather than building the product.
- The best companies start as ideas that don't sound very good. Evidence: Historical examples like Google, Facebook, and Airbnb all began as inconsequential side projects.

## Quotes

> It’s far better to be thought of—and to think of yourself—as a project than a company for as long as possible.
> The risk of seeming stupid when something is just a project is almost zero, and no one cares if you fail.
> Great companies often start as projects.
