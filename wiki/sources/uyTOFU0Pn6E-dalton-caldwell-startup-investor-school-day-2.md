## Summary

Dalton Caldwell, a partner at Y Combinator, provides a practical guide for startup investors on developing a structured process for evaluating and meeting with founders. He emphasizes the importance of personal conviction, avoiding groupthink, and maintaining professional, founder-friendly communication throughout the investment lifecycle.

## Key Ideas

- Investors should develop a clear, personal process funnel for evaluating startups rather than relying on groupthink or following other investors.
- The face-to-face meeting is the most critical component of the investment decision-making process.
- Investors should define their own criteria (team, market, traction, expertise) and stick to them to avoid 'wishy-washy' decision-making.
- Once an investor commits to a deal, they must follow through; backing out is a major reputational risk.
- Avoid 'Fairweather' investing—do not make your investment contingent on the founder successfully raising the rest of the round from others.
- Treat the investor meeting as a job interview where the founder is evaluating you as much as you are evaluating them.
- If you are not a 'yes,' you are a 'no.' Avoid ghosting or leading founders on with endless meetings.

## Notable Claims

- The best investment decisions are often contrarian or unfashionable. Evidence: Reference to early skepticism regarding Airbnb's 'air beds' model.
- Investors who act as 'fairweather friends' by waiting for others to invest first damage their reputation. Evidence: Founders communicate with each other and share negative experiences with investors who lack conviction.
- If you are not a 'yes,' you are a 'no.' Evidence: Ambiguity and 'maybe' land wastes founder time and prevents them from focusing on successful fundraising.
