## Summary

Paul Graham offers advice to computer science undergraduates, emphasizing that becoming a skilled hacker requires working on hard, engaging projects rather than just following class assignments. He discusses the role of math, the importance of choosing worthwhile fields of study, the realities of graduate school, and the necessity of pursuing work one genuinely loves.

## Key Ideas

- To become a good hacker, focus on programming and solving hard, engaging problems.
- Academic assignments are often less valuable than self-directed projects.
- Learn by finding smart people and working on projects that interest you.
- Math is valuable as a source of metaphors and rigorous thinking, even if not always directly applicable to coding.
- Avoid fields influenced by intellectual fashion; focus on hard sciences, engineering, and history.
- Graduate school is useful for networking and gaining confidence, but the dissertation process is often grueling.
- Admissions for PhD programs are driven by personal recommendations from research professors, not just grades.

## Notable Claims

- The amount of math needed for most hacking is significantly less than what university departments claim. Evidence: Most hacking requires only high school math and basic theory of computation concepts.
- Social sciences are often influenced by intellectual fashion and are less valuable than hard sciences. Evidence: Physicists can teach colleagues from 100 years ago, whereas psychologists would likely just argue ideologically.
- PhD admissions are primarily driven by recommendations from known professors. Evidence: The author cites his network of CS professor friends who prioritize personal knowledge of the recommender.

## Quotes

> Try to learn something about everything and everything about something.
> It's hard to trick professors into letting you into grad school, and impossible to trick problems into letting you solve them.
> The best thing you can do in college, whether you want to get into grad school or just be good at hacking, is figure out what you truly like.
