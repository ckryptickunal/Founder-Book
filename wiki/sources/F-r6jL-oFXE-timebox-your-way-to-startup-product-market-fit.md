# Timebox your way to startup product market fit

## Summary

Garry Tan explains the critical importance of 'timeboxing' in startup development to achieve product-market fit. He argues that founders often fail by repeating ineffective strategies, and suggests that by setting strict time limits on hypotheses, founders can iterate faster, conserve runway, and systematically test 'lead bullets' to find scalable growth.

## Key Ideas

- Founders have a finite number of attempts to find product-market fit; timeboxing maximizes the number of tries.
- A product-market hypothesis consists of a specific set of people and a specific problem to solve for them.
- There are no 'silver bullets' in customer acquisition, only 'lead bullets'—a variety of tactics that must be tested.
- The 'pile of pilots' problem occurs when startups secure initial interest but fail to expand or retain users, indicating a lack of true product-market fit.
- If a hypothesis is proven false by time, founders must have the discipline to quit and pivot to a new idea rather than repeating the same failed actions.

## Topics

- [[topics/timeboxing|Timeboxing]]: A management technique of setting a fixed time limit for a specific task or hypothesis to prevent wasting resources on ineffective strategies.
- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The state where a product satisfies a strong market demand and achieves scalable, sustainable growth.
- [[topics/customer-acquisition|Customer Acquisition]]: The process of bringing new users to a product, which requires testing multiple channels like SEO, SEM, and partnerships.
- [[topics/the-pile-of-pilots-problem|The Pile of Pilots Problem]]: A failure mode in B2B startups where initial pilots are secured but fail to convert into long-term, expanding contracts.

## Notable Claims

- Bad ideas are zeroes that nullify perfect execution. Evidence: Founders with great engineering and marketing often fail if the underlying product-market hypothesis is fundamentally flawed.
- Timeboxing prevents the waste of critical startup runway. Evidence: By setting a deadline for a hypothesis, founders avoid the 'insanity' of repeating failed tactics indefinitely.
