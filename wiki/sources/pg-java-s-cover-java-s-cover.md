# Java's Cover

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the concept of 'hacker's radar'—an intuitive ability to judge technology by its outward signs—using Java as a case study. He argues that while he has not used Java, its marketing, design philosophy, and corporate origins exhibit patterns typical of failed or mediocre technologies, contrasting them with successful 'hacker-designed' languages like C, Perl, and Lisp.

## Key Ideas

- Experienced programmers develop an intuitive 'radar' for identifying good versus bad technology without needing to master every tool.
- Languages designed by their creators for their own use (C, Perl, Lisp) tend to be superior to those designed by committees for 'other people' (Cobol, Ada, Java).
- Aggressive marketing and hype are often signs of a weak product; true standards emerge organically.
- Technology designed to limit programmer capability for the sake of large organizations often fails to attract the best talent.
- The alignment of a technology with bureaucratic entities like the DoD is often a negative indicator for its long-term success among hackers.

## Notable Claims

- Languages designed for the creator's own use are superior to those designed for other people. Evidence: Historical success of C, Perl, and Lisp compared to the failure or mediocrity of Cobol, PL/I, and Ada.
- Java was designed to undermine Microsoft rather than to solve programming problems. Evidence: The context of Sun Microsystems' business strategy and market position at the time.
- The DoD's endorsement of a programming language is a sign of its likely failure among hackers. Evidence: The historical example of the DoD's preference for Ada.

## Quotes
