# How to Survive the Next Tech Revolution (w/ Brett Gibson)

## Summary

Garry Tan and Brett Gibson discuss the evolution of software engineering, the necessity of rewriting products as technology matures, and the parallels between early crypto infrastructure and current AI development. They emphasize the importance of building for user value, the role of generalist engineers in early-stage startups, and the necessity of rigorous testing in high-stakes technical domains.

## Key Ideas

- The 'v2' rewrite is often necessary as underlying infrastructure and APIs commoditize, allowing founders to replace custom-built solutions with off-the-shelf tools.
- Engineering for AI requires navigating non-deterministic systems, which necessitates new 'first-generation' hacks and best practices.
- Successful early-stage startups often require generalist engineers who can bridge the gap between front-end, API, and back-end development.
- Investing in software testing early, even when under time pressure, is a critical factor in long-term success for complex technical products.
- The North Star for any technical decision should be the user experience, not the technology itself.
- Founders and investors succeed by holding contrarian beliefs that eventually become reality.

## Notable Claims

- Rewriting software is easier and more efficient after the first iteration due to improved tooling and open-source APIs. Evidence: The transition from Posterous to Post Haven, where custom email authentication was replaced by Mailgun.
- Investing heavily in core software tests early, even if it slightly slows initial development, is a net positive for long-term velocity. Evidence: Lessons learned from the development of Bison Trails in the high-stakes crypto custody space.
- Large language models are fundamentally non-deterministic, which is a key factor in their utility. Evidence: The inherent nature of generative AI compared to traditional deterministic software engineering.
