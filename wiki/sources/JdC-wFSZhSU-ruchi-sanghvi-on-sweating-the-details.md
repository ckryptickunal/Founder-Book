# Ruchi Sanghvi on Sweating the Details

## Summary

Ruchi Sanghvi discusses her transition from Facebook to Dropbox, highlighting the shift in engineering philosophy from 'move fast and break things' to 'sweating the details' due to the critical nature of user data storage.

## Key Ideas

- Engineering values must align with the specific product mission; what works for social media does not work for data storage.
- The 'move fast and break things' mantra is inappropriate for services where reliability and data integrity are paramount.
- Quality assurance for critical software often requires significantly more engineering time than feature development.
- Scaling a company effectively often requires prioritizing recruiting over direct product work when teams are spread too thin.
- Professional titles are less important than the actual impact and quality of the work produced.

## Topics

- [[topics/engineering-culture|Engineering Culture]]: The importance of tailoring development methodologies to the specific risks and goals of a product.
- [[topics/scaling-organizations|Scaling Organizations]]: Strategies for rapid team growth and the necessity of prioritizing recruiting during high-growth phases.
- [[topics/product-quality|Product Quality]]: The trade-off between release speed and the 'polish' required for mission-critical software.

## Notable Claims

- The 'move fast and break things' philosophy is dangerous for file storage products. Evidence: Users store critical life data like wedding photos and PhD theses; data loss is unacceptable.
- The last 10% of polish in software development consumes 50% of engineering time. Evidence: Sanghvi observed this ratio while working on the Dropbox desktop client.

## Quotes

> I realized that values like move fast and break things didn't apply to Dropbox.
> We needed to foster what was unique to Dropbox and at Dropbox that was sweating the details.
> The only thing that mattered was doing what it took to win.
> 10 years from now no one is going to remember that title but they are going to remember you by what you built and its impact on the world.
