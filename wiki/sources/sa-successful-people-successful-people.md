# Successful people

## Metadata

## Summary

Sam Altman explores the hierarchy of success, suggesting that the most impactful founders are driven by mission-oriented visions akin to religions rather than the mere goal of building a company.

## Key Ideas

- Success scales from creating companies to creating countries, and ultimately to creating religions.
- The most successful founders are motivated by a deep mission rather than the desire to start a business.
- Forming a company is often just a pragmatic vehicle for achieving a larger, mission-driven goal.
- Significant, world-changing companies are rarely the result of pivots.

## Entities

- [[entities/sam-altman|Sam Altman]] (person): Author of the essay and President of Y Combinator.
- [[entities/qi-lu|Qi Lu]] (person): Former executive at Microsoft and Baidu.

## Topics

- [[topics/entrepreneurial-motivation|Entrepreneurial Motivation]]: The distinction between building a company for profit versus building one to fulfill a mission-driven vision.
- [[topics/business-strategy|Business Strategy]]: The observation that major, successful companies are typically not the result of pivots but of singular, focused missions.

## Notable Claims

- The most successful founders do not set out to create companies. Evidence: Altman posits that they are driven by a mission that necessitates a company structure as the easiest path to realization.
- Big companies do not come from pivots. Evidence: Altman suggests that the lack of a foundational, mission-driven vision makes pivots less likely to result in massive success.

## Quotes

> Successful people create companies. More successful people create countries. The most successful people create religions.
> The most successful founders do not set out to create companies. They are on a mission to create something closer to a religion.
