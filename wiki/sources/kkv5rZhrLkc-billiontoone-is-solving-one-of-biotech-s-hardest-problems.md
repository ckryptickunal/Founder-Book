# BillionToOne Is Solving One of Biotech’s Hardest Problems

## Summary

BillionToOne is a molecular diagnostics company that has revolutionized genetic testing by using synthetic DNA to remove noise from blood samples, allowing for the detection of rare DNA fragments. Founded by PhD students Oge and David, the company successfully scaled from a small lab bench to a public company with a $4 billion valuation by following a strategic, multi-step roadmap: starting with prenatal genetic testing, moving into late-stage cancer diagnostics, and aiming for early-stage cancer screening.

## Key Ideas

- The 'needle in a haystack' problem in diagnostics involves detecting rare DNA fragments (fetal or tumor) amidst billions of base pairs.
- BillionToOne uses synthetic DNA molecules added to samples before amplification to measure and subtract noise, turning a biological problem into a mathematical one.
- The company employs a 'Tesla-like' phased strategy: starting with a lower-barrier market (prenatal) to generate revenue and resources before tackling more complex, high-stakes markets (oncology).
- Interdisciplinary hiring is a core philosophy; the company seeks individuals who bridge the gap between data science and laboratory chemistry.
- The company utilizes AI and computer vision to automate sample accessioning and processing, allowing for high-throughput operations.

- [[topics/liquid-biopsy|Liquid Biopsy]]: A non-invasive blood test used to detect cancer by identifying tumor DNA circulating in the bloodstream.
- [[topics/cell-free-dna-cfdna|Cell-free DNA (cfDNA)]]: DNA fragments that are shed by tissues (including tumors and fetuses) into the bloodstream.

## Notable Claims

- BillionToOne's technology can detect genetic abnormalities that were previously only detectable via invasive amniocentesis. Evidence: The company uses synthetic DNA to remove amplification noise, allowing for high-accuracy sequencing of rare fetal DNA from maternal blood.
- The company's technology can identify treatment options for cancer patients that traditional tumor biopsies miss. Evidence: A case study of a metastatic colorectal cancer patient who was identified as eligible for immunotherapy through a blood test after a standard tumor biopsy failed to show the marker.
