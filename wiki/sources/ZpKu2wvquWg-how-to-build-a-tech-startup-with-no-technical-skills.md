# How To Build A Tech Startup With No Technical Skills

## Summary

Y Combinator partners Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell explain that for software-based startups, the most critical success factor for non-technical founders is the ability to recruit a world-class technical co-founder. They argue that attempting to build a tech company without a technical partner is a major strategic error and that successful business founders distinguish themselves by their ability to attract and partner with top-tier engineering talent.

## Key Ideas

- The best business founders are defined by their ability to recruit amazing technical co-founders.
- Software startups require technical leadership; outsourcing development or using low-code tools is rarely a viable path to long-term success.
- Founders often self-sabotage by setting unrealistic requirements for co-founders or by failing to ask the best people they know.
- The pitch to a potential co-founder should be an invitation to an 'adventure' and a partnership, not a request for an employee to build a specific idea.
- If you cannot find a technical co-founder, the best move may be to work at an existing startup to expand your network and gain experience.

## Notable Claims

- Software startups that rely on outsourced development or white-label software have a near-zero chance of success. Evidence: Observations of successful companies like DoorDash and Airbnb that relied on internal, high-speed shipping of software.
- Business founders often disqualify potential co-founders by setting arbitrary, high-level experience requirements. Evidence: Founders often demand 10+ years of experience from candidates when the founders themselves are young and inexperienced.
