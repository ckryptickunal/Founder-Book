# Mathematical Approaches to Image Processing with Carola Schönlieb

## Summary

Carola Schönlieb, a professor of applied mathematics, discusses the evolution of image processing from classical partial differential equations (PDEs) to modern deep learning techniques. She highlights the importance of mathematical rigor in image restoration, denoising, and inverse problems, particularly in biomedical imaging and cultural heritage preservation.

## Key Ideas

- Mathematical models like partial differential equations (PDEs) provide a foundational framework for image restoration and denoising.
- Inverse problems, such as CT scans, involve reconstructing images from indirect measurements like line integrals (Radon transform).
- Edge preservation is a critical challenge in image denoising, often addressed by techniques like total variation regularization.
- Deep learning is increasingly outperforming handcrafted models, but it lacks the interpretability and stability guarantees of classical mathematics.
- Hybrid approaches that combine neural networks with structured mathematical models offer a path toward more reliable and interpretable AI.
- Virtual restoration allows for the non-destructive analysis and reconstruction of fragile historical artifacts like illuminated manuscripts.

## Topics

- [[topics/image-denoising|Image Denoising]]: The process of removing noise from images while preserving essential features like edges.
- [[topics/inverse-problems|Inverse Problems]]: Mathematical problems where one must infer the original object from indirect, often incomplete, measurements.
- [[topics/virtual-restoration|Virtual Restoration]]: Using digital imaging and mathematical algorithms to reconstruct damaged historical artifacts without physical intervention.
- [[topics/neural-network-interpretability|Neural Network Interpretability]]: The challenge of understanding the decision-making process of deep learning models, particularly in high-stakes fields like medicine.

## Notable Claims

- Handcrafted mathematical models provide better stability and interpretability than deep neural networks. Evidence: Mathematical proofs can be derived for classical models regarding their behavior under perturbation, whereas neural networks are often 'black boxes'.
- Deep learning models can fail when applied to data outside their training distribution. Evidence: Models trained on specific scanner types (e.g., GE vs. Siemens) may fail when presented with images from a different manufacturer.
