# The Powerful Alternative To Fine-Tuning

## Metadata

## Summary

Ian Fischer, co-founder of Poetic, discusses their approach to AI development using 'recursive self-improvement' to create reasoning harnesses. These harnesses act as 'stilts' for existing foundation models, allowing startups to achieve state-of-the-art performance without the massive costs of fine-tuning or training models from scratch, effectively insulating them from the 'bitter lesson' of rapid model obsolescence.

## Key Ideas

- Recursive self-improvement allows AI to optimize its own reasoning strategies and prompts automatically.
- Building 'harnesses' on top of foundation models is more cost-effective and durable than fine-tuning.
- The 'stilts' metaphor describes how Poetic's system sits on top of any model to consistently outperform the base model's capabilities.
- Automated optimization of reasoning strategies (code-based) is significantly more effective than manual prompt engineering.
- Startups can avoid the 'bitter lesson' by using modular, model-agnostic agentic systems that remain relevant even as frontier models evolve.

## Topics

- [[topics/recursive-self-improvement|Recursive Self-Improvement]]: The process where an AI system iteratively improves its own reasoning and performance without human intervention.
- [[topics/agentic-systems|Agentic Systems]]: AI architectures that use multiple model calls, code, and data to solve complex, multi-step problems.
- [[topics/the-bitter-lesson|The Bitter Lesson]]: The observation in machine learning that methods relying on massive compute and scale eventually outperform human-engineered heuristics.
- [[topics/model-agnosticism|Model Agnosticism]]: The ability of a system to maintain performance advantages regardless of which underlying foundation model is used.

## Notable Claims

- Poetic's system can achieve state-of-the-art results on benchmarks like ARC AGI V2 and Humanity's Last Exam at a fraction of the cost of training frontier models. Evidence: Poetic achieved 54% on ARC AGI V2 at $32/problem and 55% on Humanity's Last Exam with under $100k in optimization costs.
- Fine-tuning is a losing strategy for most startups because frontier model releases quickly render fine-tuned models obsolete. Evidence: Rapid release cycles of models like GPT-4o and Claude Opus make expensive, static fine-tuning investments obsolete.
- Reasoning strategies written in code are more effective than manual prompt engineering. Evidence: Internal experiments showed performance jumps from 5% to 95% when adding reasoning strategies to prompts.
