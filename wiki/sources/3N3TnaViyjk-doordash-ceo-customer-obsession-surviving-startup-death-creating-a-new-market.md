# DoorDash CEO: Customer Obsession, Surviving Startup Death & Creating A New Market

## Summary

Tony Xu, CEO and co-founder of DoorDash, discusses the company's origins, the importance of customer obsession, and the philosophy of building a long-term, mission-driven business. He details the early struggles of DoorDash, including the 'Stanford football game' crisis, the challenges of fundraising, and the strategic decision to focus on suburban markets, ultimately leading to the company becoming a category leader.

## Key Ideas

- The best way to become an expert is to do the work yourself, including the unglamorous tasks.
- Customer obsession, rather than competitor focus, is the primary driver for sustainable growth.
- Building a company should be viewed as a long-term, potentially eternal mission to empower physical businesses and grow local GDP.
- Suburban markets offered better unit economics and higher demand than city centers, contrary to initial industry assumptions.
- Crisis situations provide clarity of purpose, making difficult decisions easier to execute.
- The physical world remains a massive, largely untapped frontier for technology compared to the digital world.

## Notable Claims

- DoorDash's success was driven by focusing on suburban markets rather than dense city centers. Evidence: Suburban areas had higher basket sizes, easier parking, and a higher percentage of families, leading to better unit economics.
- The company almost failed due to a surge in demand during a Stanford football game. Evidence: The system could not handle the volume, leading to late deliveries and a near-depletion of cash reserves due to refunds.
- DoorDash's driver workforce is distinct from ride-sharing platforms. Evidence: A test showed that drivers self-selected based on the type of work, with DoorDash skewing younger and more female.
