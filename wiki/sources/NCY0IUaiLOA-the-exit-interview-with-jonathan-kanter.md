## Summary

Jonathan Kanter, former head of the DOJ Antitrust Division, discusses his tenure, the importance of antitrust enforcement in protecting 'little tech' and innovation, and his vision for future competition policy in sectors like healthcare and AI.

## Key Ideas

- Antitrust enforcement is essential during periods of major technological shifts to prevent incumbents from using business practices to stifle competition.
- The DOJ Antitrust Division operates as law enforcement, not regulation, providing necessary 'lines on the road' for a functioning market.
- Corporate power can translate into political power, which poses a threat to democratic participation.
- There is a need to distinguish between legitimate expertise and industry-captured advocacy in academia and policy circles.
- Antitrust enforcement is increasingly bipartisan, supported by those who view it as a catalyst for entrepreneurial opportunity and growth.

## Topics

- [[topics/antitrust-enforcement|Antitrust Enforcement]]: The use of legal action to prevent monopolies and ensure fair competition in the marketplace.
- [[topics/little-tech-vs-big-tech|Little Tech vs. Big Tech]]: The struggle of startups and smaller companies to compete against established, dominant technology platforms.
- [[topics/ai-and-competition|AI and Competition]]: The role of antitrust in ensuring that the AI revolution remains open to new entrants rather than being captured by incumbents.
- [[topics/regulatory-capture|Regulatory Capture]]: The concern that industry influence over experts and academia distorts the objective search for truth and policy-making.

## Notable Claims

- Antitrust enforcement is most critical during times of major technological transformation. Evidence: Historical examples include the AT&T case leading to IBM, and the Microsoft case leading to the internet and Google.
- The Antitrust Division can operate efficiently without being a burden to taxpayers. Evidence: Kanter claims the division operated with a budget surplus funded by Hart-Scott-Rodino merger filing fees.
- Corporate power can lead to dangerous levels of political power. Evidence: Kanter cites the visibility of big tech CEOs at the presidential inauguration as evidence of their influence.
