## Summary

Phaedra Ellis-Lamkins, founder and CEO of Promise, shares her journey from labor organizing and working with Prince to founding a YC-backed startup focused on criminal justice reform. She emphasizes the importance of mission-driven entrepreneurship, leveraging personal experiences to solve systemic problems, and building sustainable, diverse companies.

## Key Ideas

- Entrepreneurship can be a tool for social change and addressing systemic inequality.
- Founders should focus on solving real-world problems rather than just chasing trends.
- Relationship-based sales and deep industry knowledge are critical, especially when selling to government entities.
- Building a company requires resilience, the ability to 'fake it until you make it,' and the confidence to hire experts for technical gaps.
- Investors value founders who are mission-driven and have a clear understanding of their market, even if they lack a traditional tech background.
- The importance of building a company culture that allows women to be their authentic selves rather than mimicking traditional male leadership styles.

## Notable Claims

- 74% of people in jail are awaiting trial and have not been convicted of a crime. Evidence: Stated by the speaker as a core statistic driving the mission of Promise.
- Relationship-based sales are more effective than cold outreach for government contracts. Evidence: The speaker notes they have not sent a single cold email to secure government clients.
- Investors need founders more than founders need investors. Evidence: The speaker argues that a strong deck and a clear vision make founders highly attractive to capital.
