## Summary

Karan Goel, CEO of Cartisia, discusses the company's focus on developing new machine learning architectures, specifically state-space models (SSMs), to move beyond the limitations of transformer-based architectures. The company aims to build models that better emulate human-like intelligence by prioritizing efficient compression, long-context handling, and multimodality, with an initial commercial application in voice AI for call centers.

## Key Ideas

- Transformers are limited by their nature as 'retrieval machines' rather than 'compression machines'.
- State-space models (SSMs) offer a path toward more efficient, abstract, and human-like intelligence.
- Multimodality is defined as mapping signals (audio, video, robotics) to discrete symbols, not just text-to-text.
- Product-driven research imposes intellectual honesty and discipline that academic research sometimes lacks.
- The goal is to move toward end-to-end learning that eliminates the need for manual tokenization.
- Startup gravity and YC principles are essential for research-heavy companies to remain grounded.

- [[topics/state-space-models-ssms|State-Space Models (SSMs)]]: A class of recurrent models designed for efficient sequence modeling and compression.
- [[topics/multimodality|Multimodality]]: The integration of different data types (audio, video, text) into a unified learning framework.

## Notable Claims

- Transformers are fundamentally limited by their inability to compress representations effectively. Evidence: Transformers act as retrieval machines that recall raw historical data rather than building abstract, compressed world models.
- Solving audio-to-text modeling provides the recipe for solving other multimodal tasks like robotics. Evidence: Common problems across modalities involve signal-to-token representation and hierarchical abstraction.
- Product-focused research is more intellectually honest than purely academic research. Evidence: Customer requirements force researchers to prioritize performance and utility over novelty for the sake of publication.
