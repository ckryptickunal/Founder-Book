## Summary

David Lee, a YC group partner, explains how cohort retention is the most effective quantitative metric for determining if a startup has truly 'made something people want.' He details how to build, interpret, and avoid common pitfalls when analyzing cohort retention curves, emphasizing that the shape of the curve—specifically whether it flattens out—is more important than the absolute number of users.

## Key Ideas

- Cohort retention tracks the percentage of new users from a specific time-based group who continue to perform a value-driven action over time.
- The most critical indicator of product-market fit is a retention curve that flattens out, rather than trending toward zero.
- Avoid 'vanity metrics' by choosing a specific, value-oriented action (e.g., viewing a photo) rather than a shallow action (e.g., opening an app).
- Selecting the correct time granularity (daily, weekly, or quarterly) is essential and should match the intended usage frequency of the product.
- A 'layer cake' chart visualizes how a growing user base is composed of retained users from past cohorts, which is the foundation of a large-scale business.
- If retention curves are poor, founders should focus on improving the product, refining user acquisition, enhancing onboarding, or leveraging network effects.

## Topics

- [[topics/cohort-retention|Cohort Retention]]: The practice of tracking user groups based on their acquisition date to measure long-term engagement.
- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The degree to which a product satisfies a strong market demand, evidenced by stable retention.
- [[topics/data-analytics|Data Analytics]]: The methodology of building and interpreting user behavior logs to inform business decisions.

## Notable Claims

- If your retention curves do not flatten out, you have not yet made something people want. Evidence: Continuous churn indicates that new users are constantly replacing old ones, preventing the accumulation of a stable user base.
- Using 'is paying' as the sole measure of active usage is often misleading. Evidence: Users often continue paying for subscriptions (like Netflix) long after they stop using the product, creating a false sense of engagement.

## Quotes

> The only thing that matters is whether your cohort curves get flat.
> If your curves don't get flat, I would say it's a pretty good sign that you haven't yet made something people want.
