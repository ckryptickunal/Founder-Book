# Doing these things might feel good, but they won’t derisk your startup.

## Metadata

- Video ID: `zuADJKphtxE`
- Channel: Y Combinator
- Published: 2023-04-11T19:14:16Z
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zuADJKphtxE

## Summary

A critique of startup founders who prioritize performative activities like collecting mentors and joining accelerators over the essential tasks of building a product and engaging with customers.

## Key Ideas

- Founders often engage in 'startup theater' to avoid the fear of building and launching.
- Collecting advisors and mentors is often a time-consuming distraction rather than a de-risking strategy.
- The only two actions that truly de-risk a startup are talking to customers and building/launching a product.
- Avoid the 'bottomless pit' of mentorship cycles that delay actual market validation.

## Entities

- [[entities/y-combinator|Y Combinator]] (organization): A startup accelerator that provides seed funding and advice to startups.

## Topics

- [[topics/startup-de-risking|Startup De-risking]]: The process of identifying and mitigating the primary risks that cause a startup to fail, specifically focusing on market need and product viability.
- [[topics/founder-psychology|Founder Psychology]]: The tendency for founders to seek comfort in mentorship and advisory boards to avoid the anxiety of direct market feedback.

## Notable Claims

- Collecting mentors and advisors does not effectively de-risk a startup. Evidence: These activities often become a 'bottomless pit' of time that prevents founders from actually building or selling.
- Talking to customers and launching a product are the only two ways to truly de-risk a startup. Evidence: These actions provide direct market validation, whereas mentorship and advisory boards provide only theoretical feedback.

## Quotes

> The two things that ridiculously de-risk your startup: first one is talking to your customers, the second one is building and launching a product.
> You could be in that bottomless pit for years and be a startup founder that's never built a product and has never gotten a single customer.
