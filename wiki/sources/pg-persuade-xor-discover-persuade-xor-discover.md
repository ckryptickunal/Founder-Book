# Persuade xor Discover

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the fundamental conflict between writing to persuade and writing to discover. He argues that conventional persuasive writing often requires 'padding' or 'spin' to avoid offending readers' existing beliefs, which ultimately obscures the truth and hinders the discovery of new, surprising ideas.

## Key Ideas

- Persuasive writing often requires adding 'sugar' or 'spin' to make uncomfortable truths palatable to readers.
- Writing to persuade and writing to discover are diametrically opposed goals.
- The effort required to sell controversial ideas to an audience creates 'drag' that prevents the writer from pursuing new, surprising insights.
- Conciseness is essential for clarity; you don't fully understand an idea until it is distilled to its fewest words.
- Pandering to an audience's misconceptions can lead a writer to unconsciously avoid challenging ideas.

## Notable Claims

- Writing to persuade is fundamentally different from writing to discover. Evidence: Persuasion requires catering to reader misconceptions, while discovery requires following ideas wherever they lead, regardless of how offensive they may be.
- Conciseness is a tool for intellectual clarity. Evidence: Graham argues that you cannot fully understand an idea until you have stripped away all unnecessary words.
