# How to Write Usefully

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that the primary goal of an essay should be 'usefulness,' defined by a combination of correctness, importance, novelty, and strength. He provides a methodology for achieving this through rigorous self-editing, the 'Morris technique' of only publishing ideas one is certain of, and using oneself as a proxy for the reader to ensure relevance.

## Key Ideas

- Useful writing is defined by four components: correctness, importance, novelty, and strength.
- The 'Morris technique' involves not publishing any idea unless you are certain it is worth hearing, which necessitates heavy editing and discarding of weak drafts.
- Writing is a tool for discovery; one should write to figure things out, not just to communicate existing knowledge.
- Qualification is a vital skill that allows a writer to express degrees of certainty rather than just making binary true/false statements.
- The internet has removed the barriers to entry for essayists, meaning that most essays—and the ideas within them—are still waiting to be written and discovered.

- [[topics/publication-bias|Publication Bias]]: In science, it is considered a flaw, but in essay writing, it is a necessary strategy for maintaining high quality.

## Notable Claims

- Useful writing is bold but true, avoiding both vague academic writing and false demagoguery. Evidence: Graham contrasts the precision of specific claims (e.g., location of Pike's Peak) against the emptiness of vague academic generalizations.
- You can use yourself as a proxy for the reader to ensure importance and novelty. Evidence: If a topic is important to the author and a realization surprises them, it will likely resonate with a significant portion of the audience.
- The internet is causing an explosion in the essay form similar to how cryptography impacted number theory. Evidence: The removal of publication costs and gatekeepers allows for a direct path to becoming an essayist, enabling the discovery of previously unexplored ideas.
