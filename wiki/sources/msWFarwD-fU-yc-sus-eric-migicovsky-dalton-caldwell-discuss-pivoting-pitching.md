# YC SUS: Eric Migicovsky & Dalton Caldwell discuss pivoting & pitching

## Summary

Eric Migicovsky and Dalton Caldwell from Y Combinator discuss the nuances of pivoting, pitching, and the YC admissions process. They emphasize that founders often waste time on non-productive tasks like legal restructuring or patent filings instead of focusing on product-market fit, customer acquisition, and actual revenue.

## Key Ideas

- Pivoting is often easier within the same corporate entity rather than starting a new company.
- The most important metric for a pivot is whether you can get at least one customer to pay for and use the product.
- Avoid 'hypothetical land' where founders spend time debating strategy instead of talking to customers.
- Hard tech companies face additional financing risk and must build a business model that aligns with customer budgets.
- YC prioritizes founders who can build and ship products over those who try to 'hack' the application process.
- A good one-liner is the most critical part of a YC application.
- Investors often use 'TAM' (Total Addressable Market) as a polite way to say they aren't interested in the idea.

## Notable Claims

- Most founders spend too much time on non-productive work like legal restructuring. Evidence: Founders often create new corporate entities when they could simply change the product focus within the existing one.
- The best way to get into YC is to have a good startup, not to hack the application. Evidence: Partners read every application thoroughly; gimmicks like bit.ly links or fake recommendations are counterproductive.
- Social apps are not inherently harder to fund than B2B apps, but they are often more crowded and lack differentiation. Evidence: Successful social app founders usually have a history of shipping products that gained users.
