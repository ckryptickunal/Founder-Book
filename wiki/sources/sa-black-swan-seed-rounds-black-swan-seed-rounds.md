# Black Swan Seed Rounds

## Summary

Sam Altman reflects on his early seed-stage investing experience, noting that highly competitive 'hot' seed rounds are often anti-correlated with long-term success, and that the most successful companies often appear as bad ideas to the broader investor community at the start.

## Key Ideas

- Hotly-competed seed rounds often underperform compared to less popular deals.
- Great companies at the seed stage often look like bad ideas to most investors.
- Fundraising ability is not a proxy for the ability to run a successful company.
- Investors are often more risk-averse than they claim, causing them to avoid high-potential, high-risk early-stage startups.
- Being contrarian is only valuable if you are also correct; being contrarian and wrong is still a failure.

## Notable Claims

- Hot seed rounds are anti-correlated with very successful investments. Evidence: Altman's personal experience with 40 investments where the most successful ones were not competitive.
- Most investors are risk-averse despite their claims. Evidence: Great companies look risky at the seed stage, and most investors avoid them.

## Quotes

> The most striking observation is that, in my experience, the 'hot seed rounds' that everyone is fighting to get in are anti-correlated with very successful investments.
> Being contrarian and wrong is still bad—you have to be contrarian and right.
> Great companies often look like bad ideas at the beginning.
