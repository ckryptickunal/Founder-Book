# Silicon Valley's Cargo Culting Problem

## Summary

In this discussion, Y Combinator's Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell define 'cargo culting' in the startup world as the superficial imitation of successful companies without understanding the underlying reasons for their success. They categorize this behavior into classic, modern, and superficial levels, warning founders against blindly copying strategies—such as office culture, hiring practices, or growth tactics—from companies like Google, Facebook, and Uber without first validating them against their own specific user needs and business context.

## Key Ideas

- Cargo culting is the act of superficially copying successful behaviors without understanding the underlying 'why' or the context that made them successful.
- Founders often fall into the trap of copying superficial elements (like office aesthetics or hiring practices) instead of focusing on core product value.
- Successful strategies are often context-dependent; what worked for Google or Facebook in their early days may not apply to a different business model or market.
- Modern cargo culting involves copying companies that have high valuations but unproven business models or revenue, which is even more dangerous than copying established giants.
- True innovation requires synthesizing influences and digesting information to create original work, rather than blindly plagiarizing strategies.
- The best way to avoid cargo culting is to start with the user's needs rather than starting with a competitor's fundraising announcement or public persona.

## Notable Claims

- Copying Google's office culture (open offices, free snacks) does not lead to the success Google achieved. Evidence: Google's success was driven by solving a hard technical problem in search, not by its office layout.
- Blindly following the 'Facebook Playbook' of not charging users to build a massive database is not a viable strategy for most businesses. Evidence: Facebook's strategy relied on high user engagement (hours per day), which is not applicable to products with low usage frequency.
- Founders often base their strategy on fundraising announcements rather than revenue or product-market fit. Evidence: Founders frequently cite high valuations or series B funding as proof of a company's success without knowing its actual revenue.
