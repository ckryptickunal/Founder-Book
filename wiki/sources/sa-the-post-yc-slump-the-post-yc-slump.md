# The Post-YC Slump

## Summary

Sam Altman explains the 'post-YC slump,' a common phenomenon where startups lose their growth momentum after graduating from Y Combinator. He attributes this decline to founders shifting focus from product development and growth to 'fake work' like PR, conferences, and strategic planning, and emphasizes the necessity of maintaining high intensity and accountability to succeed.

## Key Ideas

- Startups often experience a flatlining of growth shortly after the YC program ends.
- Founders frequently distract themselves with 'fake work'—tasks that feel productive but do not directly contribute to growth.
- Momentum is the most critical factor for startup survival; losing it creates a downward spiral.
- The feeling of having 'made it' after a funding round often leads to a dangerous reduction in intensity.
- Successful startups continue to operate with the same intensity and focus on product/growth as they did during the accelerator program.
- Accountability mechanisms, such as regular investor updates and peer pressure, are essential to prevent stagnation.

## Topics

- [[topics/startup-momentum|Startup Momentum]]: The critical velocity of growth that allows a startup to overcome obstacles and survive.
- [[topics/fake-work|Fake Work]]: Non-essential activities like PR, conferences, and excessive strategic planning that distract founders from building and selling.
- [[topics/founder-psychology|Founder Psychology]]: The tendency for founders to lose urgency, feel entitled to success, or fear admitting that their product isn't working.

## Notable Claims

- Startups that go dark on their investors are almost always doing poorly. Evidence: Observation of YC portfolio company behavior patterns.
- If every founder maintained the intensity of the YC period, the number of successful startups would double. Evidence: Altman's assessment of the impact of focus and momentum on long-term success.
