## Summary

Garry Tan interviews Lee Hoffman, co-founder of Runwise, a startup that modernizes building heating and energy systems using wireless sensor networks and software to reduce carbon emissions and operational costs.

## Key Ideas

- Buildings are responsible for approximately 33% of US carbon emissions.
- Most existing buildings rely on outdated, mechanical, 1960s-era control systems that are inefficient and difficult to service.
- Runwise uses wireless, battery-operated sensors to retrofit buildings 10 times faster and cheaper than traditional wired systems.
- The 'outcomes as a service' model shifts the risk of installation and maintenance from the property owner to the service provider.
- Optimizing building operations could reduce US carbon emissions by over 10%.
- Founder success often comes from identifying 'weird' problems by talking directly to customers in niche industries.

## Topics

- [[topics/climate-tech|Climate Tech]]: The application of technology to reduce greenhouse gas emissions in the built environment.
- [[topics/outcomes-as-a-service|Outcomes as a Service]]: A business model where customers pay for the result (e.g., energy savings) rather than the software or hardware itself.
- [[topics/building-retrofitting|Building Retrofitting]]: The process of upgrading legacy building infrastructure with modern, efficient technology.

## Notable Claims

- Runwise can cut carbon emissions and energy usage in buildings by 20% to 25%. Evidence: Operational data from existing building retrofits.
- If all US buildings were run efficiently, it could reduce national carbon emissions by over 10%. Evidence: Calculated based on the fact that buildings account for one-third of US carbon emissions.
- Outcomes as a service could become a larger driver of GDP than Software as a Service (SaaS). Evidence: Market observation regarding the preference for tangible outcomes over software tools.
