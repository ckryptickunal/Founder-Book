## Summary

Abigail Slater, the Assistant Attorney General for the Antitrust Division at the US DOJ, discusses the shift toward a more robust, bipartisan approach to antitrust enforcement. She outlines the 'America First' or 'MAGA' antitrust philosophy, which prioritizes the interests of American citizens, workers, and consumers, and emphasizes that antitrust enforcement is a necessary backstop to maintain free, competitive markets.

## Key Ideas

- Antitrust enforcement is increasingly bipartisan, with both the left and right concerned about economic concentration.
- The 'America First' antitrust approach aims to give American citizens a seat at the table in economic policy.
- National security arguments are not a valid defense for companies to avoid antitrust scrutiny; competition often drives the innovation needed for national security.
- The consumer welfare standard should be interpreted broadly to include quality, innovation, and output, rather than being narrowly focused on price.
- The DOJ is open to consent decrees but will be selective and rigorous in their application.
- Antitrust enforcement is a historical Republican tradition, dating back to John Sherman and the Sherman Act.

## Topics

- [[topics/antitrust-enforcement|Antitrust Enforcement]]: The application of laws to prevent monopolies and maintain competitive markets.
- [[topics/america-first-antitrust|America First Antitrust]]: A policy framework prioritizing the economic well-being of American citizens and workers.
- [[topics/national-champions|National Champions]]: The argument that large tech companies are essential to national security and should be exempt from antitrust laws.
- [[topics/consumer-welfare-standard|Consumer Welfare Standard]]: The legal standard for evaluating antitrust cases, which Slater argues should be broader than just price.

## Notable Claims

- Breaking up monopolies can lead to increased innovation. Evidence: The 1980s breakup of AT&T led to the development of the wireless industry and the internet modem.
- Large platforms have the power to suppress politically unpopular news. Evidence: Slater notes that if one platform controls 50% of news distribution, it can effectively make stories disappear.
