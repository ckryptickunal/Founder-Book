# François Chollet: How We Get To AGI

## Summary

François Chollet argues that the current AI paradigm of scaling pre-trained models has reached a plateau because it focuses on static skill memorization rather than fluid intelligence. He proposes that true AGI requires 'test-time adaptation' and a hybrid approach combining 'Type 1' (perception/intuition) and 'Type 2' (discrete program search/reasoning) abstractions to enable autonomous invention.

## Key Ideas

- Intelligence is the efficiency of operationalizing past experience to handle novel, uncertain future situations.
- Scaling laws for pre-training lead to automation, not general intelligence.
- The 'Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus' (ARC) serves as a benchmark for fluid intelligence, resisting brute-force scaling.
- Type 1 abstraction (continuous/perception) and Type 2 abstraction (discrete/programmatic) must be integrated to achieve AGI.
- Future AI systems should function like 'programmers' that synthesize models on-the-fly using a library of reusable abstractions.
- Discrete program search guided by deep learning intuition is the path to overcoming combinatorial explosion.

## Topics

- [[topics/fluid-intelligence|Fluid Intelligence]]: The ability to adapt and solve novel problems on-the-fly, distinct from static skill memorization.
- [[topics/test-time-adaptation|Test-Time Adaptation]]: Techniques allowing models to modify their behavior dynamically during inference.
- [[topics/program-synthesis|Program Synthesis]]: The process of generating code or symbolic structures to solve tasks, rather than relying on pattern matching.
- [[topics/compositional-generalization|Compositional Generalization]]: The ability to recombine known abstractions to solve complex, unseen problems.

## Notable Claims

- Scaling up pre-training does not lead to AGI. Evidence: Performance on the ARC benchmark remained near zero despite a 50,000x increase in model scale.
- Intelligence is not skill. Evidence: Skill is the output of a process; intelligence is the process of building the ability to handle new situations.
- Deep learning models lack compositional generalization. Evidence: They struggle with simple discrete tasks like sorting or digit manipulation when provided as sequences.
