# How People Get Rich Now

## Summary

Paul Graham analyzes the shift in the sources of American wealth from 1982 to 2020, noting a decline in inherited wealth and a rise in fortunes built through technology-driven startups. He argues that the mid-20th century, characterized by corporate oligopolies, was an economic anomaly, and that the current trend of entrepreneurship is a return to a historical norm, accelerated by lower startup costs and faster company growth.

## Key Ideas

- Inherited wealth has significantly declined as a source of top fortunes since 1982.
- The mid-20th century was an anomaly defined by corporate consolidation and oligopolies, which suppressed entrepreneurship.
- Modern wealth is primarily generated by 'tech' companies that win through superior technology rather than deal-making.
- Starting a company is cheaper and faster today, leading to higher valuations and younger founders.
- The rise in the Gini coefficient is a natural result of more people successfully building highly valuable, fast-growing companies.

## Notable Claims

- The mid-20th century was an economic anomaly where corporate consolidation made starting a company non-viable. Evidence: Historical data shows that in 1892, inheritance accounted for only 20% of millionaires, similar to today, while the mid-20th century was dominated by government-backed cartels and oligopolies.
- Technology has made it cheaper and faster to build and scale companies. Evidence: The time taken for companies to reach a billion dollars in revenue has dropped from 45 years (IBM) to 7-8 years for modern startups.
