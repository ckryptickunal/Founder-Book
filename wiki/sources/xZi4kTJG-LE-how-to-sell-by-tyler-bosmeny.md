## Summary

Tyler Bosmeny, CEO of Clever, outlines the fundamental principles of sales for startup founders, emphasizing that sales is a core responsibility that requires direct founder involvement, active listening, and a structured, persistent approach to the sales funnel.

## Key Ideas

- Founders should not wait to hire salespeople; they must own the sales process initially to understand the customer and product-market fit.
- Sales is not about being a 'charming' salesperson, but about being a world-class listener who solves problems.
- The sales funnel consists of prospecting, having conversations, and closing; each stage requires persistence and iteration.
- Conferences are highly underrated for B2B sales; they provide direct access to users and decision-makers.
- Avoid the 'free trial' trap; seek commitment and validation through annual agreements with opt-out clauses.
- Do not build custom features for a single customer unless it is part of a strategic commitment to buy.
- Sales motion must align with the price point of the product (e.g., high-touch for expensive enterprise deals, low-touch/marketing-led for cheap products).

## Topics

- [[topics/sales-funnel|Sales Funnel]]: The process of moving from prospecting to closing, requiring persistence and data-driven iteration.
- [[topics/founder-led-sales|Founder-Led Sales]]: The necessity for founders to handle early sales to gain deep customer insights and validate the product.
- [[topics/enterprise-sales|Enterprise Sales]]: High-touch, long-cycle sales processes involving multiple stakeholders and contract negotiations.
- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The state achieved when customers are willing to pay for the solution, validated by 'yeses' rather than 'nos'.

## Notable Claims

- Founders should spend 70% of their time listening during sales calls. Evidence: Observation of top-performing salespeople and analysis of call metrics via tools like UberConference.
- Conferences are the most effective way to meet potential buyers. Evidence: Personal experience at Clever where many early partnerships were formed by booking meetings in advance at industry events.
- You cannot learn product-market fit from 'nos'. Evidence: Customers often provide polite, non-truthful reasons for rejection; only 'yeses' provide true validation.

## Quotes

> Hire the salespeople? That's you.
> You should be spending every moment of every day doing one of two things: building your product or talking to users.
> I don't think you discover a product market fit from the nos. I think you only discover it from the yeses.
