# Stop Innovating (On The Wrong Things)

## Summary

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel from Y Combinator discuss the common pitfall of 'over-innovating' on non-essential aspects of a startup. They argue that founders have a limited amount of 'innovation juice' and should focus it exclusively on the primary challenge of achieving product-market fit, while adopting standard best practices for everything else.

## Key Ideas

- Founders have a finite amount of energy; wasting it on non-core innovation increases the risk of failure.
- Achieving product-market fit is a miracle; do not complicate it by adding unnecessary, high-risk side bets.
- Standardization (e.g., Delaware C-Corps, standard pricing models) reduces friction for customers and investors.
- Innovation should be reserved for the core product, not for corporate governance, tech stacks, or contrarian business models.
- Many founders confuse 'being different' (branding/fashion) with 'being better' (product utility).

## Topics

- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The primary goal of a startup; the hosts argue this is the only area where 'innovation juice' should be spent.
- [[topics/corporate-governance|Corporate Governance]]: The legal structure of a company; the hosts advise using standard Delaware C-Corp structures rather than experimenting with exotic setups.
- [[topics/technical-debt-and-choices|Technical Debt and Choices]]: The tendency for engineers to choose fun or idiosyncratic tech stacks over proven, reliable ones.
- [[topics/customer-centricity|Customer-Centricity]]: The principle that business decisions should be made to reduce friction for the customer, not to satisfy the founder's ego or desire for uniqueness.

## Notable Claims

- Founders should avoid non-standard corporate structures like Wyoming LLCs or complex non-profit/for-profit hybrids. Evidence: These structures create unnecessary legal and investment friction without providing a competitive advantage.
- Trying to prove standard startup advice wrong is a distraction that hurts the customer. Evidence: Founders often prioritize being contrarian over solving the customer's actual problem.
- Pricing models should follow industry standards to avoid confusing potential customers. Evidence: If a customer cannot understand how to buy the product, they will not buy it, regardless of the product's quality.
