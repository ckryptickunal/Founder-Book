# The Trouble with the Segway

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-the-trouble-with-the-segway`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: July 2009
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-the-trouble-with-the-segway

## Summary

Paul Graham analyzes the commercial failure of the Segway, attributing it to the 'smug' appearance of the rider and the company's insulation from real-world feedback due to excessive early funding.

## Key Ideas

- The Segway's failure is largely social; riders appear 'smug' because they seem to be exerting no effort.
- Perception of effort matters: motorcycles are accepted because the rider's posture implies engagement, whereas Segway riders look like they are in a sedan chair.
- Design aesthetics that resemble medical devices rather than recreational equipment contribute to the 'dork' factor.
- Excessive early capital can be a disadvantage, preventing companies from iterating based on real-world user feedback.

## Topics

- [[topics/product-design|Product Design]]: The importance of aesthetics and social perception in the adoption of new technology.
- [[topics/startup-funding|Startup Funding]]: The risks of having too much capital, which can lead to a lack of market-driven iteration.

## Notable Claims

- The Segway failed because riders look smug and lazy. Evidence: Anecdotal comparison between the Segwell and the Eunicycle, where the former receives abuse and the latter receives smiles.
- Excessive early funding caused Segway to fail by insulating them from market feedback. Evidence: The company operated in secret with focus groups instead of iterating through real-world usage.
