# Competition is for Losers with Peter Thiel (How to Start a Startup 2014: 5)

## Summary

Peter Thiel argues that startups should aim for monopoly rather than competition, asserting that competition destroys profits and that true value is created by building unique, proprietary businesses. He emphasizes that companies should start by dominating a small, niche market before expanding, and warns against the 'mimetic' human tendency to compete in crowded, low-margin fields.

## Key Ideas

- Competition is for losers; monopolies are the only way to build long-term, valuable businesses.
- A valuable business creates X dollars of value and captures Y percent of it; X and Y are independent variables.
- Startups should focus on dominating a very small market first before expanding concentrically.
- Most value in successful tech companies exists far in the future, making durability more important than current growth rates.
- The 'last mover' advantage is superior to the 'first mover' advantage.
- Human nature is inherently mimetic, leading people to compete for the same things rather than seeking unique paths.
- Vertical integration is an underexplored and powerful modality for building complex, defensible monopolies.

## Notable Claims

- The cumulative profits of the US airline industry have been approximately zero. Evidence: Comparison of airline industry revenue versus profit margins over a 100-year history.
- Most value in tech companies comes from cash flows in the distant future. Evidence: Discounted cash flow analysis showing that 75-85% of value is realized 10+ years out.
- Lean Startup methodology is often overrated. Evidence: Thiel argues that great companies often require a 'quantum' leap rather than iterative, survey-based development.
