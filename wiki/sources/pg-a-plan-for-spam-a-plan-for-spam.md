# A Plan for Spam

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-a-plan-for-spam`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: August 2002
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-a-plan-for-spam

## Summary

Paul Graham proposes a statistical approach to spam filtering using Bayesian analysis of word probabilities. By training filters on individual user mail corpora, the system achieves high accuracy with zero false positives, forcing spammers to change their tactics and ultimately making spam economically unviable.

## Key Ideas

- Content-based Bayesian filtering is more effective than rule-based feature recognition.
- Spam is defined as unsolicited automated email, regardless of commercial intent.
- Individualized training corpora allow filters to adapt to a user's specific vocabulary and communication patterns.
- False positives are the primary danger of spam filtering and must be avoided at all costs.
- Statistical filtering forces spammers to dilute their sales pitches, reducing the economic efficiency of spamming.
- Bayesian filters evolve alongside spam, making them more resilient than static rule-based systems.

## Topics

- [[topics/bayesian-filtering|Bayesian Filtering]]: A statistical method that calculates the probability of an email being spam based on the frequency of individual words within a user's specific corpus.
- [[topics/false-positives|False Positives]]: The accidental classification of legitimate email as spam, which the author considers the most critical failure mode for any filter.
- [[topics/spam-economics|Spam Economics]]: The theory that spam persists because it is cheap to send; increasing the cost of delivery through effective filtering will eventually drive spammers out of business.

## Notable Claims

- Bayesian filters can achieve less than 5 misses per 1000 spams with zero false positives. Evidence: Results observed by the author using a personalized corpus of 4000 spam and 4000 non-spam messages.
- Spammers will eventually stop sending spam if the response rate is driven low enough. Evidence: Spam is a business; if the cost of reaching an audience exceeds the revenue generated by the low response rate, the business model fails.

## Quotes

> The Achilles heel of the spammers is their message.
> For most users, missing legitimate email is an order of magnitude worse than receiving spam, so a filter that yields false positives is like an acne cure that carries a risk of death to the patient.
> The real advantage of the Bayesian approach, of course, is that you know what you're measuring.
> The defining feature of spam (in fact, its raison d'etre) is not that it is unsolicited, but that it is automated.
