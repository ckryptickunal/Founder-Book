# How to sell your startup for millions

## Summary

Garry Tan and Andrew Lee discuss the strategic realities of selling a startup, categorizing acquisitions into three types: strategic threats, revenue-generating, and talent/tech acquisitions. They emphasize that companies are bought, not sold, and provide a framework for founders to evaluate their situation, manage the M&A process, and avoid common pitfalls.

## Key Ideas

- Companies are bought, not sold; founders should focus on building value rather than actively shopping the company.
- Three primary types of acquisitions: Strategic Threat (highest value), Revenue Generating, and Team/Talent (lowest value).
- Founders should use a four-step questioning process (based on 'Being Mortal') to assess their situation: understanding the landscape, identifying hopes and fears, defining trade-offs, and determining a course of action.
- Always maintain a BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement), such as fundraising or increasing revenue, to maintain leverage.
- Avoid treating M&A like a fundraising process; corporate development teams have different incentives than venture capitalists.
- The best time to sell is often when the company is growing and doesn't 'need' to sell, rather than when it is failing.

## Notable Claims

- The best time to sell a company is when it is growing and doesn't need to sell. Evidence: Founders are usually approached by acquirers when the company is doing well, which provides more leverage.
- Treating M&A like a fundraising process is a mistake. Evidence: Corporate development teams are incentivized to minimize costs, whereas VCs are incentivized by competitive bidding and growth.
- Strategic threat acquisitions command the highest purchase prices. Evidence: Incumbents pay a premium to neutralize threats that could fundamentally change their industry, such as Google buying YouTube.
