# The Secrets To Setting Smarter Goals

## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell of Y Combinator discuss the common pitfalls startup founders encounter when setting and pursuing goals. They emphasize that goal-setting is an internal tool for personal and company growth, not a performance for investors or a status game. The conversation highlights the dangers of 'playing stupid games'—such as vanity metrics, sandbagging, and premature scaling—and the 'stupid prizes' that follow, including loss of control, toxic company culture, and legal trouble.

## Key Ideas

- Goals should be motivational tools to help you act smarter and faster, not just metrics to please outsiders.
- Avoid 'fake metrics' like downloads or website hits; focus on revenue or active usage that proves product-market fit.
- Sandbagging (setting easy goals to look good) is a career-survival tactic in big companies but is self-destructive for founders.
- The 'stupid prize' of raising too much money is often losing control of your company and being forced to scale negative margins.
- Founders should avoid 'cargo culting' behaviors like hiring executives or angel investing before their own startup has achieved product-market fit.
- If a goal isn't met, don't make excuses; accept the reality, pivot, and focus energy on solving the hardest problems.

## Notable Claims

- Raising too much money can make a pivot impossible. Evidence: Excessive capital creates momentum and stakeholder expectations that make it difficult to change direction when the current path is failing.
- Sandbagging is a bad strategy for founders. Evidence: While it helps in corporate environments to get promoted, founders need to be honest about their progress to solve the hardest problems effectively.
