# Ramen Profitable

## Summary

Paul Graham defines 'ramen profitable' as a startup generating just enough revenue to cover the founders' basic living expenses. Unlike traditional profitability, which signals a successful, mature business, ramen profitability is a strategic milestone that grants founders independence from investors, improves morale, and allows for better fundraising terms by reducing the urgency to secure capital.

## Key Ideas

- Ramen profitability provides a buffer that prevents startups from being at the mercy of investors.
- It serves as a validation of the business model, proving that customers are willing to pay and that founders are disciplined.
- The primary benefit is the removal of the 'distraction' of fundraising, allowing founders to focus entirely on product development.
- It is a survival tactic, not a final destination; the ultimate goal of a startup remains rapid growth.
- There is a risk of 'sliding' into a consulting business model, which lacks the scalability required for a true startup.

## Notable Claims

- Ramen profitability makes startups more attractive to investors. Evidence: It demonstrates that the founders can acquire paying customers, are focused on user needs, and possess financial discipline.
- Fundraising is a significant productivity killer for startups. Evidence: Graham notes that the process occupies the founders' thoughts constantly, distracting them from product development.
- Ramen profitability is not the same as bootstrapping. Evidence: Most startups still require external investment to scale; ramen profitability is merely a tool to gain leverage during that process.
