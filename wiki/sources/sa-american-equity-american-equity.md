# American Equity

## Summary

Sam Altman proposes 'American Equity,' a policy where every US citizen receives an annual share of the national GDP to foster inclusive prosperity, mitigate the wealth-concentrating effects of automation, and replace inefficient social welfare programs.

## Key Ideas

- Aligning national success with individual prosperity through GDP-linked equity.
- Using a 'floor and no ceiling' social contract to drive economic growth.
- Transitioning from land-based wealth distribution (Homestead Acts) to capital-based distribution.
- Counteracting the wealth concentration inherent in automation.
- Funding the program by reforming the tax system and consolidating existing aid programs.

## Entities

- [[entities/homestead-acts|Homestead Acts]] (other): Historical US laws that distributed land to citizens.

## Topics

- [[topics/universal-basic-income-ubi-equity|Universal Basic Income (UBI) / Equity]]: The concept of providing a guaranteed financial floor for all citizens to eliminate poverty and increase productivity.
- [[topics/automation|Automation]]: The economic shift caused by technology that threatens to concentrate wealth and requires new social contracts.
- [[topics/tax-reform|Tax Reform]]: The necessity of updating tax structures, such as taxing capital and labor equally, to fund new social programs.

## Notable Claims

- Concentrating wealth in a small number of families leads to long-term national decline. Evidence: Historical observation of countries that fail to implement inclusive systems.
- Eliminating poverty will increase overall economic productivity. Evidence: Altman argues that removing the fear of basic survival allows people to contribute more effectively to the economy.
- The joint-stock company model can be adapted to national governance to align citizen interests. Evidence: The success of the joint-stock model in aligning individuals toward common goals.

## Quotes

> I think that every adult US citizen should get an annual share of the US GDP.
> The default case for automation is to concentrate wealth (and therefore power) in a tiny number of hands.
> We would no longer motivate people through the fear of not being able to eat.
