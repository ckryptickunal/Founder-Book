## Summary

Former FTC Chair Lina Khan discusses the importance of antitrust enforcement, the dangers of unchecked corporate power, and the necessity of maintaining competitive markets for innovation. She addresses the political landscape surrounding her tenure, the impact of recent FTC leadership changes, and the role of government in regulating gatekeepers in the AI era.

## Key Ideas

- Monopolies and gatekeepers stifle innovation by creating bureaucratic red tape and preventing new entrants from competing.
- Antitrust enforcement is a bipartisan issue because concentrated economic power threatens both consumer welfare and democratic liberties.
- The 'for-cause' removal of FTC commissioners is illegal and threatens the agency's autonomy and ongoing litigation against major corporations.
- Open-source AI is critical for preventing dominant tech companies from controlling access to transformative technologies.
- Antitrust remedies must be forward-looking to prevent companies from profiting from past lawbreaking while replicating anti-competitive behaviors in new markets.

- [[topics/ai-governance|AI Governance]]: The debate over whether AI development should be open-source or closed-source and how to prevent gatekeepers from controlling the technology.
- [[topics/corporate-gatekeeping|Corporate Gatekeeping]]: The practice of dominant firms using their market position to create barriers to entry for startups and innovators.

## Notable Claims

- The removal of FTC commissioners Slaughter and Bedoya was illegal. Evidence: Khan cites existing law stating FTC commissioners can only be removed 'for cause', which was not alleged.
- Concentrated economic power threatens democratic liberties. Evidence: Khan argues that unchecked power in either government or private monopolies poses a risk to core freedoms.
- AI models are appropriating value from creators without consent. Evidence: Khan points to ongoing copyright litigation and the lack of compensation for authors and designers whose work is ingested by AI.

## Quotes

> It probably isn't a great idea to have our economy be centrally planned by monopolists.
> I think we should be wary about extrapolating [local problems] to the whole country.
> If we allow an ecosystem to appropriate value from creators without actually adequately compensating them or really getting their consent, I worry that that is going to dampen innovation in the long term.
