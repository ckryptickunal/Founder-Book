# Hackers and Painters

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that computer programming, or 'hacking,' is fundamentally a creative act akin to painting, architecture, and writing, rather than a branch of science or engineering. He contends that the academic and corporate classification of software development as 'computer science' or 'software engineering' misrepresents the nature of the work, which is best performed by makers who prioritize design, empathy, and iterative sketching over rigid adherence to specifications or formal research methods.

## Key Ideas

- Hackers are makers, not scientists; their work is a medium of expression similar to paint or concrete.
- The term 'computer science' is a misleading historical accident that forces hackers to prioritize research papers over building beautiful software.
- Programming should be treated as 'sketching'—an iterative process of refinement rather than a linear translation of a pre-existing specification.
- Great software requires empathy for the user and the reader of the code, as software is ultimately a human-facing medium.
- Startups have a competitive advantage in design because they allow the same people to both design and implement the product, avoiding the 'design by committee' trap of large corporations.
- The best way to learn to hack is by doing it and by studying the source code of great programs, similar to how painters study masters.

## Notable Claims

- Programming languages should be malleable, like a pencil, rather than rigid, like a pen. Evidence: The need for iterative sketching and the ability to change specifications on the fly during the development process.
- Big companies win by 'sucking less' rather than by making great products. Evidence: The tendency of large organizations to damp design oscillations to avoid disasters, which also eliminates the potential for high-quality breakthroughs.
- Hackers need to understand the theory of computation about as much as painters need to understand paint chemistry. Evidence: The assertion that practical making is more important than theoretical knowledge for the actual act of creation.
