## Summary

Michael Seibel, CEO of Y Combinator, shares his candid perspective on angel investing, emphasizing that he views it primarily as a form of 'giving back' rather than a primary wealth-generation strategy. He provides practical advice on check sizes, the importance of speed in decision-making, and how to evaluate startups effectively.

## Key Ideas

- Angel investing should be treated as a long-term commitment with a high risk of failure, often akin to charity.
- Investors must write checks large enough to be meaningful in the event of a billion-dollar exit.
- Speed is a critical trait of an 'A-grade' investor; founders value those who can decide and wire funds quickly.
- The 'FOMO/Friend' rule: if a friend is starting a company and you would regret missing out on a billion-dollar outcome, invest.
- Do not rely solely on Demo Day; research companies early and maintain relationships with founders post-launch.
- Avoid over-intellectualizing investments; recognize that luck plays a significant role in success.

## Notable Claims

- The most hyped investment on Demo Day is often not the best one. Evidence: Seibel notes that crowd sentiment is frequently inaccurate and that he has seen less-hyped companies outperform highly-touted ones.
- An 'A-grade' investor gives money quickly, signs paperwork quickly, and shuts up. Evidence: Based on YC's experience with founders who prioritize efficiency and low-friction capital.
- Angel investing has a high failure rate and requires a long-term view. Evidence: Seibel's personal scorecard shows that out of 50 investments, only a few achieved significant exits.
