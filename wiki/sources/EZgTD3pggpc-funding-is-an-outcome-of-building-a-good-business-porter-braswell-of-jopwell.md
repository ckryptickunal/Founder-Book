## Summary

Porter Braswell, co-founder and CEO of Jopwell, discusses the company's mission to diversify corporate applicant funnels by connecting Black, Latino, and Native American professionals with career opportunities. He shares his journey from a finance career at Goldman Sachs to founding a tech-enabled recruiting platform, emphasizing the importance of building a sustainable business over chasing venture capital, and the necessity of intentional leadership as a company scales.

## Key Ideas

- Jopwell serves as a 'referral at scale' for underrepresented talent, helping companies move beyond the 'pipeline problem' excuse.
- Funding should be viewed as an outcome of building a good business, not the primary goal of a startup.
- Founders should focus on solving a specific, well-understood pain point rather than trying to build a generic platform.
- As a company scales, the founder's role shifts from being a peer/friend to being a CEO who must provide clear vision, structure, and critical feedback.
- Diversity is a business imperative; companies that fail to reflect the changing demographics of the workforce risk losing relevance and innovation.
- When pitching as an underrepresented founder, flipping the dynamic by positioning oneself as the expert on the specific problem creates leverage.

## Notable Claims

- The 'pipeline problem' is often an excuse used by companies to avoid addressing systemic recruitment failures. Evidence: Braswell notes that there are 44 million Black, Latino, and Native American professionals in the workforce, proving the talent exists.
- A diverse workforce leads to better bottom-line results. Evidence: Cites studies from McKinsey and BCG regarding the correlation between diversity and innovation/profitability.
