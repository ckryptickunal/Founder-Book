# General and Surprising

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the difficulty of achieving insights that are simultaneously general and surprising, arguing that even small increments of novelty in highly general topics provide significant value.

## Key Ideas

- The most valuable insights are both general and surprising, but this territory is rare and difficult to reach.
- Most insights are either general but unoriginal (platitudes) or surprising but narrow (gossip).
- Adding a small amount of novelty to a general idea creates a disproportionately valuable insight.
- Repeating oneself is inevitable when exploring general ideas, but re-articulation often yields new, valuable perspectives.
- Persistence in exploring general ideas is necessary because small insights can lead to larger, more novel discoveries.

## Notable Claims

- The most valuable insights are both general and surprising. Evidence: Cites F=ma as an example of a general and surprising physical law.
- Repeating oneself is not necessarily a failure when writing about general ideas. Evidence: Variation in re-articulation increases the chance of discovering a new, critical delta of novelty.
