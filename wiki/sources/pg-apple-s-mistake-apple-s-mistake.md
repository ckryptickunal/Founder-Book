# Apple's Mistake

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-apple-s-mistake`

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that Apple's restrictive App Store approval process is damaging its relationship with developers, potentially undermining its long-term success by alienating the very people who create the software that makes its platforms valuable.

## Key Ideas

- Apple's App Store approval process is broken and harms its reputation with programmers.
- Apple incorrectly treats software like music or books, failing to understand that software development requires rapid iteration.
- The 'launch-fast-and-iterate' model is essential for modern software, and Apple's delays force developers to release inferior, buggy products.
- Alienating developers is a strategic error because they are the ones who build the applications that define a platform's success.
- A company that wins by exercising power rather than doing better work eventually loses its competitive edge.
- Programmers are currently stuck in an 'abusive relationship' with the iPhone, but they are looking for alternatives.

## Notable Claims

- Apple's App Store approval process is an 'ongoing karma leak' that is destroying their reputation with programmers. Evidence: Anecdotal reports from developers who feel their apps are 'old and crappy' due to long review delays.
- Apple treats software like hardware, failing to understand that software design benefits from evolution. Evidence: Apple's model of 'work until finished, then release' is incompatible with the modern software development cycle.
- Alienating developers will hurt Apple's ability to hire top talent. Evidence: Historical precedent of Microsoft's struggle to hire top programmers when they were perceived as 'the Empire'.
