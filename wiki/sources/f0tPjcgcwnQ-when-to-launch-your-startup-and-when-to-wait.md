# When to Launch Your Startup and When to Wait

## Summary

YC partners Michael Seibel, Harj Taggar, and Brad Flora discuss the common tendency for startup founders to delay launching their products. They debunk the myth of the 'perfect launch,' emphasize that launching is an iterative process rather than a singular event, and explain why most founders should prioritize speed over perfection, while acknowledging rare exceptions for experienced founders.

## Key Ideas

- Founders often delay launching because they fear a negative reaction or believe their product must be perfect, like an 'Oscar ceremony'.
- 'Startup fast' is significantly faster than 'big company fast' and often requires feeling uncomfortable.
- A launch is not a singular, precious event; it is an iterative process that happens repeatedly.
- Waitlists are often used as a psychological crutch to avoid the reality of putting a product in front of users.
- Exceptions to the 'launch early' rule, such as Parker Conrad (Rippling), are rare and usually involve founders who have already built similar products and possess deep domain expertise.
- Founders should look at the early versions of successful companies (like GitHub or Instacart) to realize how simple and 'dirty' their initial products were.

## Notable Claims

- Founders who have already built a successful company in the same domain can afford to take longer to launch. Evidence: Parker Conrad's experience with Zenefits allowed him to build a more robust MVP for Rippling.
- A waitlist is not a substitute for a launch. Evidence: A waitlist is just a form and does not provide real product feedback from active users.
