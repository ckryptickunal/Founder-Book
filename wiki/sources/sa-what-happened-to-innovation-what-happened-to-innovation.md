# What happened to innovation?

## Metadata

- Video ID: `sa-what-happened-to-innovation`

## Summary

Sam Altman explores the perceived stagnation of innovation, arguing that while software and internet-based innovation have accelerated, progress in the physical world—such as energy, transportation, and materials science—has slowed. He attributes this shift to a preference for short-term, low-capital, and incremental projects, driven by investor risk aversion, regulatory hurdles, and the high economic rewards of the software industry.

## Key Ideas

- Innovation has shifted from long-term, capital-intensive physical breakthroughs to short-term, incremental software improvements.
- The internet and software industries offer faster iteration cycles and lower regulatory barriers, making them more attractive to investors.
- Current investment models and tax policies prioritize short-term growth graphs over long-term, high-risk technological advancements.
- The lack of breakthrough progress in physical sectors like energy and transportation is a systemic issue that may require new funding models or government intervention.
- The current economic landscape heavily incentivizes computer science over other STEM fields, creating a talent drain from physical engineering.

## Notable Claims

- Innovation has not slowed overall, but has been redirected toward short-term software opportunities. Evidence: The massive growth in mobile and internet users compared to the lack of progress in energy generation and transportation speed.
- The current economic system makes learning computer science significantly more profitable than other STEM fields. Evidence: The observation that learning to code in ten weeks offers a better career payoff than a decade of studying physics.
