# How to Apply and Succeed at Y Combinator by Dalton Caldwell

## Summary

Dalton Caldwell, Head of Admissions at Y Combinator, outlines the application and interview process for the startup accelerator. He emphasizes that the application process itself is a valuable exercise for founders to clarify their business model, equity splits, and competitive landscape, regardless of the final outcome. He debunks common myths about eligibility and provides actionable advice on creating clear, concise applications and conducting oneself during interviews.

## Key Ideas

- The act of applying to YC forces founders to answer critical questions about their business that they should be addressing anyway.
- There is no such thing as 'too early' or 'too far along' to apply; YC funds a wide variety of stages and industries.
- Clarity of thought is the most important trait in an application; avoid jargon, buzzwords, and obfuscation.
- YC interviews are not adversarial; they are 10-minute conversations to understand the business and assess if the founders are people YC wants to work with long-term.
- Founders should focus on building a great company rather than trying to 'hack' the application process or network their way in.
- Rejection is not permanent; applying again and showing progress is a strong positive signal to YC admissions.
- Founders should be wary of 'predatory advisors' who claim to have secret connections to YC in exchange for equity or cash.

## Notable Claims

- The act of applying to YC is a good use of time even if you are not accepted. Evidence: It forces founders to answer fundamental questions about their business model, equity, and competition.
- It is impossible to network your way into YC. Evidence: The system is designed to fund strangers; cold emails and attempts to 'game' the system are discouraged and ineffective.
- There is no 'too early' to apply to YC. Evidence: YC has funded companies that pivoted mid-batch or had zero revenue at the time of application.
- Over-preparing for interviews by reading 'adversarial' blog posts is counterproductive. Evidence: It leads to memorized, unnatural responses that hinder the ability to have a genuine, productive conversation.
