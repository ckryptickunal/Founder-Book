# The Risk of Discovery

## Summary

Paul Graham explores how historical bias and hindsight distort our perception of scientific discovery, arguing that figures like Isaac Newton took significant risks on pursuits that now appear irrational only because we know the eventual outcomes.

## Key Ideas

- Biographies often sanitize the mistakes and unconventional pursuits of famous scientists.
- Hindsight bias makes past scientific breakthroughs seem inevitable rather than risky.
- What we consider 'crazy' pursuits in the past were often viewed as equally promising as 'serious' science at the time.
- Innovation requires making bets on uncertain outcomes where the potential payoff is high.

## Notable Claims

- Newton's work in alchemy and theology was viewed as equally promising as his physics work during his lifetime. Evidence: The author notes that in Newton's day, the payoff for physics was unknown, placing all three fields in the same category of high-potential inquiry.

## Quotes
