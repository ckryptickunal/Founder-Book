# Vidit Aatrey on Building Meesho, India's Top Reselling Platform, with Adora Cheung

## Summary

Vidit Aatrey, CEO and co-founder of Meesho, discusses the company's evolution from a hyperlocal fashion delivery service to India's leading social commerce platform. The platform empowers individuals, particularly women in Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities, to start and grow online businesses via social media channels like WhatsApp, Facebook, and Instagram, effectively bridging the trust deficit in Indian e-commerce.

## Key Ideas

- Meesho enables entrepreneurship for people without capital by allowing them to act as resellers for unbranded products.
- The platform leverages existing social networks (WhatsApp, Facebook) to build trust, which is crucial in the Indian market.
- Macro trends like the rise of Reliance Jio (affordable data) and UPI (digital payments) were instrumental in the company's rapid growth.
- The company maintains a 'user-first' culture by having management participate in customer support and maintaining direct communication with top resellers.
- Pivoting is a necessary part of the startup journey; Meesho successfully pivoted twice based on user behavior and market feedback.
- Founders should focus on solving problems for their specific target audience rather than just building for themselves or copying Western models.

## Topics

- [[topics/social-commerce|Social Commerce]]: The practice of selling products through social media platforms, leveraging personal trust networks.
- [[topics/trust-deficit|Trust Deficit]]: The cultural challenge in India where consumers prefer buying from known community members over faceless corporate entities.
- [[topics/startup-pivoting|Startup Pivoting]]: The process of changing business direction based on market data and user behavior.
- [[topics/tier-2-3-market-growth|Tier 2/3 Market Growth]]: The rapid expansion of internet usage and digital commerce in smaller Indian cities and towns.

## Notable Claims

- 90% of total commerce in India happens in small mom-and-pop stores. Evidence: Cited as a general market observation by the founder to explain the need for social commerce.
- The best way to learn as a founder is by talking to other founders. Evidence: Aatrey's personal experience with peer networks like those at YC.
- Building a product for someone else is harder than building for yourself, but necessary for the Indian market. Evidence: Aatrey's experience building for housewives in Tier 2/3 cities.
