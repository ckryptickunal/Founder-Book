# Black Swan Farming

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-black-swan-farming`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: September 2012

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the counterintuitive nature of startup investing, arguing that financial success is driven by extreme outliers (Black Swans) that often appear as bad ideas initially, requiring investors to ignore conventional metrics like fundraising success.

## Key Ideas

- Startup returns are highly concentrated in a few massive winners; most companies are essentially a cost of doing business.
- The best startup ideas often look like bad ideas at first, making them difficult to identify.
- Fundraising success after Demo Day is a misleading metric for long-term potential and can even be an inverse indicator of risk-taking.
- Investors must consciously ignore their intuition and conventional wisdom, similar to a pilot relying on instruments rather than physical sensation.
- The probability of a startup succeeding 'big' is not a constant fraction of the probability of it succeeding at all.

## Notable Claims

- Fundraising success is a misleading metric for startup potential. Evidence: There is no correlation between the percentage of startups that raise money and the financial returns of a batch.
- The best startup ideas often appear as bad ideas. Evidence: Successful companies like Facebook, Microsoft, and Apple were initially dismissed as niche or unimportant.
- Investors are too conservative. Evidence: The high success rate of fundraising after Demo Day suggests investors are not taking enough risks to capture the full potential of outlier returns.

## Quotes
