## Summary

This session from Y Combinator's Startup Investor School features partners Dalton Caldwell and Paul Buchheit, along with YC President Michael Seibel, discussing the art and process of angel investing. The speakers emphasize the importance of having a clear investment process, avoiding groupthink, maintaining personal conviction, and being founder-friendly. They share personal anecdotes of successful and failed investments, highlighting the necessity of backing ambitious, determined founders who can clearly communicate their vision.

## Key Ideas

- Develop a personal investment process funnel to avoid wishy-washy decision-making.
- Avoid groupthink; the best investments are often contrarian or initially unappealing to others.
- Prioritize the team; look for founders who are smarter than you and possess high determination.
- Move fast once you have conviction; slow decision-making leads to adverse selection.
- Don't invest based on 'bargain' prices or cynical ideas; invest in the future you want to see.
- Be founder-friendly: be responsive, don't waste their time, and don't be heavy-handed with unsolicited advice.
- Acknowledge that angel investing involves significant luck and the potential for total loss.

## Notable Claims

- The best startup ideas often look like bad ideas initially. Evidence: Examples like Airbnb (airbeds in homes) and Google (search as a commodity) were initially dismissed by experts.
- Too much capital can be a liability for early-stage startups. Evidence: Companies like Juicero had too much money, allowing them to avoid market reality and build unnecessary products.
- Determination is the most important trait in a founder. Evidence: Startups are inherently difficult; founders need the grit to persist through repeated failure.
