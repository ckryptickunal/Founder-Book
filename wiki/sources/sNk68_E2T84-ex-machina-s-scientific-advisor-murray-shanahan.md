## Summary

Murray Shanahan, a researcher at DeepMind and professor at Imperial College, discusses his career trajectory from 1980s symbolic AI to modern deep reinforcement learning, his role as a scientific advisor for the film 'Ex Machina', and the philosophical implications of artificial general intelligence (AGI).

## Key Ideas

- The 'frame problem' remains a fundamental challenge in AI, concerning how to distinguish relevant information from irrelevant data in complex environments.
- Modern deep reinforcement learning systems like DQN show promise as early forms of AGI but currently lack the efficiency and conceptual understanding of human intelligence.
- Human-machine interaction is heavily influenced by our biological tendency to project consciousness onto entities that exhibit human-like behavior.
- Science fiction, while useful for exploring ethical dilemmas, often prioritizes narrative drama over technical or scientific realism.
- The future of AI likely involves human-machine partnerships where machines provide analytical power and humans provide creative direction.

## Topics

- [[topics/frame-problem|Frame Problem]]: The challenge of determining what information is relevant to a cognitive process in a dynamic environment.
- [[topics/embodiment|Embodiment]]: The theory that intelligence originates from and is shaped by physical interaction with the world.
- [[topics/deep-reinforcement-learning|Deep Reinforcement Learning]]: A machine learning technique where agents learn to make decisions by trial and error to maximize rewards.
- [[topics/artificial-general-intelligence-agi|Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)]]: The hypothetical ability of an intelligent agent to understand or learn any intellectual task that a human being can.

## Notable Claims

- We are currently far from achieving human-level AGI despite rapid progress in specialized AI. Evidence: Current systems lack genuine understanding and require massive amounts of data compared to human learning efficiency.
- The 'frame problem' is a recurring theme in both symbolic AI and modern machine learning. Evidence: Both paradigms struggle with filtering irrelevant information, such as the color of an object in a game versus its functional role.
- Asimov's Laws of Robotics are not technically relevant to modern AI development. Evidence: Current AI systems lack the capability to comprehend or reason about moral laws, and such laws are narrative devices rather than engineering principles.
