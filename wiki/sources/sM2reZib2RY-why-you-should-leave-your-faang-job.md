# Why You Should Leave Your FAANG Job

## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell of Y Combinator discuss why aspiring startup founders should be cautious about working at FAANG companies. They argue that these companies use sophisticated retention tactics, such as staggered equity vesting and lifestyle inflation, to trap talented engineers in roles that often lack meaningful impact or relevant experience for entrepreneurship.

## Key Ideas

- FAANG companies use gamified retention strategies like staggered equity vesting to create 'loss aversion' in employees.
- Many technical roles at large tech companies are unfulfilling, focusing on minor tasks like ad servers or settings rather than core innovation.
- The skills learned at large corporations are often irrelevant to early-stage startups, which lack the massive infrastructure and tooling of big tech.
- Aspiring founders often fall into the trap of lifestyle inflation, making it financially difficult to leave a high-paying job to start a company.
- If you are a talented engineer, you can always return to big tech if a startup fails, minimizing the risk of leaving.
- Founders should enter a FAANG job with a specific, time-bound plan rather than treating it as a default career path.

## Notable Claims

- Most FAANG employees work on insignificant projects rather than core technical challenges. Evidence: Anecdotal evidence from founders who report working on ad servers or minor UI elements.
- The 'experience' gained at big tech is often irrelevant to building a startup from scratch. Evidence: Founders report that the lack of internal tooling in a startup makes their big-tech experience inapplicable.
- Loss aversion regarding unvested equity is a primary reason people stay in jobs they dislike. Evidence: The psychological tendency to overvalue potential future earnings that are contingent on staying.
