## Summary

Sam Altman and Gary Tan conduct live office hours with three early-stage startups (Salary Fairy, Pare Up, and a personal finance app for couples) at Y Combinator's Startup School NY 2014, providing strategic advice on product focus, user retention, and growth tactics.

## Key Ideas

- Focus on building a product that a small group of users loves rather than a broad product that many find interesting.
- Avoid the trap of trying to serve multiple distinct user groups (e.g., workers and employers) simultaneously; pick one and master it.
- Growth should be built into the product rather than treated as an afterthought or relying solely on press/blogs.
- For early-stage startups, high-touch relationships and manual acquisition are necessary to validate the product before scaling.
- Retention metrics and user dependency are the most important indicators of product-market fit.
- Mobile apps are often essential for products requiring frequent, location-based, or real-time interaction.

- [[topics/growth-hacking|Growth Hacking]]: Strategies for acquiring initial users and building virality into the product core.

## Notable Claims

- A startup cannot effectively serve two distinct user groups (e.g., employees and employers) at the same time. Evidence: The partners argue that splitting focus dilutes the product experience and makes it harder to achieve deep user dependency.
- Mobile apps are superior to web apps for products requiring frequent, location-based interaction. Evidence: The partners note that web-based tests for mobile-first behaviors often yield bad data and lower engagement.
- The best way to validate a salary prediction is to help the user secure a job at that price point. Evidence: The partners suggest that statistical data is insufficient to prove a user is underpaid compared to actual market offers.

## Quotes

> It's way more important to build something a small number of users really love than something that a lot of users find a little bit interesting.
> You just have to focus on one, one, one tight little thing and then you can expand from there.
> If you build a sufficiently great product, sometimes that happens. But you don't want to have growth be an afterthought.
