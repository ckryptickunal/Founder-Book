## Summary

Adora Cheung, co-founder of Homejoy, provides a practical guide for early-stage startups on moving from zero to many users. She emphasizes the importance of deep industry immersion, manual processes before automation, rapid iteration based on user feedback, and maintaining a focus on sustainable growth metrics like retention and customer lifetime value.

## Key Ideas

- Dedicate focused, compressed time to your startup rather than fragmented hours.
- Avoid the 'build in stealth' trap; launch early to get real user feedback.
- Become an expert in your industry by performing the service yourself to identify inefficiencies.
- Use manual processes to understand the business before attempting to automate.
- Focus on a specific customer segment initially to optimize the user experience.
- Prioritize retention and customer feedback over vanity metrics.
- Differentiate your product with one or two clear, compelling benefits rather than many minor improvements.
- Pivot if you experience sustained lack of growth despite hard work and iteration.

## Topics

- [[topics/customer-acquisition|Customer Acquisition]]: Strategies for getting initial users, including manual outreach, leveraging personal networks, and finding high-intent locations.
- [[topics/growth-metrics|Growth Metrics]]: The importance of tracking retention, CLV (Customer Lifetime Value), CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost), and Net Promoter Score.
- [[topics/product-development|Product Development]]: Building a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) and the danger of the 'Frankenstein approach' of adding too many features based on unvetted user requests.
- [[topics/pivoting|Pivoting]]: Recognizing when to move on from an idea based on lack of growth, poor retention, or unsustainable economics.

## Notable Claims

- Manual processes should precede automation. Evidence: The speaker explains how they manually handled cleaning professional onboarding to identify key performance indicators before building an automated application system.
- Paid users provide more honest feedback than free users. Evidence: The speaker presents an 'honesty curve' showing that random, paying customers are more likely to provide critical, actionable feedback than friends or family.
- If you go 3-4 weeks with no growth, it may be time to pivot. Evidence: The speaker suggests this as a heuristic for early-stage startups to determine if their fundamental hypothesis is flawed.
