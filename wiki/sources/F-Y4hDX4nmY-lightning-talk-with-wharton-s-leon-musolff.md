## Summary

Leon Musolff presents a field experiment analyzing the sources of Google's market power in web search, specifically investigating the roles of default settings, switching costs, and consumer misperceptions of search engine quality. The study uses a browser extension to track user behavior and concludes that while Google dominates due to high quality, default effects and lack of experience with competitors significantly inflate its market share.

## Key Ideas

- Google's dominance is driven by a combination of high product quality, default effects, and consumer inattention.
- Search engines are 'experience goods,' meaning users often underestimate the quality of alternatives like Bing because they rarely use them.
- Standard choice screens (like those in the EU) are largely ineffective at shifting market share because they do not address the lack of user experience with competitors.
- Banning default payments can reduce Google's market share but risks significantly lowering consumer welfare if users are forced into inferior search experiences.
- A 'delayed choice screen' policy—allowing users to experience a new default for a period before making an active choice—is proposed as a more effective regulatory remedy.

## Notable Claims

- Active choice screens are ineffective at reducing Google's market share. Evidence: Experimental data showed that when users were presented with a choice screen, they overwhelmingly continued to choose Google.
- Consumers have misperceptions about the quality of non-Google search engines. Evidence: Users who were incentivized to use Bing for 14 days reported that their opinion of Bing improved, with many noting it was 'not as bad as I thought'.
- Default settings are highly sticky. Evidence: In the default change group, a significant portion of users remained with the new default search engine even after the incentive period ended.
