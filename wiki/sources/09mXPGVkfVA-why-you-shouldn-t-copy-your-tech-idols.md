# Why You Shouldn't Copy Your Tech Idols

## Summary

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel from Y Combinator discuss the common pitfall of founders attempting to emulate the current advice of tech icons (like Sam Altman, Elon Musk, and Peter Thiel) without considering the actual, often more conventional, paths these individuals took to achieve their initial success.

## Key Ideas

- Successful tech leaders often give advice based on their current perspective rather than the steps they took to reach their initial success.
- Most founders are 'mere mortals' who must follow foundational steps (like building a standard startup) before they can attempt the high-level, capital-intensive strategies of industry titans.
- Advice from icons is often well-intentioned but can be misleading if the recipient lacks the specific advantages, network, or capital the icon currently possesses.
- Contextualizing advice through a person's full history and personal advantages is essential for determining if that advice is applicable to one's own situation.
- Personalized advice is superior to general advice because it accounts for an individual's specific background, strengths, and constraints.

## Notable Claims

- Most founders cannot skip the 'step one' of building a standard startup to jump directly into high-capital, high-risk ventures. Evidence: The hosts note that even icons like Elon Musk had to build conventional businesses (Zip2, PayPal) to gain the capital necessary for his later, more ambitious projects.
- Advice from successful people is often what they wish they could tell their younger selves, rather than what they actually did. Evidence: The hosts observe that successful people often project their current capabilities and desires onto the advice they give to beginners.
