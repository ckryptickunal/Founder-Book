# How to Fund a Startup

## Metadata

## Summary

Paul Graham provides a comprehensive guide to startup funding, detailing the five primary sources of capital—friends and family, consulting, angel investors, seed firms, and venture capital—while emphasizing the importance of maintaining control, managing investor relationships, and understanding the realities of deal-making.

## Key Ideas

- Startup funding functions like gears; take only enough money to reach the next stage of growth.
- Consulting is a dangerous path for startups because it lacks scalability and distracts from product development.
- Investors are not just sources of money but potential sources of conflict; managing them is a critical founder skill.
- Competition is the only real leverage a founder has when negotiating with investors or acquirers.
- Valuation in early-stage startups is largely arbitrary; focus on product and growth rather than optimizing for valuation.
- The 'exit strategy' is a fundamental requirement for venture-backed startups, not just an investor demand.

## Notable Claims

- Consulting is where product companies go to die. Evidence: Consulting work does not scale and distracts founders from building a product that sells to many people.
- The best way to find investors is through personal introductions. Evidence: Cold-calling or emailing business plans is largely ineffective and often viewed as a sign of laziness.
- Cheapness is power for a startup. Evidence: Lower burn rates provide founders with more options and reduce the pressure to accept unfavorable deals.
