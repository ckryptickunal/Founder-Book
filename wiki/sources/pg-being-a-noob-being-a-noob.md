# Being a Noob

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-being-a-noob`

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the psychological discomfort of feeling like a 'noob' (novice), arguing that this sensation is often a positive indicator of intellectual growth and engagement with novelty rather than a sign of incompetence.

## Key Ideas

- The feeling of being a noob is inversely correlated with actual ignorance.
- Evolutionary biology conditioned humans to dislike the feeling of being a noob because it signaled a lack of competence in a stable environment.
- In a rapidly changing modern world, the aversion to feeling like a noob is counterproductive to learning and innovation.
- Embracing the discomfort of being a novice is essential for global knowledge acquisition.

## Notable Claims

- The feeling of being a noob is inversely correlated with actual ignorance. Evidence: Moving to a new country or field increases knowledge while simultaneously increasing the feeling of being a novice.
- The aversion to feeling like a noob is an evolutionary relic. Evidence: Hunter-gatherers needed to prioritize competence in stable environments, whereas modern life requires constant adaptation to new fields.

## Quotes

> The more of a noob you are locally, the less of a noob you are globally.
> Though it feels unpleasant, and people will sometimes ridicule you for it, the more you feel like a noob, the better.
