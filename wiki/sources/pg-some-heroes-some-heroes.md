## Summary

Paul Graham reflects on his personal heroes, defining them not merely as admirable figures, but as individuals who influenced his own decision-making process. He identifies a common pattern among them: an excessive dedication to their work and an uncompromising, subversive honesty. The list spans diverse fields including sports, art, literature, engineering, and technology.

## Key Ideas

- A hero is someone whose example you use to decide what to do in a given situation.
- True heroes possess two core qualities: excessive care for their work and absolute, non-pandering honesty.
- The best work is often done for oneself rather than for an audience.
- Taste is a critical, often overlooked, component of leadership and product design.
- To achieve greatness, one must focus on significant, difficult questions rather than immediate, small rewards.
- Integrity involves being as rigorous about identifying one's own errors as one is about the ideas themselves.

## Notable Claims

- The best work is often created for oneself, not for an audience. Evidence: Leonardo da Vinci's drawings were explorations of the world that hold their own as art despite not being intended for public consumption.
- It is possible to avoid being wrong by only speaking when one is certain. Evidence: Robert Morris's approach to communication and his track record of accuracy.
