# Starting a Startup After Business School - Reham Fagiri and Kalam Dennis of AptDeco

## Summary

Reham Fagiri and Kalam Dennis, founders of AptDeco, discuss their journey from traditional corporate backgrounds and business school to building a successful furniture marketplace. They emphasize the importance of practical, hands-on problem solving, the value of Y Combinator's mentorship, and the necessity of building scalable operational structures.

## Key Ideas

- Validate ideas by testing them in the real world, such as adding delivery to existing Craigslist listings to gauge demand.
- The 'sharing economy' model can be applied to furniture by solving the logistics and trust issues inherent in peer-to-peer transactions.
- Founders should not be afraid to do unscalable tasks (like manual deliveries) early on to understand the business before automating.
- Business school and corporate experience provide valuable skills in financial modeling, team management, and conflict resolution, which are often overlooked in startup culture.
- Transparency and regular, structured check-ins (KPI meetings, 1-on-1s) are essential for maintaining team stability and accountability.
- Avoid premature scaling, such as leasing a fleet of vans before understanding seasonal demand or unit economics.
- Focus on a few acquisition channels and master them rather than spreading resources too thin.

## Notable Claims

- Warehousing furniture is cost-prohibitive and limits scalability. Evidence: The founders argue that a lightweight, direct-pickup model is more sustainable and avoids the space constraints of traditional consignment.
- If an employee is surprised by being fired, the manager has failed. Evidence: The founders advocate for constant, transparent feedback loops so that performance issues are addressed long before termination becomes necessary.
- Out-of-home advertising (subway ads) can be an effective, albeit risky, early growth channel. Evidence: AptDeco used subway ads to create a 'halo effect' that made the company appear larger and more established than it was.
