# How Should Little Tech Approach D.C. and Brussels?

## Summary

This panel discussion at a Y Combinator event explores how early-stage 'little tech' founders can effectively engage with policymakers in Washington D.C. and Brussels. The speakers emphasize that founders do not need expensive lobbyists to influence policy; instead, they should leverage their personal stories, build trust networks, and directly contact antitrust regulators and legislative offices to report anti-competitive behavior.

## Key Ideas

- Founders can influence policy by sharing direct, anecdotal evidence of market exclusion rather than relying on professional lobbyists.
- The 'monopoly lobbying industrial complex' is powerful, but policymakers are increasingly looking for 'little tech' perspectives to balance the conversation.
- The Digital Markets Act (DMA) in Europe provides concrete, actionable rights for developers, such as interoperability requests, which are currently underutilized.
- Building dense, high-trust networks among founders is a key strategy for sharing tribal knowledge about navigating government and regulatory landscapes.
- State-level engagement and working with advocacy groups are low-cost, high-impact ways for small companies to amplify their voices.

## Topics

- [[topics/antitrust-advocacy|Antitrust Advocacy]]: The process of reporting anti-competitive behavior to government bodies to ensure a level playing field.
- [[topics/regulatory-engagement|Regulatory Engagement]]: Strategies for startup founders to communicate with legislators and regulators without traditional lobbying infrastructure.
- [[topics/interoperability|Interoperability]]: The technical and legal requirements for different software systems to work together, mandated by regulations like the DMA.

## Notable Claims

- Monopoly power is profitable, and large companies spend significant resources on lobbying to maintain that power. Evidence: The panel notes that monopoly rents are used to fund lobbyists who visit government offices with stacks of paperwork.
- Founders do not need to hire expensive lobbyists to be heard by Congress. Evidence: Congressional staffers state they are willing to meet with founders who have clear, fact-based stories and examples.
- The DMA provides immediate, enforceable rights for developers. Evidence: The European Commission has already issued specification decisions regarding Apple's iOS interoperability.
