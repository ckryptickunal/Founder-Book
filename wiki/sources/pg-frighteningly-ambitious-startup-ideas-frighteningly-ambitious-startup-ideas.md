# Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas

## Summary

Paul Graham explores why the most ambitious and potentially transformative startup ideas are often the most intimidating to founders, arguing that the best approach is to start with small, manageable projects that move in a 'westerly' direction rather than attempting a direct, high-pressure frontal assault on massive problems.

## Key Ideas

- The most ambitious startup ideas are often frightening because they threaten a founder's identity and sense of capability.
- Big ideas are often invisible to founders because their subconscious filters them out due to fear.
- Successful startups often start with a small, 'harmless' product before expanding into a massive, industry-disrupting platform.
- Founders should operate like explorers heading west rather than architects building a rigid future.
- The best way to compete with incumbents is to build something for a small, elite group of users (like hackers) that is genuinely better than the status quo.

## Notable Claims

- The best startup ideas are just on the right side of impossible. Evidence: Historical examples of companies like Google and Facebook starting with small, niche user bases.
- Revenue is a lagging indicator in the technology business. Evidence: The example of Microsoft continuing to grow revenue while losing its innovative edge.
- Ongoing medical diagnosis will eventually replace symptom-based care. Evidence: The inefficiency and danger of waiting for physical symptoms to appear for conditions like heart disease and cancer.

## Quotes

> The best ideas are just on the right side of impossible.
> If you want to take on a problem as big as the ones I've discussed, don't make a direct frontal attack on it.
> Empirically, the way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things.
> Don't try to construct the future like a building, because your current blueprint is almost certainly mistaken.
