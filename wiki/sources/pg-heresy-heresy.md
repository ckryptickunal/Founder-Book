## Summary

Paul Graham explores the modern resurgence of 'heresy'—the social practice of punishing individuals for expressing specific opinions regardless of their truth value. He argues that this phenomenon is driven by 'aggressively conventional-minded' people who use ideologies to enforce orthodoxy, mirroring historical religious intolerance and the Cultural Revolution.

## Key Ideas

- Heresy is defined by two traits: it prioritizes ideological purity over truth, and it outweighs all other virtues or past actions of the speaker.
- The 'aggressively conventional-minded' are the primary enforcers of orthodoxy, often uniting in mob-like dynamics when guided by a new ideology.
- Modern 'cancel culture' functions structurally like historical heresy, where the punishment (losing one's job) is treated as a moral necessity.
- The current wave of intolerance is a secular, ideological phenomenon that has narrowed the window of free expression since the mid-1980s.
- The most effective way to resist heresy is to develop 'social antibodies' and recognize the patterns of ideological suppression to prevent future waves.

## Topics

## Notable Claims

- The current wave of intolerance is structurally equivalent to historical religious heresy. Evidence: Both prioritize ideological adherence over truth and treat the expression of forbidden ideas as a crime that outweighs all other personal virtues.
- The window of free expression in Western society has been shrinking since the mid-1980s. Evidence: The author notes a negative derivative in freedom of expression, contrasting it with the expansion of rights seen in the decades prior to 1985.
