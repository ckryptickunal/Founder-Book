# Design and Research

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-design-and-research`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: January 2003
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-design-and-research

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that programming language development should be approached as a design discipline rather than a research project. He emphasizes that good design requires focusing on the user's actual needs, maintaining a tight feedback loop through prototyping, and ensuring the designer is also a user of the product to maintain quality and morale.

## Key Ideas

- Design focuses on 'good' while research focuses on 'new'; the best work converges both.
- Designers must solve for what users need, not just what they ask for, similar to a doctor-patient relationship.
- The best design occurs when the designer is also the intended user.
- Programming languages are mediums for development, not just finished products; the process of writing code should be as elegant as the result.
- Prototyping and iterative refinement ('Worse is Better') are superior to 'Hail Mary' strategies of building finished products in one go.
- Good design requires a single person in control; 'design by committee' typically results in poor outcomes.

## Notable Claims

- Programming languages created for the designer's own use are generally better than those created for others. Evidence: Graham cites C, Lisp, and Smalltalk as examples of languages created by their designers for personal use, contrasting them with languages like Cobol, Ada, and Java.
- Good design requires a single person in control. Evidence: Graham notes that 'design by committee' is a synonym for bad design and that true collaboration in the arts is rare compared to the sciences.

## Quotes

> You have to design for the user, but you have to design what the user needs, not simply what he says he wants.
> If you think you're designing something for idiots, the odds are that you're not designing something good, even for idiots.
> Design by committee is a synonym for bad design.
