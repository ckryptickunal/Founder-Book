## Summary

This episode of the Light Cone podcast explores how startups can build powerful, specialized generative AI models with limited resources, challenging the narrative that only massive, well-funded organizations like OpenAI can compete in the field. The discussion highlights how Y Combinator companies are leveraging high-quality data, efficient computation hacks, and rapid learning to build foundation models for diverse applications ranging from video and audio to weather prediction, biology, and hardware design.

## Key Ideas

- Startups can compete with major AI labs by focusing on niche, high-value verticals rather than general-purpose models.
- Expertise is not a barrier to entry; founders can reach the cutting edge by intensely studying AI papers and networking within a few months.
- Data quality and synthetic data generation are more critical than having massive, uncurated datasets.
- Computational efficiency can be achieved through techniques like custom kernels, smaller model architectures, and compressing data.
- The 'SpaceTime' patch concept used in video models like Sora is being applied to other sequential data types, such as EEG signals.
- Foundational models are effective general-purpose function approximators capable of simulating complex real-world physics.

## Topics

- [[topics/spacetime-patches|SpaceTime Patches]]: A technique for processing sequential data (video, EEG) by chunking it into spatial and temporal components.
- [[topics/computational-efficiency|Computational Efficiency]]: Methods to reduce GPU requirements, such as using custom kernels and smaller, specialized models.

## Notable Claims

- Startups can build foundation models with only $500,000 in funding. Evidence: Multiple companies in the YC Winter 2024 batch successfully trained specialized models within the batch period.
- Synthetic data is a viable way to improve model performance. Evidence: Companies like 'Find' used synthetic data from programming competitions to create high-quality coding co-pilots.
- AI models can outperform traditional physics-based simulations. Evidence: Atmo's AI weather model is more accurate than the billion-dollar, traditional Noah-funded model.
