# A founder-friendly term sheet

## Metadata

## Summary

Sam Altman outlines his philosophy and specific practices for creating founder-friendly term sheets for early-stage investments, emphasizing simplicity and the removal of predatory clauses.

## Key Ideas

- Investors should prioritize attracting the best companies by offering fair terms rather than focusing on downside protection.
- Option pools should not be carved out of pre-money valuation, as dilution for new hires should be shared by all shareholders.
- Complex deal structures often lead to unintended negative consequences and set poor precedents for future funding rounds.
- Investors should cover their own legal fees to preserve company capital.

## Notable Claims

- Taking an option pool out of the pre-money valuation is an artificial manipulation of valuation. Evidence: The author argues that new hires benefit all shareholders, therefore dilution should be shared by everyone, not just founders.
- Early-stage investors should not focus on downside protection. Evidence: The author suggests that the upside risk theory makes it more beneficial to offer compelling terms to attract the best companies.

## Quotes

> If you believe the upside risk theory, then it makes sense to offer compelling terms and forgo some downside protection to get the best companies to want to work with you.
> I have an allergic reaction to complex deal structures, as they invariably end up with all sorts of unintended consequences.
