## Summary

The first day of Y Combinator's Startup Investor School provides a foundational overview of angel and seed investing, emphasizing the power law, the importance of founder relationships, and the mechanics of the SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) instrument.

## Key Ideas

- Angel investing is driven by the power law: a single successful investment often returns more than all others combined.
- The primary goal is to identify potential 'homeruns' rather than focusing on failure rates or small, safe returns.
- Founders are the most critical factor; look for obsession, intelligence, communication skills, and rapid execution speed.
- Avoid 'scenesters' and those motivated solely by quick wealth; prioritize mission-driven founders.
- The SAFE is a simple, non-debt instrument designed for early-stage startups to avoid complex legal negotiations.
- Reputation is a critical asset; being a helpful, responsive, and fair investor leads to better deal flow and founder referrals.
- Avoid 'value investing' mentalities; the best investments often feel expensive or like 'bad ideas' that are actually good.

## Topics

- [[topics/power-law|Power Law]]: The principle that a small number of investments generate the vast majority of returns in a portfolio.
- [[topics/founder-evaluation|Founder Evaluation]]: Assessing founders based on traits like obsession, communication, and rate of improvement rather than just the business idea.
- [[topics/safe-mechanics|SAFE Mechanics]]: Technical details on how SAFE instruments convert into equity during priced rounds, including valuation caps and pro-rata rights.
- [[topics/investor-reputation|Investor Reputation]]: The long-term value of being a founder-friendly investor to secure future deal flow and maintain positive relationships.

## Notable Claims

- The best startup investments often sound like bad ideas initially. Evidence: Examples like Reddit were dismissed by many as 'dumb' or 'unprofitable' in their early stages.
- A startup's current market size is less important than its potential market size in 10 years. Evidence: Massive companies like Uber and Facebook entered markets that were either non-existent or tiny at their inception.
- The SAFE is not debt and should not be treated as such. Evidence: It lacks maturity dates, interest, and repayment obligations, functioning as a convertible security for future equity.
