## Summary

Andrej Karpathy discusses the evolution of software development in the era of AI, categorizing it into three paradigms: Software 1.0 (traditional code), Software 2.0 (neural network weights), and Software 3.0 (LLMs programmed via natural language). He emphasizes that LLMs act as new operating systems and utilities, requiring developers to build 'partial autonomy' applications that keep humans in the loop for verification. He advocates for 'meeting LLMs halfway' by creating agent-friendly documentation and infrastructure to facilitate faster, more reliable AI-assisted development.

## Key Ideas

- Software is evolving through three distinct paradigms: 1.0 (code), 2.0 (neural weights), and 3.0 (natural language prompts).
- LLMs function as 'people spirits' or stochastic simulators with superhuman knowledge but significant cognitive deficits like hallucinations and lack of persistent memory.
- The most effective current AI applications are 'partial autonomy' tools that use a GUI to allow humans to quickly verify and audit AI-generated work.
- Developers should build for agents by creating LLM-readable documentation (e.g., markdown, removing 'click' instructions in favor of API/curl commands).
- The 'autonomy slider' concept: products should allow users to tune the level of AI agency, moving from augmentation to full automation over time.
- We are currently in the 1960s era of LLM computing, characterized by centralized cloud-based 'time-sharing' of expensive intelligence.

## Notable Claims

- LLMs are currently in the '1960s' of computing. Evidence: Compute is expensive, centralized in the cloud, and accessed via time-sharing, similar to early mainframe computing.
- Software 2.0 is eating the software stack. Evidence: At Tesla, neural networks replaced significant portions of C++ code in the Autopilot system.
- LLMs flip the traditional technology diffusion model. Evidence: Unlike electricity or GPS, which started with government/corporate use, LLMs were adopted by consumers first.
