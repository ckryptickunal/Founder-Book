# The Submarine

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the influence of the public relations industry on mainstream media, arguing that a significant portion of non-political news is manufactured by PR firms to create artificial trends and buzz. He contrasts this 'artificial' corporate journalism with the authenticity of online blogging, suggesting that the structural decline of traditional media is driven by readers' preference for honest, individual voices over PR-driven content.

## Key Ideas

- PR firms act as a 'submarine' beneath the news, planting stories that appear as organic trends.
- Reporters often rely on PR firms due to laziness or being overworked, leading to the recycling of press releases.
- The 'buzz' generated by PR is often a self-reinforcing cycle where multiple outlets report the same story simultaneously.
- PR diving is the practice of tracing multiple news articles back to a single source document or pitch.
- The decline of traditional media is structural, as readers increasingly favor the authenticity of online writing over corporate journalism.
- PR firms are beginning to target bloggers, potentially threatening the integrity of the online writing environment.

## Notable Claims

- More than half of non-political, non-disaster news stories in traditional media originate from PR firms. Evidence: Graham's personal experience as a startup founder paying for PR services to secure press hits.
- The 'suit is back' trend stories are manufactured by PR firms for clients like The Men's Wearhouse. Evidence: Analysis of multiple articles across different publications that cite the same experts and themes.
- The decline of traditional media is structural, not cyclic. Evidence: Readers are abandoning traditional media because they recognize the artificiality of the writing compared to online alternatives.

## Quotes
