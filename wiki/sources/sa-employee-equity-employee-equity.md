# Employee Equity

## Summary

Sam Altman outlines the systemic issues with startup employee equity compensation, arguing that current practices are often unfair or opaque. He proposes structural changes to option grants, exercise windows, tax treatment, and transparency to better align employee incentives with long-term company success.

## Key Ideas

- Employees are often under-compensated in equity relative to the value they create.
- The 90-day post-termination exercise window is a major flaw that forces employees to forfeit vested options.
- Startups should adopt 10-year exercise windows to provide employees with actual liquidity opportunities.
- Transparency regarding fully-diluted ownership percentages and liquidation preferences is essential for employees.
- Standard 4-year vesting schedules may be outdated; longer or back-weighted vesting with higher grants can better align long-term incentives.
- Option pools are flexible accounting tools and should not be used as an excuse to limit employee grants.

## Notable Claims

- The 90-day exercise window is unfair to employees. Evidence: Employees often lack the cash to cover the strike price and tax bill for options upon leaving a company.
- Option pools are a fiction. Evidence: Boards can increase the size of the option pool at any time, meaning it should not be a constraint on hiring.
- Employees should demand to know their percentage of fully-diluted shares. Evidence: The number of shares is meaningless without context regarding the total share count and potential liquidation preferences.

## Quotes

> Option pools are complete fiction; boards can increase them whenever they want.
> Employees should demand to know what percentage of the fully-diluted shares their stock options represent, and be very suspect of any startup that won’t tell them.
> Tax optimization is a second-order issue, and for an immediate solution, I think extending exercise windows to 10 years is the most important thing to do.
