# Party rounds

## Metadata

- Video ID: `sa-party-rounds`

## Summary

Sam Altman critiques the Silicon Valley trend of 'party rounds,' where startups raise small amounts of capital from a large number of investors, arguing that this practice lacks the necessary oversight and strategic support required for long-term success.

## Key Ideas

- Party rounds involve raising small amounts from many investors (10-50+), which often leads to a lack of accountability.
- Investors tend to be involved in proportion to the amount of capital they have at stake.
- A dedicated investor provides a 'focusing function' through regular progress reviews.
- Party rounds can make subsequent fundraising more difficult due to a lack of lead investor support and social proof.
- The ease of raising party rounds may lower the bar for quality, allowing weaker companies to get funded.

## Notable Claims

- Party rounds are detrimental to the long-term success of startups. Evidence: Lack of a single investor with enough stake to provide consistent oversight and guidance.
- Companies that raise party rounds have a harder time raising subsequent rounds. Evidence: Observation that these companies lack the coordination and bridging support provided by a lead VC.

## Quotes

> It turns out that investors are generally about as involved as they are invested.
> The cadence and rhythm of meeting with someone every month to review progress and goals turns out to be an important focusing function.
> Good investors stay out of the way when you don’t need them and help you a lot when you do.
