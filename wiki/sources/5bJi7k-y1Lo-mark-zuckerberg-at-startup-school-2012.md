## Summary

Mark Zuckerberg discusses the early days of Facebook at Y Combinator's Startup School 2012, highlighting the importance of real identity, bootstrapping, and the value of building products that solve fundamental human needs.

## Key Ideas

- Real identity was established by leveraging school-issued email addresses to prevent fake accounts.
- Growth was constrained by server capacity and budget, which forced the team to focus on efficiency and product quality.
- Facebook's expansion strategy involved targeting schools with existing social networks to prove the product's superiority.
- The 'Zuckerberg's Law' observation suggests that the amount of content individuals share online doubles every year.
- Flexibility and exploring hobbies are crucial for entrepreneurs before committing to a specific company.
- Social networks extend fundamental human capacities for connection and social interaction.

## Notable Claims

- People share twice as much content online every year. Evidence: Zuckerberg observes an exponential growth trend in sharing behavior, similar to Moore's Law.
- Facebook's early success was driven by the requirement of school email addresses. Evidence: This ensured real identity and prevented fake accounts, creating a trusted community.
- Facebook was not initially intended to be a company. Evidence: Zuckerberg built it as a hobby to solve a personal need for connection at Harvard.
