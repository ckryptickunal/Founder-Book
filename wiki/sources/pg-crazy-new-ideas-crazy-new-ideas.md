# Crazy New Ideas

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-crazy-new-ideas`

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the counterintuitive value of 'crazy' ideas proposed by domain experts, arguing that such ideas are often dismissed due to societal conservatism, envy, or the limitations of current paradigms, and suggests that we should treat them with curiosity rather than immediate skepticism.

## Key Ideas

- Implausible ideas from reasonable domain experts are disproportionately likely to be valuable.
- The current paradigm acts as a filter that causes even the originators of new ideas to undervalue them.
- Dismissing new ideas is often driven by envy, a desire to appear sophisticated, or a vested interest in the status quo.
- New ideas are often 'feeble' at birth, making them easy targets for criticism compared to established, 'full-grown' ideas.
- The most effective response to a seemingly wrong idea from an expert is to ask questions rather than make statements.

## Topics

- [[topics/epistemology|Epistemology]]: The study of how we evaluate the truth and potential of new, unconventional ideas.
- [[topics/paradigm-shifts|Paradigm Shifts]]: The process by which scientific and intellectual frameworks change over time, often meeting resistance.
- [[topics/innovation-psychology|Innovation Psychology]]: The social and psychological factors that lead people to either support or attack radical new concepts.

## Notable Claims

- If a reasonable domain expert proposes a crazy idea, it is more likely to be interesting than wrong. Evidence: The expert's deep knowledge suggests they see something others do not, and the 'efficient market' of ideas suggests crazy-sounding ideas have the highest potential impact.
- People attack new ideas to appear sophisticated. Evidence: New ideas are often 'feeble' at birth, making them easy targets for 'devastating' attacks that make the attacker look clever.

## Quotes

> If the person proposing the idea is reasonable, then they know how implausible it sounds. And yet they're proposing it anyway. That suggests they know something you don't.
> The wise response to such an idea is not to make statements, but to ask questions, because there's a real mystery here.
> Few understand how feeble new ideas look when they first appear.
