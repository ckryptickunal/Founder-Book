# The Airbnbs

## Summary

Paul Graham reflects on the early days of Airbnb during their time at Y Combinator, highlighting the founders' exceptional work ethic, resilience during the 2008 recession, and their commitment to achieving 'ramen profitability' through hands-on customer engagement.

## Key Ideas

- Founders' earnestness and work ethic are primary indicators of startup success.
- Extreme conditions reveal the true nature and commitment of founders.
- Ramen profitability is a critical milestone that grants a startup independence from investors.
- Growth is best achieved by focusing on a specific, high-demand subset of the market.
- Direct, hands-on interaction with early users (like taking professional photos for hosts) provides invaluable insights and drives growth.

## Topics

- [[topics/startup-resilience|Startup Resilience]]: The ability of founders to persist through financial hardship and lack of initial market traction.
- [[topics/ramen-profitability|Ramen Profitability]]: The threshold where a startup generates enough revenue to cover the founders' basic living expenses.
- [[topics/growth-hacking|Growth Hacking]]: The strategy of focusing on a specific market segment and performing manual, high-touch tasks to improve product-market fit.
- [[topics/founder-psychology|Founder Psychology]]: The importance of genuine belief in a product's future over purely financial motivations.

## Notable Claims

- The Airbnb founders had the best attitude of any startup funded by Y Combinator at that time. Evidence: Paul Graham's direct observation of their work ethic, note-taking, and rapid implementation of feedback.
- Airbnb was on the verge of failure when they joined Y Combinator. Evidence: They had maxed out credit cards and had no growth after a year of work.
- Focusing on a specific market segment (New York City) was the catalyst for Airbnb's growth. Evidence: The founders traveled to NYC to improve listings, which led to measurable revenue increases.

## Quotes

> People, like matter, reveal their nature under extreme conditions.
> Ramen profitability is not, obviously, the end goal of any startup, but it's the most important threshold on the way, because this is the point where you're airborne.
> We are not going to slow down.
