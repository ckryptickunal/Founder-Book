# The Untold Stories of Y Combinator with Co-Founder Jessica Livingston

## Summary

Jessica Livingston, co-founder of Y Combinator, discusses her unconventional path from a traditional upbringing to becoming a pivotal figure in the startup ecosystem. She reflects on her early career in finance and PR, the accidental creation of Y Combinator with Paul Graham, and the importance of building community, events, and providing agency to founders.

## Key Ideas

- The importance of having a 'nest egg' to provide the flexibility and risk tolerance needed to start a company.
- Y Combinator's success was built on a combination of software, events, and community, rather than just capital.
- The value of 'social radar'—the ability to read people and situations—is developed through high-frequency exposure to many founders and their stories.
- Founders should focus on building things and solving problems rather than worrying about traditional career paths or prestige.
- The power of having a co-founder with complementary skills and shared values.
- The 'untold' stories of startups—the struggles, failures, and early days—are the most valuable for inspiring future entrepreneurs.

## Notable Claims

- Anyone who is sufficiently ambitious and determined can start a startup. Evidence: Jessica's observation of thousands of founders who all start as 'nobodies' and overcome adversity.
- The 10-minute interview format is highly effective for assessing founders. Evidence: The ability to observe body language and interpersonal dynamics between co-founders in a short timeframe.
- Having a financial safety net is crucial for taking career risks. Evidence: Personal reflection on the inability to leave jobs when living paycheck to paycheck.

## Quotes

> I always felt like I got involved in startups like 15 years too late.
> If you feel excited about something, to do something where you don't need to have a boss or you don't need to ask permission. To do it for yourself.
> I do think that my varied background... I was able to draw on certain things for Y Combinator.
> I've always believed and I still believe that anyone who is sufficiently ambitious and determined and reasonably smart can start a startup.
