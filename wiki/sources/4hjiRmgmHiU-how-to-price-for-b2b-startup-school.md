## Summary

Tom from Y Combinator provides a framework for B2B startup pricing, emphasizing the 'value equation' as the primary driver for setting prices. He advises founders to avoid underpricing, focus on value-based metrics rather than cost-plus models, and use iterative testing to find the optimal price point.

## Key Ideas

- Use a 'value equation' to calculate the cost savings or revenue increases your product provides and charge roughly 25-50% of that value.
- Costs should be treated as a floor for pricing, not the basis for it; aim for 80-90% gross margins.
- Avoid price wars with competitors; instead, differentiate your product through unique functionality or niche focus.
- Align pricing structure with industry norms (e.g., flat fees vs. usage-based) to reduce friction.
- Use pilots to prove the value equation, but keep them short and tied to clear success criteria.
- If unsure, start with a price and increase it by 50% for each new prospect until you reach a 25% loss rate due to price.

## Notable Claims

- Founders often underprice their software because they lack calibration on what large companies pay. Evidence: Founders compare B2B software to personal subscriptions like GitHub or ChatGPT, leading to unrealistically low pricing.
- Pricing solely based on cost leads to unsustainable business models. Evidence: Cost-plus pricing ignores the actual value provided to the customer, often resulting in margins that cannot support a sales team.
- A 5:1 ratio between new ARR and total sales compensation is a good rule of thumb. Evidence: This ratio helps determine if a contract size is large enough to support an outbound sales team.
