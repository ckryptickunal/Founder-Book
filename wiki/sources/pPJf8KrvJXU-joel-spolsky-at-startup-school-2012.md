## Summary

Joel Spolsky explains the fundamental distinction between 'organic growth' (bootstrapped) companies and 'get big fast' (VC-funded) companies, using his experiences with Fog Creek Software, Stack Exchange, and Trello to illustrate how to choose the right strategy based on market dynamics and network effects.

## Key Ideas

- Distinguish between organic growth businesses (long-term, profitable, no network effects) and 'get big fast' businesses (land grabs, high network effects, high burn rate).
- Bootstrapping allows founders to retain control and delay dilution, making future fundraising easier and more favorable.
- Network effects and user lock-in are the primary drivers for 'get big fast' strategies.
- Trying to straddle both models simultaneously is a recipe for failure.
- Use profitable 'cash cow' products to fund experimental 'get big fast' projects internally without needing external VC capital.

## Topics

- [[topics/bootstrapping|Bootstrapping]]: Building a business using internal revenue rather than external capital to maintain control and ensure survival.
- [[topics/network-effects|Network Effects]]: The phenomenon where a product becomes more valuable as more people use it, creating a 'land grab' necessity.
- [[topics/fundraising-strategy|Fundraising Strategy]]: The importance of delaying fundraising until the company has traction to minimize equity dilution.
- [[topics/product-lifecycle|Product Lifecycle]]: Managing multiple products with different growth profiles within a single organization.

## Notable Claims

- You cannot be the CEO of two companies simultaneously due to intellectual property conflicts. Evidence: Spolsky notes he had to step down from active management at Fog Creek to focus on Stack Exchange.
- Raising money is significantly easier after building a reputation and proving traction. Evidence: Spolsky raised money for Stack Exchange in two weeks because of his history with Fog Creek and the site's growth.
- Organic growth companies have a much higher probability of becoming a $10 million revenue business than a startup has of becoming a $10 billion business. Evidence: Based on the statistical rarity of 'get big fast' successes compared to the abundance of sustainable small-to-medium businesses.

## Quotes

> There isn't enough money in all the VCs in California to fund all your companies.
> You have to decide if you're going to be a slow growth company or get big fast company because anytime you try to straddle those two lines, painful things happen to you.
> At any given time, you always want to be raising the minimum amount of money you need to barely survive.
