# Simple Products That Became Big Companies – Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel

## Summary

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel from Y Combinator discuss common 'rookie mistakes' made by startup founders, emphasizing the importance of starting with a simple, functional product rather than over-engineering, avoiding the trap of 'boiling the ocean,' and the necessity of obtaining ground truth from customers instead of relying on vanity metrics or external validation.

## Key Ideas

- A product with one working feature is infinitely better than a product with many broken features.
- Founders often fall into 'Google-itis,' trying to build a complex, mature product immediately rather than starting with the simple MVP that successful companies began with.
- Validation from investors, press, or awards is not a substitute for actual customer demand and revenue.
- Discipline in doing focused work is more valuable than raw intelligence for startup success.
- Crowdfunding hardware before having a proven, manufacturable product often leads to public backlash and reputational damage.
- Founders must observe problems firsthand rather than relying on historical patterns or 'copy-pasting' business models from other markets.

## Notable Claims

- A product with many features that don't work is worse than a product with one feature that works. Evidence: The hosts compare it to a hypothetical payroll company that offers benefits and 401k but fails to pay employees, which would be universally hated.
- Discipline is more important than raw intelligence for startup success. Evidence: The hosts note that focused work and execution consistently outperform high-intelligence founders who are distracted by conferences and hiring.

## Quotes

> For every ounce of discipline you have, you can probably lose five ten ounces of intelligence and still outperform.
