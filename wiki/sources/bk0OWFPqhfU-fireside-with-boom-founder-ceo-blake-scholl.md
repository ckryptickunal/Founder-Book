## Summary

Blake Scholl, founder and CEO of Boom Supersonic, discusses his mission to make supersonic flight affordable and sustainable. He argues that the aerospace industry has stagnated due to government-led prestige projects like the Concorde and the Apollo program, which lacked commercial viability. Scholl emphasizes that Boom is leveraging existing, proven technologies—rather than inventing new ones—to build a commercially successful supersonic airliner, aiming to re-industrialize America and compete with global rivals.

## Key Ideas

- Supersonic flight should be affordable and accessible to the general public, not just a luxury for the elite.
- Government-led prestige projects like the Concorde and Apollo hindered commercial aerospace innovation by ignoring economic sustainability.
- Hardware innovation is best achieved by combining proven technologies in new ways rather than making high-risk technical bets on day one.
- The US needs to rebuild its commercial industrial base to ensure national security and economic leadership.
- IP and patent litigation are secondary to maintaining a high rate of product innovation and delivering the best product.
- Boeing's decline as an innovator has created a vacuum that new entrants must fill to maintain American aerospace leadership.

## Notable Claims

- The Concorde and Apollo programs killed their respective industries by prioritizing government prestige over commercial sustainability. Evidence: Both projects were Cold War-era government ventures that lacked a viable commercial business model.
- Boom Supersonic is not inventing new technology but rather applying 20-year-old proven technology to a new design. Evidence: The company uses existing carbon fiber composites, digital aerodynamics, and turbofan engines.
- China is running a 'slow motion takeover' of the aerospace industry by cloning American aircraft designs. Evidence: China is currently developing clones of the 737, 787, and is now working on a supersonic jet.
