## Summary

FTC Chair Lina Khan visited Y Combinator to discuss the Federal Trade Commission's role in regulating technology markets, focusing on antitrust enforcement, consumer protection, and the impact of government policy on innovation and startups.

## Key Ideas

- Antitrust enforcement is essential for maintaining open, competitive markets that allow new startups to scale.
- Historical precedents like the 1956 AT&T consent decree demonstrate how government intervention can spur innovation by opening up locked-in technologies.
- The FTC is concerned with 'moat-building' and anti-competitive acquisitions that prevent new entrants from competing.
- The FTC is actively investigating bottlenecks in the AI ecosystem, including compute, data, and model access.
- The FTC aims to be a 'friend to entrepreneurs' by ensuring a level playing field where success is based on merit rather than exploiting existing privileges.
- The agency is increasingly hiring technologists to better understand and regulate algorithmic collusion and complex digital business models.

- [[topics/artificial-intelligence-regulation|Artificial Intelligence Regulation]]: The FTC's approach to monitoring competition, privacy, and bottlenecks in the AI development pipeline.

## Notable Claims

- Antitrust enforcement is critical for allowing the next tier of innovators to scale. Evidence: Historical examples like the 1956 AT&T consent decree and the 1990s Microsoft case.
- The FTC is not an enemy of tech, but a friend to open markets. Evidence: The agency's mandate is to ensure that success is based on the quality of products rather than exploiting market power.
- Dominant platforms often switch from pro-consumer strategies to 'moat-building' once they achieve scale. Evidence: Observations of the lifecycle of dominant firms and their defensive acquisitions.
