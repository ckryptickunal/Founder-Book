## Summary

Steve Bannon and Ro Khanna discuss the evolving relationship between the Trump administration, populist economic nationalism, and big tech. They explore the tension between the 'MAGA' movement's populist goals and the influence of tech oligarchs, debating whether the current administration will pursue aggressive antitrust enforcement or succumb to corporate lobbying.

## Key Ideas

- The Trump administration represents an uneasy coalition between populist nationalists and big tech interests.
- There is a growing cross-party consensus, including figures like Lina Khan and Ro Khanna, on the need to curb the power of tech monopolies.
- The 'neoliberal' and 'neocon' establishment in Washington remains a significant obstacle to populist economic reforms.
- The defense budget and tax policy for the wealthy are identified as critical litmus tests for the administration's commitment to the working class.
- Big tech companies are accused of using national security arguments (e.g., competing with China) to secure bailouts and government resources.

## Topics

- [[topics/antitrust-policy|Antitrust Policy]]: The effort to break up or regulate large tech monopolies to foster competition and protect small businesses.
- [[topics/populist-nationalism|Populist Nationalism]]: A political movement prioritizing the interests of the working and middle classes over globalist and corporate elites.
- [[topics/economic-nationalism|Economic Nationalism]]: Policies aimed at bringing high-value manufacturing back to the U.S. and prioritizing domestic economic health over global trade agreements.
- [[topics/big-tech-influence|Big Tech Influence]]: The concern that tech oligarchs exert undue control over government policy, media, and the financial system.

## Notable Claims

- Big tech companies are using the threat of China to demand government bailouts and control of national labs. Evidence: Bannon cites the push for a $500 billion 'Mercury program' and requests for access to national labs.
- The current political system is rigged by big law firms, investment banks, and lobbyists. Evidence: Bannon notes that most of Congress voted against raising overdraft fees, favoring corporate interests.
- The 2008 financial crisis bailouts created the current environment of corporate power. Evidence: Khanna and Bannon agree that the lack of accountability for Wall Street mismanagement empowered current oligarchs.
