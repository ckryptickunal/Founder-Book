# Should You Follow Your Passion? – Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel

## Summary

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel discuss the common pitfall of 'solution in search of a problem' startups, arguing that founders often rely on extrinsic motivators like prestige and hot technology rather than genuine problem-solving. They suggest that true passion is often a byproduct of success—seeing a business work, generating revenue, and solving real problems—rather than a prerequisite for starting.

## Key Ideas

- Startups should not be built around 'hot' technology without a clear, existing problem to solve.
- Founders often treat startups like school, seeking external validation (investments, peer approval) rather than focusing on actual business utility.
- Passion is often developed through the feedback loop of success, such as seeing revenue grow or users engaging with a product.
- Intrinsic motivation—working on something because you care about the problem or enjoy the process—is more sustainable than extrinsic motivation.
- Founders should intentionally design their environment to include clear, measurable metrics like revenue to create positive feedback loops.
- The 'solution in search of a problem' trap is often fueled by a desire for superficial similarity to successful startups.

## Topics

- [[topics/extrinsic-vs-intrinsic-motivation|Extrinsic vs. Intrinsic Motivation]]: The difference between building a startup for status/approval versus building one for personal fulfillment and solving real problems.
- [[topics/solution-in-search-of-a-problem|Solution in Search of a Problem]]: The common mistake of building technology first and trying to find a market for it later, rather than identifying a market need first.
- [[topics/startup-feedback-loops|Startup Feedback Loops]]: How metrics like revenue and user growth act as psychological motivators that sustain founders through difficult periods.

## Notable Claims

- Success and revenue are the primary drivers of long-term passion in a startup. Evidence: Founders who see their business working and their bank account growing are more likely to stay motivated than those chasing abstract 'passions'.
- The 'follow your passion' advice is often unhelpful for young founders. Evidence: Many founders lack unique passions and end up choosing the same 'hot' startup ideas as their peers, leading to failure.
- Intrinsic motivation is a better predictor of startup survival than extrinsic motivation. Evidence: Founders who are willing to work on 'un-cool' problems without external validation are more likely to survive the initial years of a startup.
