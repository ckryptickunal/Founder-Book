## Summary

Alex Schultz, VP of Growth at Facebook, discusses the fundamentals of startup growth, emphasizing that retention is the most critical metric. He argues that startups should focus on achieving product-market fit before attempting growth hacks, and outlines strategies for scaling, including defining a 'North Star' metric, identifying the 'magic moment' for users, and executing experiments with speed.

## Key Ideas

- Retention is the single most important metric for growth; if a product's retention curve doesn't flatten out, it lacks product-market fit.
- Startups should not have a dedicated 'growth team' early on; the entire company should be the growth team led by the CEO.
- Identify and optimize for the 'magic moment'—the specific action that hooks a user on the product's value.
- Focus on the 'marginal user' rather than optimizing for power users or oneself.
- A 'North Star' metric is essential to align the entire company's efforts toward a single, meaningful goal.
- Move fast and execute: a good plan executed today is better than a perfect plan tomorrow.

## Topics

- [[topics/retention|Retention]]: The percentage of users who continue to use a product over time, serving as the primary indicator of product-market fit.
- [[topics/growth-hacking|Growth Hacking]]: Tactics used to accelerate user acquisition, which the speaker argues should only be applied after achieving strong retention.
- [[topics/north-star-metric|North Star Metric]]: A single key performance indicator that best captures the core value a product delivers to its customers.
- [[topics/magic-moment|Magic Moment]]: The specific user experience that demonstrates the product's value and encourages long-term retention.
- [[topics/internationalization|Internationalization]]: The process of adapting a product for global markets to remove barriers to growth.

## Notable Claims

- Retention is the single most important thing for growth. Evidence: If a retention curve slopes down to the x-axis, the business is not viable regardless of acquisition tactics.
- Startups should not have a dedicated growth team. Evidence: The entire company should be focused on growth, and the CEO should lead the effort to ensure alignment.
- Facebook's growth was driven by word-of-mouth, not viral marketing. Evidence: Facebook lacked native tools to contact non-users, relying instead on the product's inherent value to drive referrals.

## Quotes
