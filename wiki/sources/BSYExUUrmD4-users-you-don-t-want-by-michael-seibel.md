# Users You Don't Want by Michael Seibel

## Summary

Michael Seibel discusses the importance of identifying and removing 'hijack users'—individuals who use a product in ways that provide no long-term value or actively harm the business—to prevent them from negatively influencing a startup's product roadmap.

## Key Ideas

- Startups often feel pressured to accept every user, but this can lead to product dilution.
- Users exist on a spectrum: from those using the product as intended, to those using it in interesting/valuable ways, to those providing no value, and finally to 'hijackers' who cause harm.
- Hijack users should be removed even if they are currently paying, as the long-term negative impact outweighs short-term revenue.
- Founders must maintain a strong opinion about their product's purpose rather than letting users dictate the roadmap.
- Distinguish between users who provide learning opportunities versus those who simply drain resources or damage the brand.

## Topics

- [[topics/product-roadmap-management|Product Roadmap Management]]: The necessity of maintaining a clear product vision and preventing outlier users from steering development in the wrong direction.
- [[topics/user-segmentation|User Segmentation]]: Categorizing users based on their intent, value, and impact on the platform's long-term health.
- [[topics/startup-growth-strategy|Startup Growth Strategy]]: Balancing the desire for user acquisition with the need for high-quality, sustainable growth.

## Notable Claims

- Hijack users can harm a business even if they are paying customers. Evidence: The hypothetical example of users turning Airbnb rentals into illegal drug dens, which brings revenue but destroys the business model and reputation.
- Not all 'hijack' users are bad; some can lead to successful pivots. Evidence: Justin.tv users who started streaming video games, which eventually led to the creation of Twitch.

## Quotes

> By and large these hijackers are users you do not want.
> If you can identify the hijack users you need to kick them out like they're actually going to hurt you more than they're gonna help you.
> At the end of the day you have to have some opinion about your product, you can't just be like I don't know if the market's gonna take it wherever the customers take it.
