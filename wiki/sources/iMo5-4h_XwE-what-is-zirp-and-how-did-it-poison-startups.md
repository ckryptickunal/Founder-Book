# What Is ZIRP And How Did It Poison Startups?

## Summary

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel of Y Combinator discuss the 'ZIRP' (Zero Interest Rate Phenomenon) era, explaining how near-zero interest rates led to an influx of cheap capital into venture capital, causing market distortions, inflated valuations, and unsustainable business models in the startup ecosystem.

## Key Ideas

- ZIRP (Zero Interest Rate Phenomenon) created a surplus of capital that forced investors to seek yield in riskier asset classes like venture capital.
- The influx of 'hot money' led to irrational valuations, such as 350x revenue multiples, and encouraged founders to prioritize spending over innovation.
- Founders who treated money as a substitute for product-market fit or operational efficiency often failed when the market corrected.
- The most successful founders during this period maintained a balance of optimism and realism, using the capital to build enduring businesses rather than just burning cash.
- Building a business that relies on an unsustainable economic environment is a form of 'platform risk' tied to the Federal Reserve's interest rate policies.

## Topics

- [[topics/zirp-zero-interest-rate-phenomenon|ZIRP (Zero Interest Rate Phenomenon)]]: An economic period where central banks set interest rates at or near zero, leading to massive capital liquidity.
- [[topics/venture-capital-distortions|Venture Capital Distortions]]: The negative impact of excess capital on startup discipline, valuation metrics, and long-term sustainability.
- [[topics/founder-psychology|Founder Psychology]]: The tendency of founders to mimic market trends and compete for capital rather than focusing on building enduring businesses.

## Notable Claims

- Giving startups too much money often kills innovation. Evidence: Founders stop trying to solve problems efficiently and instead hire more people to fill gaps, mimicking the bloated behavior of large incumbents.
- Lending-based startups were particularly vulnerable to the end of ZIRP. Evidence: These businesses relied on low cost-of-capital to function; when interest rates rose, their business models became unsustainable.
