# Weird Languages

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-weird-languages`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: August 2021

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that while most programming involves routine library integration, the 'weird' 0.5% of programming—often found in unconventional languages—offers profound insights into problem-solving and cognitive expansion.

## Key Ideas

- Most programming (99.5%) is merely gluing together library calls, which makes popular languages feel equivalent.
- The remaining 0.5% of programming is where the most interesting and innovative work happens.
- Weird languages are often designed to support specific, advanced programming paradigms that popular languages cannot easily replicate.
- Lisp macros serve as a prime example of a technique that enables domain-specific language creation.
- Learning unconventional languages forces programmers to think in new ways by enabling them to express concepts that are difficult or impossible in mainstream languages.

## Notable Claims

- Learning a weird language changes how you think. Evidence: By learning to express concepts that are impossibly inconvenient in mainstream languages, programmers expand their cognitive toolkit.
- The 'weirdness' of good programming languages is intentional. Evidence: It usually indicates the presence of advanced programming techniques that go beyond standard library integration.

## Quotes

> In the process of learning how to say things you couldn't previously say, you'll probably be learning how to think things you couldn't previously think.
> 99.5% of programming consists of gluing together calls to library functions.
