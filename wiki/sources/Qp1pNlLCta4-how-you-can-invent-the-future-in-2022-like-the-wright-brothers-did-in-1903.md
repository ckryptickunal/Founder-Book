## Summary

Garry Tan and guest Jason Crawford analyze the Wright brothers' success in achieving manned flight, contrasting their methodical, self-funded, first-principles approach with the failure of the over-funded, credentialed Samuel Langley. The video serves as a lesson for modern innovators on the importance of iterative testing, calculated risk, and avoiding the 'fake it till you make it' culture.

## Key Ideas

- First-principles thinking is essential for solving problems where existing data or consensus is flawed.
- Progressive iteration—solving small, safe problems before moving to complex ones—is a key to breakthrough innovation.
- Over-funding and reliance on credentials can lead to premature public failure and loss of independence.
- True innovation requires 'shipping' a proven product rather than seeking premature publicity.
- The Wright brothers succeeded by self-funding their research, allowing them to maintain control and avoid the pressure of external expectations.

## Topics

- [[topics/first-principles-thinking|First Principles Thinking]]: Boiling problems down to fundamental truths rather than reasoning by analogy.
- [[topics/progressive-iteration|Progressive Iteration]]: The process of solving smaller, safer sub-problems before tackling the main challenge.
- [[topics/credentialism|Credentialism]]: The tendency to over-rely on established experts or institutions, which can stifle innovation.
- [[topics/self-funding|Self-Funding]]: Maintaining independence in innovation by relying on personal profits rather than external capital.

## Notable Claims

- Samuel Langley's failure was largely due to over-funding and premature public demonstration. Evidence: Langley had 70 times the funding of the Wrights but failed publicly because he was pressured to show results before the technology was ready.
- The Wright brothers' success was due to their methodical, iterative approach to safety. Evidence: They spent years gliding over soft ground to master control before ever adding a motor.
