## Summary

Kirsty Nathoo and Carolynn Levy from Y Combinator provide a foundational guide for startup founders on the essential legal and accounting mechanics required to build a compliant, investable company. They emphasize simplicity, the importance of Delaware incorporation, proper equity allocation, vesting schedules, and the necessity of professional payroll and tax services to avoid common pitfalls.

## Key Ideas

- Form as a Delaware C-Corporation to ensure investor familiarity and legal clarity.
- Equity should be split equally among co-founders to reflect a shared long-term commitment.
- Implement four-year vesting with a one-year cliff for all founders to align incentives.
- Always file an 83(b) election immediately after purchasing stock to avoid significant tax issues.
- Maintain a strict separation between personal and company finances to protect against liability.
- Use standard, vanilla legal documents (e.g., via Clerky) to keep costs low and avoid complexity.
- Founders are employees and must be paid, including payroll taxes, to avoid legal and leverage issues during potential breakups.
- Hire only accredited investors to avoid future complications with unsophisticated stakeholders.

## Topics

- [[topics/incorporation|Incorporation]]: The process of forming a separate legal entity, specifically a Delaware C-Corp, to limit personal liability.
- [[topics/equity-allocation|Equity Allocation]]: The strategy of dividing ownership among founders, emphasizing equal splits to ensure alignment.
- [[topics/vesting|Vesting]]: A mechanism where stock is earned over time, ensuring founders and employees remain committed to the company.
- [[topics/fundraising-mechanics|Fundraising Mechanics]]: The use of convertible notes and SAFEs to raise capital without setting an immediate valuation.
- [[topics/employment-compliance|Employment Compliance]]: The legal requirements for paying founders and employees, including payroll taxes and worker classification.

## Notable Claims

- Founders should avoid disproportionate equity splits. Evidence: Top-performing YC companies rarely have significantly disproportionate equity splits; such splits often indicate underlying founder misalignment.
- Failure to file an 83(b) election can destroy a company's ability to raise money. Evidence: Investors and acquirers will walk away from deals if the founder cannot prove the 83(b) election was filed, as it creates unfixable tax liabilities.
- Founders must be paid a salary. Evidence: Working for free is illegal, and unpaid wages create leverage for fired founders to demand unfair concessions during breakups.
