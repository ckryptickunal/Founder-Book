## Summary

Elizabeth Iorns, a cancer biologist turned entrepreneur, discusses her journey founding Science Exchange, a B2B marketplace for outsourced scientific research. She shares insights on navigating the biotech startup landscape, the importance of reproducibility in science, and the evolving role of academics in entrepreneurship.

## Key Ideas

- Scientific research is highly fragmented, creating significant inefficiencies in finding and contracting specialized lab services.
- Marketplaces in the B2B sector require heavy curation, quality assurance, and standardized project management to succeed.
- The 'Reproducibility Initiative' was a strategic move to improve scientific quality and build brand authority, despite its controversial nature.
- Biotech startups are increasingly viable due to new therapeutic modalities (gene/cell therapy) and better access to capital and infrastructure.
- The biggest competitor for many B2B platforms is the 'status quo'—inefficient, manual internal processes like SharePoint.
- Founders should focus on 'killer experiments' early to validate hypotheses quickly, rather than delaying due to fear of failure.

## Notable Claims

- Most published scientific results are not reproducible. Evidence: Data from the cancer biology reproducibility project and other replication studies.
- B2B marketplaces perform better than traditional procurement because they standardize expectations. Evidence: Science Exchange's Net Promoter Score of 78 compared to an industry average of zero.
- The future of biotech will rely on user-pays models. Evidence: Market success of drugs for debilitating conditions like migraines where patients are highly motivated to pay.
