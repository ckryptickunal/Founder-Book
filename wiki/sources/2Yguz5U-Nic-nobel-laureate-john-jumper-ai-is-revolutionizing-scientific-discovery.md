# Nobel Laureate John Jumper: AI is Revolutionizing Scientific Discovery

## Summary

John Jumper, a Nobel Laureate and researcher at Google DeepMind, discusses the development and impact of AlphaFold, an AI system that predicts protein structures. He emphasizes that scientific breakthroughs in AI require a combination of data, compute, and, most importantly, original research ideas. Jumper highlights how making these tools accessible to the scientific community has accelerated biological discovery, enabled new drug delivery methods, and fundamentally changed how scientists approach experimental biology.

## Key Ideas

- AI for science is an amplifier for experimentalists, not a replacement.
- Research innovation is often more valuable than raw compute or data volume in machine learning.
- The 'social proof' of an AI tool—where scientists verify results against their own unpublished data—is critical for adoption.
- AlphaFold's success demonstrates that foundational models can be applied to narrow, high-impact scientific domains.
- Scientific progress is driven by hypothesis testing, which AI accelerates by providing structural insights that were previously inaccessible.

## Topics

- [[topics/ai-for-science|AI for Science]]: The application of machine learning and computational models to accelerate scientific discovery and solve complex physical or biological problems.
- [[topics/open-science|Open Science]]: The practice of making research data, code, and models publicly available to foster community-wide innovation and trust.

## Notable Claims

- Research innovation (ideas) is worth significantly more than data volume in machine learning. Evidence: AlphaFold 2 trained on 1% of the available data performed as well as the previous state-of-the-art system.
- AlphaFold has accelerated structural biology research by 5-10%. Evidence: Anecdotal evidence from researchers who saved months of experimental work and the rapid adoption of the tool in scientific literature.

## Quotes

> Science is about making hypotheses and testing them, not about the structure of a particular protein.
> When you build the right tool, when you solve the right problem, it matters and it changes the lives of people who are doing things not that you would do but building on top of your work.
> The real cost of compute is the cost of ideas that didn't work.
