## Summary

Harj Taggar, a Y Combinator alum and former partner, discusses his journey as a repeat founder, the evolution of YC, and his insights into technical recruiting through his company, Triplebyte.

## Key Ideas

- Technical recruiting is broken; companies should prioritize coding productivity and practical skills over theoretical knowledge or resume prestige.
- YC serves as a powerful forcing function for early-stage startups, providing structure, focus, and a network that helps with initial customer acquisition.
- The best way to start a company is to build deep domain expertise in a specific area rather than explicitly trying to 'start a company' from scratch.
- Second-time founders face the risk of 'blue sky vanity projects' and should avoid over-optimizing for investor perception rather than real traction.
- Remote technical interviewing using screen sharing and real-world projects is an effective way to remove bias and assess actual engineering capability.

## Notable Claims

- Top-tier boot camp graduates are often on par with or better than top CS graduates in practical web application development. Evidence: Observations from Triplebyte's screening process.
- The companies that talk the loudest during a YC batch do not correlate with future success. Evidence: Personal experience as a YC partner observing many batches.
- Take-home projects can help identify great engineers who perform poorly under the pressure of timed interviews. Evidence: Recruiting data and industry observation.
