# Why Should I Start a Startup? by Michael Seibel

## Summary

Michael Seibel discusses the motivations and realities behind starting a startup, arguing that entrepreneurship is not for everyone and that individuals must be intellectually honest about whether they thrive in unstructured environments or within the systems of large corporations.

## Key Ideas

- Most people are better suited for large companies where they can optimize within existing systems.
- Only a small percentage of people (roughly 1%) are truly wired to thrive in the high-risk, unstructured environment of a startup.
- Founders often stop doing the 'craft' they love (like coding) as they transition into management and administrative roles.
- Advice from peers and large companies is often biased and designed to serve their own interests rather than the individual's career path.
- Self-awareness regarding when you are at your 'peak capacity' is the best indicator for choosing between a startup or a corporate career.

## Topics

- [[topics/entrepreneurial-mindset|Entrepreneurial Mindset]]: The psychological traits required to succeed in a startup, specifically the ability to function without a predictable path.
- [[topics/career-advice-bias|Career Advice Bias]]: The tendency for institutions and peers to provide self-serving advice that steers individuals toward conventional paths.
- [[topics/corporate-vs-startup|Corporate vs. Startup]]: The structural differences between working in a large, optimized system versus building a new, high-risk venture.

## Notable Claims

- Large companies recruit top talent partly to prevent them from starting competing companies. Evidence: Seibel explains that companies like Google have multiple purposes for hiring, including neutralizing potential future competitors.
- Founders often stop doing the work they are actually good at. Evidence: As a company grows, the founder's role shifts from the craft (e.g., coding) to management and administrative tasks.

## Quotes

> As a founder almost by definition the thing that you're good at is the thing that you stop doing.
