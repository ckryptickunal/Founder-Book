# Twitter vs. X: Product Lessons For Startup Founders

## Summary

Y Combinator partners analyze the evolution of Twitter into X, focusing on the dangers of optimizing for single engagement metrics at the expense of user experience, product identity, and long-term retention.

## Key Ideas

- Optimizing for a single metric like dwell time or engagement can lead to 'engagement farms' that provide low-quality content and decrease user happiness.
- Founder-CEOs possess the moral authority to maintain a consistent product vision, which is essential for avoiding aimless feature bloat.
- The shift from chronological feeds to algorithmic feeds often prioritizes 'entropy' content (e.g., viral fights) over user-selected topics.
- Product names are most valuable when they become verbs; changing a brand like Twitter to X risks losing significant cultural equity.
- Successful products often have a clear, articulated purpose, whereas X currently suffers from a lack of clear direction regarding its identity as a social network versus a payment platform.
- First-party information sharing remains Twitter's strongest value proposition during breaking news events.

## Notable Claims

- Optimizing solely for engagement metrics leads to lower user satisfaction and long-term churn. Evidence: The speakers note that while their engagement on X is up, their personal happiness and desire to use the app long-term has decreased.
- Changing a brand name that has become a verb is a significant strategic error. Evidence: The speakers cite the loss of 'tweeting' as a cultural verb as a major negative consequence of the X rebrand.
- Community Notes have been a positive addition to the platform. Evidence: The speakers credit the feature with effectively distinguishing between fake and real news during high-stakes events.
