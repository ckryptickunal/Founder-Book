## Summary

Jacob and Miguel, co-founders of Revikat (YC Summer 2018), discuss how their company simplifies in-app purchases and subscriptions for mobile developers. They recount their journey from personally experiencing the pain of mobile monetization to building a solution that became a standard. The conversation covers Revikat's early days, achieving product-market fit, strategies for selling to developers, and scaling a fully remote company of over 100 people. They emphasize the importance of consistent metrics, transparency, maintaining customer connection, and the unique dynamics of a co-founder relationship. The founders also reflect on evolving their vision from initial self-doubt to aiming for a multi-billion dollar company, and the impact of the Apple vs. Epic lawsuit on the app store ecosystem. They conclude with advice for aspiring founders on resilience, customer focus, and building a company they genuinely want to work at.

## Key Ideas

- Revikat simplifies complex in-app purchases and subscriptions for mobile developers, addressing a significant pain point in monetization.
- The problem of difficult mobile monetization was exacerbated by multiple app stores (Apple, Google) and their lack of incentive to unify or improve payment systems.
- Achieving product-market fit was evidenced by sustained customer loyalty (first 10 customers still active 7 years later), organic growth, server strain, and high customer satisfaction.
- Effective strategies for selling to developers involve answering technical questions, making integration easy, and leveraging the 'long tail' of indie developers for word-of-mouth and future growth.
- Maintaining customer connection in a large, remote company is crucial, achieved through social media engagement, direct meetings, and empowering all employees to interact with customers.
- Consistent metrics, transparent investor updates, and a strong, disciplined company culture are vital for accountability and success, especially in a distributed team.
- Revikat's early adoption of a remote work model, inspired by companies like GitLab, provided significant advantages in talent acquisition and operational efficiency, especially during the COVID-19 pandemic.
- The co-founder relationship, described as a 'marriage without the good parts,' thrives on mutual respect, philosophical alignment, and continuous investment.
- Founder vision can evolve significantly, from initial skepticism about reaching a billion-dollar valuation to actively pursuing a $10 billion vision, driven by overcoming challenges and internal motivation.
- The Apple vs. Epic antitrust case, while significant, had a 'minimal' actual impact on alternative payment options, suggesting the App Store ecosystem is more efficient than often perceived.
- Key advice for aspiring founders includes expecting hard work, building a company you want to work at, caring about the mission, trusting yourself, and focusing on making something people truly want.

## Notable Claims

- In-app purchases and subscriptions were historically difficult and painful for mobile developers. Evidence: Founders' personal experience building subscription systems multiple times, dedicating half of engineering efforts to monetization in previous roles, and describing app stores as 'tough' with multiple systems.
- Apple and Google had little incentive to improve or unify their payment systems due to their monopoly positions. Evidence: The interviewer and founders state that 'it's their sort of monopolies on their store. So they don't really have an incentive to compete to make their payments system better.'
- Revikat's initial customers demonstrated strong product-market fit by remaining loyal for many years. Evidence: Jacob states that 'all of them [the first 10 customers] the ones that are extant' are still using Revikat seven years later.
- Most developers prefer not to engage with traditional sales processes. Evidence: Jacob frequently asserts, 'most developers don't want to talk to sales,' and advises focusing on technical answers and ease of adoption.
- Small, indie developers are an understated but crucial customer segment for DevTools companies. Evidence: They 'will try a lot of stuff,' act as 'champions,' generate 'word of mouth,' and often move to bigger companies, creating a 'ground swell.'
- There is a correlation between founders who consistently send investor updates and their success. Evidence: Jacob observes, 'I think there's a correlation between the person who sends updates and the person who is successful. I think there's like some like underlying correlated trait there.'
- Going remote allowed Revikat to assemble a superior team that would not have been possible otherwise. Evidence: Jacob states, 'there's no way I'd built this team if we were like confined to a city.'
- The actual impact of the Apple vs. Epic lawsuit on alternative payment options has been minimal. Evidence: Jacob reports, 'From everything we've measured, the actual impact has been like slightly positive, somewhat minimal.'
- The App Store ecosystem is not as inefficient as many people believe. Evidence: Jacob's opinion is that 'it's not as far from like market optimal as people think.'
