# Design Masterclass with the best designer I’ve ever met (Raphael Schaad with Cron Calendar)

## Summary

Garry Tan interviews Raphael Schaad, founder of Cron, to explore the meticulous design process behind building a high-performance calendar application. The discussion covers the journey from initial paper sketching to prototyping in code and refining UI elements through obsessive attention to typographic and layout details.

## Key Ideas

- Design should be an iterative process starting with low-fidelity paper sketches to explore ideas without judgment.
- The 'last mile' of design involves sanding down sharp edges and focusing on micro-interactions that improve user experience.
- Prototyping in actual code (HTML/CSS/JS) allows designers to feel the interaction and performance in the medium where the product will live.
- Visual hierarchy and typographic precision (using tabular figures, proper minus signs, and spacing) significantly improve the scanability and professionalism of UI controls.
- Design is not just how something looks, but how it works and how it is built.

## Topics

- [[topics/design-thinking|Design Thinking]]: The practice of deferring judgment during early ideation to generate a high volume of potential solutions.
- [[topics/ui-micro-interactions|UI Micro-interactions]]: The subtle details in software, such as adaptive precision in mouse clicks, that define the 'feel' of an application.
- [[topics/typographic-precision|Typographic Precision]]: The use of specific unicode characters and tabular figures to create clean, scannable data displays.

## Notable Claims

- Sketching on paper is a superior way to start the design process because it is low-commitment and encourages rapid exploration. Evidence: Raphael notes that sketching allows for quick communication with teams and helps generate multiple ideas before committing to digital tools.
- Prototyping in code is necessary to understand the true feel of an interaction. Evidence: Raphael demonstrates how he builds 'rocks and sticks' prototypes in HTML/CSS to test mouse-hover and click-drag behaviors that design tools cannot replicate.
