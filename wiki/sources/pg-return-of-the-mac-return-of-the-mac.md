## Summary

Paul Graham discusses the resurgence of Apple's Macintosh among hardcore hackers and computer scientists following the release of OS X. He argues that hackers are early adopters whose platform preferences predict mainstream technology trends, and that Apple's combination of superior design and a Unix-based operating system makes it the ideal platform for developers.

## Key Ideas

- Hackers are early adopters whose platform choices signal future mainstream technology trends.
- OS X successfully combined high-quality hardware design with a robust Unix-based operating system, attracting technical users back to Apple.
- Software developers tend to build applications for the platforms they personally enjoy using.
- Apple's current market position is unique, with popularity at both the high-end (hackers) and low-end (general consumers), but not the middle.
- The quality and openness of a platform are critical factors in attracting the developer talent necessary to drive hardware sales.

## Notable Claims

- Hackers are early adopters whose platform preferences predict mainstream technology trends. Evidence: Historical examples like Unix, bitmapped displays, the Web, and VoIP all gained popularity in research labs before becoming mainstream.
- Software developers write software for the hardware they personally use. Evidence: The success of VisiCalc on the Apple II was driven by the developers' personal preference for the machine.
