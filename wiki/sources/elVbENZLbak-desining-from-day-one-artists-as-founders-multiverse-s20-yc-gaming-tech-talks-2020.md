## Summary

The founders of Multiverse, a Y Combinator W20 startup building a next-generation tabletop RPG platform, discuss the strategic advantage of including professional artists as co-founders. They argue that artists possess inherent entrepreneurial skills, audience-building capabilities, and the creative intuition necessary for entertainment-focused tech products.

## Key Ideas

- Artists are inherently entrepreneurial, often managing their own businesses through platforms like Patreon, Twitch, and Kickstarter.
- Entertainment-focused tech products require a strong, compelling point of view that is best driven by creative talent.
- Recruiting artists from scrappy, independent backgrounds (social media/freelance) is often more effective for startups than hiring from large AAA studios.
- Art is the primary interface for player experience and a critical tool for early-stage product testing and marketing.
- Bridging the gap between tech and art requires mutual learning: artists must adapt to startup speed and terminology, while tech founders must learn to engage with creative communities.

## Notable Claims

- Artists have the same skill sets as good tech founders. Evidence: Illustrators are already managing businesses, building audiences, and handling revenue streams via platforms like Patreon and Kickstarter.
- High-quality art can be a substitute for a traditional pitch deck. Evidence: Investors were willing to invest in Multiverse based on the quality of their trailer and visual assets.
- AAA studio experience is not always the best indicator of startup success. Evidence: Artists from scrappy, independent backgrounds are more accustomed to the fast-paced, iterative nature of startups.
