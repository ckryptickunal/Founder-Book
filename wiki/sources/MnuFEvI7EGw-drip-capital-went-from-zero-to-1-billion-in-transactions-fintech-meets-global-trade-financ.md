# Drip Capital went from zero to $1 billion in transactions: Fintech meets global trade finance

## Summary

Neil Kothari, co-founder of Drip Capital, discusses the company's journey from a two-person startup to a $1 billion trade finance platform. The company addresses the working capital gap for small and midsize businesses (SMBs) in emerging markets by digitizing and automating cross-border trade finance, providing a win-win for both capital-constrained exporters and investors seeking yield.

## Key Ideas

- Drip Capital provides non-dilutive working capital to SMBs in emerging markets by financing cross-border trade.
- The company pivoted its go-to-market strategy from targeting US-based small businesses to focusing on the India-US trade corridor.
- Success was achieved by digitizing unstructured trade data and building an automated, scalable risk management model.
- The business model creates a win-win: SMBs gain access to essential capital, while investors access high-yield, low-risk assets.
- Entrepreneurial progress is highly incremental; founders must solve one bottleneck at a time rather than mapping everything out on day one.
- The global trade finance market is massive ($18 trillion), yet heavily underserved for SMBs due to incumbent banks' focus on large corporations and lack of innovation.

## Notable Claims

- Drip Capital's risk model has maintained low basis point losses even during the COVID-19 recession. Evidence: Neil Kothari states that performance held up and losses remained in the low basis points during the pandemic.
- Banks in emerging markets like India heavily favor large corporations over SMBs. Evidence: 95% of bank loan books in India go to large companies, while only 5% go to SMBs.
