# You and Your Research, by Richard Hamming

## Summary

In this 1986 lecture, mathematician Richard Hamming explores the traits, habits, and mindsets that distinguish scientists who produce 'first-class' work from those who merely do good work. He argues that greatness is not solely a matter of luck or innate intelligence, but a result of deliberate self-management, courage, emotional commitment, and working on important problems.

## Key Ideas

- Greatness is a result of deliberate choice and self-management, not just luck or raw IQ.
- 'Luck favors the prepared mind': while luck plays a role in the specific discovery, the prepared individual will inevitably find something significant.
- One must work on 'important' problems—those that have a reasonable chance of being solved and would yield significant impact.
- Compound interest applies to knowledge and productivity: working slightly harder and smarter every day leads to exponential results over a career.
- Emotional commitment to a problem is necessary to engage the subconscious mind, which often provides the breakthrough.
- Selling your work is as important as doing it; clear communication and presentation are essential for your work to have an impact.
- Working with the system rather than fighting it is a more efficient use of energy for a scientist.
- Periodically changing fields or research areas prevents stagnation and helps maintain originality.

## Notable Claims

- Great scientists are not necessarily those with the highest IQs, but those with the most drive and commitment. Evidence: Hamming cites colleagues who were more talented than him but less productive due to a lack of deep commitment.
- Working with the door open leads to more significant work than working with the door closed. Evidence: Observation that open-door workers gain more context and clues about what is important in their field.
- The 'Nobel Prize effect' can sterilize scientists. Evidence: Observation that fame and the pressure to work only on 'great' problems can hinder future productivity.
- Knowledge and productivity grow like compound interest. Evidence: The observation that small, consistent increases in effort lead to vastly superior lifetime output.
