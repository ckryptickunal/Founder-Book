# Chase the Hard Problems

## Metadata

## Summary

A brief reflection on the strategic advantage of tackling technically difficult problems in startups, balanced with the critical necessity of maintaining early and frequent customer engagement.

## Key Ideas

- High technical difficulty acts as a competitive moat because it discourages competitors.
- Technical complexity should not be used as a justification to isolate oneself from the market.
- Early customer interaction is vital, even before the product is fully developed.
- Founders should prioritize understanding the customer's daily problems over perfecting the product in isolation.

## Entities

- [[entities/algolia|Algolia]] (company): A search-as-a-service platform.

## Topics

- [[topics/startup-strategy|Startup Strategy]]: The balance between technical innovation and market validation.
- [[topics/customer-development|Customer Development]]: The importance of engaging with users early to understand their pain points.

## Notable Claims

- Technically difficult problems are better startup ideas because they create a high barrier to entry for competitors. Evidence: The speaker notes that if a problem is hard, nobody else will try to solve it, providing a competitive advantage.
- Founders often use technical development as an excuse to avoid talking to customers. Evidence: The speaker admits this happened at their own company, Algolia, and advises against it.

## Quotes

> If something is really hard on the technical side, I mean, I think that's an even better idea, like nobody else is going to try.
> Even if the product is not ready, you can learn so much from learning about their problem, like living their life.
