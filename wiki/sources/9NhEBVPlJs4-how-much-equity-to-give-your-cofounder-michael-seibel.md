## Summary

Michael Seibel of Y Combinator argues that equity splits should be designed to maximize long-term co-founder motivation rather than being treated as a negotiation. He emphasizes using vesting schedules and cliffs as a safety mechanism to allow for generous equity grants that ensure co-founders feel like true owners.

## Key Ideas

- Equity splits are a tool for long-term motivation, not just a negotiation outcome.
- Vesting schedules (typically 4 years) and a 1-year cliff are the primary safeguards against choosing the wrong co-founders.
- If a CEO is hesitant to give a co-founder a generous equity stake, they should reconsider whether that person should be a co-founder at all.
- The goal is to create a sense of ownership that drives co-founders to work hard without constant external motivation from the CEO.

- [[topics/co-founder-dynamics|Co-founder Dynamics]]: The importance of selecting the right partners and maintaining their long-term commitment.

## Notable Claims

- Equal equity splits are a good rule of thumb, though not applicable in every situation. Evidence: Seibel notes that while he has suggested equal splits in the past, CEOs must be considerate of the specific context and motivation of their team.
- A one-year cliff protects the company from bad co-founder decisions. Evidence: If a co-founder leaves or is fired within the first year, they receive no equity, preventing long-term damage to the company's cap table.
