# François Chollet: Why Scaling Alone Isn’t Enough for AGI

- Video ID: `k2ZLQC8P7dc`

## Summary

François Chollet discusses the limitations of current LLM-based AI, advocating for a shift toward symbolic program synthesis and optimal AI foundations. He introduces ARC-AGI V3, a new benchmark designed to measure agentic intelligence through interactive, novel environments, and argues that true AGI requires human-like sample efficiency rather than just massive scale.

## Key Ideas

- Current deep learning and LLM scaling are not sufficient for true AGI due to inefficiency and lack of fluid intelligence.
- Intelligence is defined as skill acquisition efficiency: the ability to learn new tasks with minimal data and compute.
- ARC-AGI V3 shifts focus from passive pattern matching to active, agentic exploration in novel environments.
- Symbolic program synthesis offers a path to more concise, efficient, and generalizable models compared to parametric curve fitting.
- Verifiable reward signals (like code execution) are the current 'unlock' for automating domains, but this is distinct from general intelligence.
- The future of AI will trend toward optimality, moving away from massive, inefficient LLM stacks toward smaller, more fundamental symbolic engines.

## Topics

- [[topics/program-synthesis|Program Synthesis]]: An approach to machine learning that seeks to generate concise symbolic models rather than fitting large parametric curves.
- [[topics/fluid-intelligence|Fluid Intelligence]]: The ability to adapt to novel tasks and environments without prior training, distinct from rote knowledge.
- [[topics/agentic-intelligence|Agentic Intelligence]]: The capacity for an AI to explore, set goals, and plan in interactive environments without explicit instructions.
- [[topics/scaling-laws|Scaling Laws]]: The observation that increasing compute and data leads to performance gains, which Chollet argues is an incomplete path to AGI.

## Notable Claims

- Scaling LLMs alone will not lead to AGI. Evidence: Base models perform poorly on ARC benchmarks despite massive scaling, indicating a lack of fluid intelligence.
- True AGI will be a small codebase. Evidence: Optimal models should be concise; Chollet predicts the final AGI engine will be under 10,000 lines of code.
- Verifiable rewards are the key to current AI success in coding. Evidence: Coding agents succeed because they can verify their own output via unit tests, allowing for iterative self-improvement.
