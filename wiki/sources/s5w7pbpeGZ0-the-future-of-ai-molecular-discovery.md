## Summary

Ankit from YC interviews Ellen Zong, an assistant professor at Princeton specializing in machine learning and structural biology. Ellen discusses her research on molecular machine learning, focusing on protein dynamics derived from cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) and small molecule structure elucidation. She shares her career journey from D. E. Shaw Research and DeepMind (AlphaFold team) to academia, highlighting the evolution of protein structure determination from computationally heavy simulations to experimental cryo-EM. The conversation emphasizes the importance of understanding protein dynamics beyond static structures and the role of machine learning in solving inverse problems from noisy experimental data. Ellen also touches on the collaborative nature of academic research and the vast, unsolved problems in biology despite recent breakthroughs like protein folding.

## Key Ideas

- Molecular machine learning is crucial for scientific discovery in structural biology, particularly for understanding protein dynamics.
- Cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) is an experimental technique that captures ensembles of protein snapshots, allowing for the inference of protein motions and conformations.
- Machine learning addresses 'inverse problems' in cryo-EM by inferring 3D structures and dynamics from incomplete and noisy 2D projection images.
- While the problem of predicting static protein structures from sequences (e.g., AlphaFold) is largely considered 'solved,' the field of protein dynamics and the study of large, complex molecular machines remains largely unexplored.
- Different research environments (industry vs. academia) offer distinct approaches to problem-solving, with academia fostering long-term, collaborative research on complex, 'messier' problems like protein design validation.
- Future advancements in understanding biology and bridging the gap to human health will require new experimental technologies combined with machine learning-enabled models.

## Notable Claims

- Cryo-EM allows for the direct observation of protein dynamics from experimental data, unlike molecular dynamics simulations which are predictive. Evidence: Ellen states that cryo-EM is an 'experimental technique' where 'we actually are just looking at the data,' contrasting it with 'simulations where you're making predictions about the motions.'
- A major shortcoming of molecular dynamics simulations is the need for validation. Evidence: Ellen notes, 'one of the shortcomings of molecular dynamics is that you still need to validate since it's still just this like simulation based approach.'
- The rise of cryo-EM technology paralleled the rise of deep learning around 2012-2013. Evidence: Ellen states, 'the rise of cryo-M actually mirrors the rise of deep learning right so there's this period in like 2012 2013 where suddenly things just started working' due to 'new technologies that enabled better images.'
- Proteins are dynamic molecular machines that constantly move and change conformations to perform their biological functions. Evidence: Ellen explains, 'everything is jiggling everything is moving in order to actually perform functions that lead to life. These are machines that do things.'
- The specific problem of predicting a genetically encoded sequence to a relatively static protein structure has largely been solved. Evidence: Ellen acknowledges, 'maybe this like specific problem of genetically encoded sequence to relatively static protein structure prediction has been solved,' referencing the Nobel Prize and AlphaFold.
- There is still a vast amount unknown in structural biology beyond static protein structure prediction, particularly regarding protein dynamics and large, complex protein assemblies. Evidence: Ellen emphasizes 'so much more beyond that' including 'the dynamics,' 'gigantic' proteins 'composed of tens or like hundreds of different complexes,' and 'lots of structural space that we still don't know even how to describe.'
