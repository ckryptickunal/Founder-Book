## Summary

Adora Cheung explains the critical importance of setting Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) and growth goals for early-stage startups. She emphasizes selecting a single 'primary metric'—ideally revenue—to focus efforts, track health, and iterate quickly, while using secondary metrics to maintain a balanced view of business performance.

## Key Ideas

- Startups should define one primary metric that represents the core value delivered to customers.
- Revenue is the best primary metric because it provides objective validation of product-market fit.
- Active users are a secondary choice, suitable for ad-based models or businesses with strong network effects.
- Growth goals should be set on a weekly basis to provide frequent feedback and manageable targets.
- A healthy growth rate for early-stage startups is typically 5-10% week-over-week.
- Avoid 'vanity metrics' and focus on lagging indicators that prove value has actually been delivered.
- If you are not hitting growth targets, the solution is usually to talk to more users to identify obstacles.

- [[topics/primary-metric|Primary Metric]]: The single most important variable that a startup should bet its success on.
- [[topics/growth-rates|Growth Rates]]: The speed at which a startup's primary metric increases, typically measured week-over-week.
- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The stage where a product satisfies a strong market demand.

## Notable Claims

- Revenue is the best primary metric for almost all startups. Evidence: It provides objective proof that customers value the product enough to exchange money for it.
- Startups should avoid using paid acquisition in the early days. Evidence: Paid growth can mask a lack of organic product-market fit and is considered 'cheating' early growth.
- A 5-10% weekly growth rate is a strong indicator of a potential billion-dollar company. Evidence: Data from successful Y Combinator startups shows this growth range is common for companies that scale.
