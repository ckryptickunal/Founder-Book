# Co-Founder Mistakes That Kill Companies & How To Avoid Them

## Summary

Y Combinator partners Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell discuss the critical importance of choosing the right co-founder and managing that relationship effectively to avoid common startup-killing mistakes.

## Key Ideas

- Prioritize personal compatibility and conflict resolution history over mere skill matching.
- Document agreements, including equity splits and vesting, early to avoid future disputes.
- Pressure-test relationships before starting a company to understand how partners handle stress and disagreements.
- Avoid 50/50 deadlocks by establishing a tie-breaker mechanism or a slight equity imbalance.
- Co-founders should ideally join before the idea is fully formed to foster a sense of shared ownership.
- If a co-founder relationship is irreparably broken, a swift and professional separation is better than prolonged dysfunction.

- [[topics/equity-and-governance|Equity and Governance]]: Structuring ownership and decision-making to prevent deadlocks and legal issues.

## Notable Claims

- Skills are learned on the job, but personality and conflict resolution styles are harder to change. Evidence: Anecdotal experience from YC founders showing that skill gaps are rarely the cause of fatal company breakups.
- A 50/50 equity split can be a liability for decision-making. Evidence: The risk of deadlocks in critical situations can paralyze a company; a tie-breaker mechanism is recommended.
- Adding a co-founder after the idea is formed creates a hierarchy of ownership. Evidence: Founders who join later often feel like it is 'your' company rather than 'our' company, impacting long-term retention.
