# YC Partner Harj Taggar's $500M mistake: A legendary startup advisor shares his lessons

## Summary

Garry Tan and Harj Taggar, former Y Combinator partners, discuss their experiences in venture capital, the lessons learned from early career mistakes, and the unique methodology behind Y Combinator's success in scaling startup growth.

## Key Ideas

- Startups often appear chaotic, fragile, and 'toy-like' even when they are on a trajectory to become massive, world-changing companies.
- The 'zero-sum' mentality in venture capital—that only 20 companies matter per year—is flawed; Y Combinator proves that the number of successful companies is a function of the inputs (funding and support).
- Peer groups and structured accountability, such as YC's group office hours, are essential for founder growth and speed.
- Experience in venture capital is gained through observing many failures and successes; YC partners gain a decade's worth of experience in a few years due to the high volume of startups they advise.
- Experienced advisors provide stability to founders during existential crises, preventing the 'extreme behavior' often seen in inexperienced board members.

## Topics

- [[topics/venture-capital-strategy|Venture Capital Strategy]]: The shift from zero-sum thinking to a model of increasing inputs to generate more successful outcomes.
- [[topics/startup-growth|Startup Growth]]: The realization that internal dysfunction is common in high-growth companies and does not necessarily signal failure.
- [[topics/founder-mentorship|Founder Mentorship]]: The importance of peer groups and experienced advisors in helping founders navigate existential crises.

## Notable Claims

- The number of billion-dollar companies is not fixed; it is a product of the inputs provided to the startup ecosystem. Evidence: Y Combinator's expansion from funding 20 companies to hundreds per batch while maintaining quality.
- It is impossible to replicate the YC experience outside of YC. Evidence: Garry Tan's attempt to replicate the YC model at Initialized Capital failed to produce the same speed and results in founders.
- Great companies often look like toys or non-serious projects in their early stages. Evidence: The early perceptions of Facebook and Google as 'clownish' or 'toys' by industry experts.
