# How AI Coding Agents Will Change Your Job

## Summary

Tom Blomfield and Dave from Y Combinator discuss the transformative impact of AI coding agents on software engineering and knowledge work. They argue that AI is rapidly automating coding tasks, enabling small teams to build complex products, and will eventually reshape the professional landscape for lawyers, doctors, and accountants, while emphasizing that this shift creates unprecedented opportunities for founders.

## Key Ideas

- AI coding agents are shifting software engineering from manual labor to high-level orchestration, similar to the impact of the combine harvester on agriculture.
- The barrier to entry for building software has dropped significantly, allowing non-technical or semi-technical individuals to build complex, production-ready applications.
- Software engineering jobs as they exist today will likely be unrecognizable in 5-10 years, focusing more on managing AI agents than writing code.
- There is a massive, untapped market for custom, on-demand software that will be created by AI to solve specific, ephemeral human problems.
- The most valuable skill for future founders will be the ability to identify and deeply understand human problems, as the technical execution becomes commoditized.
- While AI will drive massive consumer surplus and productivity, the transition period may cause significant societal and economic displacement.

## Notable Claims

- AI coding agents will lead to the disappearance of traditional software engineering roles. Evidence: The rapid improvement of coding agents and the shift toward 'vibe coding' where humans write zero lines of code.
- The demand for software is not infinite, but it is large enough to displace human developers. Evidence: Historical parallels to the combine harvester, where productivity gains led to a massive reduction in the number of human laborers.
- It is currently the best time in history to be a startup founder. Evidence: Lower costs, faster development cycles, and the ability for small teams to build products that previously required large engineering departments.
