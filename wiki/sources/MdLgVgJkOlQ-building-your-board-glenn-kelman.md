## Summary

Glenn Kelman, CEO of Redfin, shares his journey of transforming a 'fraught' relationship with his board into a productive partnership. He emphasizes that founders should prioritize the quality of individual partners over firm brand, treat the board as a strategic resource rather than an adversary, and maintain radical transparency to solve real business problems.

## Key Ideas

- Evaluate board members based on 'soulful connection' and alignment with the company mission rather than just firm prestige.
- Founders should treat the board as a partner for solving real, ugly business problems rather than a 'radio' to be ignored or a judge to be impressed.
- Use written documents instead of slide decks for board meetings to force deeper, more coherent thinking.
- Maintain control as the CEO; the board is an advisor, not the decision-maker, and you are accountable for the company's performance.
- Foster diversity on the board to improve perspective and employee morale.
- Encourage management team participation in board meetings to ensure alignment and transparency.

## Notable Claims

- If you have a board problem, it is usually a symptom of a deeper problem with the CEO or the business. Evidence: Kelman notes that CEOs often complain about boards when their product isn't working or they lack customers.
- Written documents are superior to slide decks for board meetings. Evidence: Documents force slower, more explicit thinking and prevent the 'tap dancing' performance often associated with slides.
- It is almost impossible for a founder to fire a board member. Evidence: The power dynamic is skewed because investors provide the capital, making it easy for them to fire the CEO but difficult for the reverse.
