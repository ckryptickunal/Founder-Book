## Summary

Kirsty Nathoo and Carolynn Levy provide a foundational overview of the legal and accounting mechanics essential for early-stage startups, emphasizing simplicity, standard practices, and the importance of professional organization to avoid costly mistakes.

## Key Ideas

- Form as a Delaware C-Corporation to ensure familiarity for investors and legal clarity.
- Equity should be split relatively equally among co-founders to ensure alignment and avoid red flags.
- Founders must sign formal stock purchase agreements and file 83(b) elections to avoid tax disasters.
- Vesting (typically 4 years with a 1-year cliff) is essential for all founders to ensure long-term commitment.
- Keep personal and company finances strictly separate; never use company funds for personal expenses.
- Use standard, vanilla documents (e.g., via Clerky) for incorporation and fundraising to save time and legal fees.
- Founders are employees and must be paid at least minimum wage to avoid legal liability and tax issues.
- IP assignment agreements are critical; the company must own all work created by founders and employees.

- [[topics/fundraising-mechanics|Fundraising Mechanics]]: The use of convertible notes and SAFEs to raise capital without setting an immediate valuation.

## Notable Claims

- Founders should not form as a Florida LLC. Evidence: Delaware is the standard for startups, and non-standard structures can lead to expensive reincorporation issues.
- Ideas have zero value; execution is what matters. Evidence: Investors fund teams that can execute, not just those with an idea.
- Failing to file an 83(b) election can destroy a deal. Evidence: It has significant tax implications that, if mishandled, can make a company uninvestable.
- Working for free is against the law. Evidence: Founders are employees and must be paid at least minimum wage to comply with labor laws.
