# The Acceleration of Addictiveness

## Summary

Paul Graham explores how technological progress inherently accelerates the addictiveness of everyday life, arguing that as things become more efficient at delivering immediate gratification, individuals must increasingly rely on personal discipline and eccentric habits to maintain a productive, healthy life.

## Key Ideas

- Technological progress is a double-edged sword that simultaneously improves our lives and makes them more addictive.
- Addiction is often the result of concentrating substances or experiences into more potent, immediate forms.
- The gap between 'statistically normal' behavior and 'optimal' behavior is widening as society becomes more addictive.
- Social antibodies (customs) take too long to evolve compared to the rapid pace of technological innovation.
- Individuals must proactively define their own boundaries and 'say no' to new technologies to avoid being controlled by them.

## Topics

- [[topics/technological-acceleration|Technological Acceleration]]: The observation that the rate of technological change is increasing, leading to more potent and addictive products.
- [[topics/social-antibodies|Social Antibodies]]: The cultural norms and customs that eventually develop to protect society from the negative effects of new, addictive technologies.
- [[topics/personal-agency|Personal Agency]]: The necessity of individual choice and discipline in navigating a world designed to capture human attention.

## Notable Claims

- The world is becoming more addictive over time. Evidence: The historical trend of concentrating substances (opium to heroin) and experiences (solitaire to World of Warcraft) into more potent forms.
- If people don't think you're weird, you're likely living badly. Evidence: The divergence between statistically normal behavior and optimal behavior in an increasingly addictive society.
