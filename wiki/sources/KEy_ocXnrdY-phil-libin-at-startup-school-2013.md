## Summary

Phil Libin, co-founder of Evernote, shares his entrepreneurial journey, emphasizing the importance of building products for oneself, cultivating long-term co-founder relationships, and avoiding 'exit-strategy' mindsets in favor of creating 'life's work.'

## Key Ideas

- Cultivate a core team of brilliant, high-energy friends early in your career.
- Avoid being a consultant; build products that provide long-term value.
- Build products for yourself because if you love it, others likely will too.
- Avoid 'clever' legal or corporate structures in the early stages as they can hinder fundraising.
- Don't optimize for an exit; optimize for a 'liquidity strategy' and building something epic.
- Entrepreneurship does not get easier as you scale; it gets harder, but more rewarding.

## Topics

- [[topics/entrepreneurial-strategy|Entrepreneurial Strategy]]: The shift from service-based consulting to product-based business models.
- [[topics/fundraising|Fundraising]]: The challenges of securing capital during economic downturns and the importance of traction.
- [[topics/team-building|Team Building]]: The value of long-term professional relationships and shared history among co-founders.
- [[topics/product-philosophy|Product Philosophy]]: Building for the 'target audience of one' to ensure quality and passion.

## Notable Claims

- Being a consultant is an illusion of control. Evidence: You are ultimately building value for someone else rather than creating lasting assets.
- Unconventional legal structures can kill fundraising. Evidence: Evernote's complex early structure made them 'unfundable' for 18 months.
- The tech world is more of a meritocracy today. Evidence: App stores and social media allow niche products to find their audience globally.

## Quotes

> If you have something that's your life's work you don't need an exit strategy.
