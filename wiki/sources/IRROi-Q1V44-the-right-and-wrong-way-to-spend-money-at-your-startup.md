# The Right (And Wrong) Way To Spend Money At Your Startup

## Metadata

## Summary

The Y Combinator team discusses the critical mindset and strategies for managing startup finances across different growth stages, emphasizing that money should be used to buy time to find product-market fit rather than to force growth prematurely.

## Key Ideas

- Pre-product-market fit, the only goal is to find fit; money cannot buy this, it can only buy time.
- Founders should avoid hiring for sales or marketing until they have achieved product-market fit.
- Startups are not miniature versions of big companies; mimicking big company structures (offices, branding agencies, excessive hiring) is a common, destructive mistake.
- Accountability is key: send monthly investor updates and maintain strict financial discipline.
- Advertising is often a trap for early-stage startups; it masks a lack of fundamental product-market fit and prevents founders from learning how to acquire customers organically.
- Revenue per employee should increase over time as a measure of efficiency.
- Customer support is a competitive advantage for startups and a vital source of product feedback.

## Topics

- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The primary objective for early-stage startups; all financial decisions should prioritize reaching this milestone.
- [[topics/runway-management|Runway Management]]: The practice of preserving capital to ensure the company has enough time to iterate and find success.
- [[topics/hiring-strategy|Hiring Strategy]]: Focus on hiring only essential engineering talent early on; avoid non-essential roles until revenue and demand justify them.
- [[topics/investor-relations|Investor Relations]]: The importance of transparency and accountability through regular updates to stakeholders.

## Notable Claims

- Hiring sales or marketing staff before product-market fit is a mistake. Evidence: Founders are the best at selling their own product initially; hiring others to do it before the product is proven leads to poor results.
- Spending money on ads early is addictive and prevents learning. Evidence: It masks the lack of organic customer acquisition and creates a false sense of growth that is not sustainable.
- Startups should be better at customer support than big companies. Evidence: Speed and responsiveness are the only real edges a small startup has over established competitors.
