# Standing Up For Startups - YC Goes To D.C.

## Summary

Y Combinator leadership discusses their initiative to establish a consistent presence in Washington D.C. to advocate for 'little Tech'—emerging startups and early-stage companies—against the dominance of established Big Tech interests in policy-making.

## Key Ideas

- Little Tech represents the next generation of innovation and requires a seat at the table to ensure fair market access.
- Washington D.C. policy is currently dominated by large, established technology companies, creating a vacuum for startup perspectives.
- Authentic founder stories are the primary antidote to 'AstroTurf' lobbying efforts.
- Key policy priorities include high-skilled immigration reform, a national ban on non-competes, and preventing anti-competitive self-preferencing by major platforms.
- AI regulation must be balanced to avoid stifling entrepreneurship, particularly regarding open-source models.

## Notable Claims

- AstroTurf lobbying is a major issue in Washington that distorts democratic processes. Evidence: The practice of generating fake advocacy and letters to support special interests rather than genuine public sentiment.
- A national ban on non-competes would boost innovation. Evidence: The example of California's de facto ban on non-competes and its correlation with the state's high level of innovation.
- Big Tech dominance in policy-making often ignores the needs of smaller startups. Evidence: The observation that only a handful of large companies have consistent access to policymakers and state dinners.
