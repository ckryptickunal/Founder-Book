# Y Combinator co-founder Jessica Livingston on the beginnings of YC.

## Summary

Jessica Livingston discusses the founding philosophy of Y Combinator in 2005, highlighting the lack of early-stage funding options for founders at the time and the necessity of creating a model that allowed entrepreneurs to test ideas without the pressure of traditional venture capital.

## Key Ideas

- The startup ecosystem in 2005 lacked small-scale, early-stage funding options.
- Traditional venture capital firms required established business plans and tested ideas, creating a barrier for early-stage founders.
- Y Combinator was designed to provide small checks to help founders quit their jobs and validate their business concepts.
- The Boston startup environment in 2005 was significantly different from the Silicon Valley landscape.

## Notable Claims

- In 2005, there were very few angel investors and no real small seed funds available to early-stage founders. Evidence: Livingston notes that the landscape was dominated by VCs requiring millions in investment and business plans, with a notable lack of small-check alternatives.

## Quotes

> We really did start it because there was nothing else like that at the time.
> There needs to be a very early stage company that's just going to write a small check just so a Founder can sort of quit their job pay the rent and test out if their idea even worked.
