# How Generosity Built Tech Giants

## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell of Y Combinator discuss the fundamental principle of building successful tech companies: creating more value for customers than you extract. They argue that early-stage founders often fail by prioritizing growth or fundraising over solving actual customer problems, and they encourage founders to embrace 'consulting'—deeply engaging with early users to understand their economic levers—to build truly valuable, scalable products.

## Key Ideas

- The core of technological progress is providing a tool that allows customers to make more money than the tool costs them.
- Founders often build 'shitty' products because they fear being perceived as consultants or are overly focused on premature scaling.
- Early-stage 'consulting' is actually a powerful way to learn about customer problems and identify generalizable solutions.
- Value should be given upfront, even in initial customer outreach, rather than just asking for time or feedback.
- The history of computing is rooted in the 'free software' movement, where giving away value ultimately created massive economic ecosystems.

## Notable Claims

- If you help your customer make more money, they will likely adopt your product. Evidence: Historical success of Google Ads and Microsoft Office in increasing user productivity and revenue.
- Founders avoid deep customer engagement because they fear being 'consultants' rather than 'product builders'. Evidence: Observation of founder behavior during early-stage customer outreach.
- Giving value upfront in cold outreach increases response rates. Evidence: Example of a startup providing free video audits of onboarding flows to potential customers.
