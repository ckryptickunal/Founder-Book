## Summary

Eric Migicovsky, YC partner and founder of Pebble, discusses the realities of building hardware and 'hard tech' startups. He emphasizes the importance of frugality, rapid iteration, and finding product-market fit through direct customer engagement rather than over-investing in early-stage manufacturing or marketing.

## Key Ideas

- Hardware startups should prioritize finding product-market fit over building complex, expensive prototypes.
- Frugality is a vital survival strategy for hardware companies, especially when managing inventory.
- Crowdfunding should be used either for early validation with a small group or as a marketing channel after a product is proven, not as a substitute for business fundamentals.
- Founders should avoid outsourcing core development to contractors; instead, they should build flexible, multi-disciplinary teams.
- Hard tech companies should focus on 'business model-company fit' and avoid forcing subscription models where they don't naturally belong.
- For hard tech, the MVP is often successful peer-reviewed science or a sub-scale prototype that demonstrates a clear path to commercialization.
- Sales should be pursued early, even before the product is perfect, to validate the problem and build momentum.

- [[topics/hard-tech|Hard Tech]]: Startups involving complex engineering, regulatory constraints, or long development cycles, such as space, biotech, or robotics.
- [[topics/sales-and-customer-discovery|Sales and Customer Discovery]]: The necessity of talking to customers early to ensure the product solves a real, painful problem.

## Notable Claims

- Hardware startups should avoid hiring industrial design or engineering firms early on. Evidence: These firms are expensive and often produce the wrong product before the founder has true product-market fit.
- Crowdfunding is best used for early validation or as a marketing channel, not as a primary funding source for unproven products. Evidence: Most high-production-value campaigns that lack a proven product fail to ship.
- Patents are rarely an effective moat for early-stage startups. Evidence: Enforcing patents is expensive and time-consuming; a great product and network effects are better defenses.
