# Disconnecting Distraction

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the evolving nature of digital distraction, arguing that the Internet has become a sophisticated, addictive force that mimics work. He details his struggle to maintain productivity and proposes a physical separation of work and leisure computing as a strategy to combat procrastination.

## Key Ideas

- Distraction is not static; it evolves and seeks out the user, similar to drug-resistant bacteria.
- The Internet is particularly dangerous because it masks procrastination as productive work.
- Addictive habits should be treated as sentient adversaries that exploit any available path.
- Physical separation of devices—using one computer for work and another for Internet access—creates the necessary friction to reduce mindless browsing.
- Visibility and awareness are the primary tools for breaking the cycle of denial regarding time-wasting habits.

## Notable Claims

- The Internet has become a more potent distraction than television because it is integrated into the workspace. Evidence: The author notes that office workers are now tethered to computers connected to the Internet, making distractions only a click away.
- Physical separation of work and leisure devices is an effective way to curb Internet addiction. Evidence: The author reports that moving to a separate computer for web browsing made him significantly more aware of his usage, limiting it to about an hour a day.
