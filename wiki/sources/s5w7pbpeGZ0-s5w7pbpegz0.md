## Summary

Ankit from YC interviews Ellen Zong, an assistant professor at Princeton, about her research in molecular machine learning, specifically focusing on protein dynamics, cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM), and the intersection of AI with structural biology.

## Key Ideas

- Cryo-EM allows for the imaging of proteins in various conformations, providing insights into their dynamic 'jiggling' motions rather than just static structures.
- Machine learning is essential for solving the inverse problem of reconstructing 3D atomic structures from noisy 2D cryo-EM projection images.
- While static protein structure prediction (e.g., AlphaFold) is largely considered solved, understanding protein dynamics and complex molecular machines remains a significant, under-explored frontier.
- Effective scientific research requires a combination of clear problem definition, reproducibility, and close collaboration between computational researchers and experimentalists.
- Future breakthroughs in biology will likely require a synergy between new experimental data collection technologies and advanced machine learning models.

- [[topics/protein-dynamics|Protein Dynamics]]: The study of the motions and conformational changes of proteins, which are essential for their biological functions.

## Notable Claims

- The rise of cryo-EM technology mirrors the rise of deep learning in terms of timing and impact. Evidence: Both fields saw significant breakthroughs around 2012-2013 that enabled new capabilities in imaging and data analysis.
- Static structure prediction (like AlphaFold) is solved, but the field of protein dynamics is still in its infancy. Evidence: Current models excel at predicting static folds, but we lack a comprehensive understanding of how proteins move and function as molecular machines.
