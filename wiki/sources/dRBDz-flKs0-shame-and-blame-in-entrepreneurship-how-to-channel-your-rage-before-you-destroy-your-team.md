# Shame and blame in entrepreneurship. How to channel your rage before you destroy your team.

## Summary

Garry Tan explores the role of shame in entrepreneurship, using the 2008 MobileMe launch disaster as a case study. He argues that while shame is a natural evolutionary mechanism for social regulation, leaders must learn to channel it into process improvement rather than projecting it onto their teams through blame and fear.

## Key Ideas

- Shame is an evolutionary tool designed to prevent social devaluation and repair relationships.
- Founders often feel deep shame when products fail, which can lead to toxic leadership if mismanaged.
- Steve Jobs' reaction to the MobileMe failure serves as an example of destructive leadership driven by unmanaged shame.
- Top-down communication failures often cause product disasters, not the incompetence of the team.
- Leaders should redirect shame from personal blame toward systemic process changes.
- Great builders use the feeling of shame as a signal to re-align with the goal of 'making something people want'.

## Notable Claims

- Shame is a functional evolutionary mechanism. Evidence: Research by Daniel Sznycer suggests shame prevents social damage and motivates relationship repair.
- The MobileMe failure was a systemic issue, not a team failure. Evidence: Erin Caton noted that engineers voiced concerns that were overruled by leadership.
