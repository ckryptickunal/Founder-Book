# What I Did this Summer

## Summary

Paul Graham reflects on the inaugural Summer Founders Program (SFP) in 2005, confirming the hypothesis that young hackers can successfully build viable companies. The program demonstrated that age and business experience are less critical than motivation, energy, and independence, leading Y Combinator to adopt the batch-based funding model.

## Key Ideas

- Success in startups depends on intelligence and energy rather than age or prior business experience.
- Startups can be launched with minimal capital, allowing for rapid iteration and evolution.
- Founders are driven more effectively by a desire to build something useful than by pure financial speculation.
- Over-engineering early-stage software is a mistake; the initial version should be a vehicle for design experimentation.
- The 'incubator' model is often flawed because it exerts too much control, whereas independence is a key driver of founder success.
- The batch-based funding model is more efficient for investors and provides a necessary social support structure for founders.

## Notable Claims

- Young hackers can successfully start viable companies. Evidence: The 2005 SFP program saw 8 startups, with most showing high potential and securing further funding.
- Competitors are rarely as dangerous as they seem. Evidence: Most competitors self-destruct or fail to execute, making market crowding less of a threat than founders fear.
- Incubators are generally a bad idea. Evidence: They exert too much control and select for the wrong type of founders who lack the necessary independence.
