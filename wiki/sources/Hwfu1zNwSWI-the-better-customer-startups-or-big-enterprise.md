# The Better Customer–Startups or Big Enterprise?

## Metadata

- Video ID: `Hwfu1zNwSWI`
- Channel: Y Combinator
- Published: 2022-08-26T20:40:59Z
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Hwfu1zNwSWI

## Summary

Y Combinator partners Michael, Harj, and Brad discuss the strategic considerations for startups deciding whether to target other startups or large enterprises as their primary customers. They warn against 'cargo culting' successful models without understanding the underlying business mechanics, emphasizing that selling to startups is not inherently easier and often requires a clear path to long-term growth or market expansion.

## Key Ideas

- Selling to startups is a viable strategy only if it fits a specific business model, such as growing with the customer (Stripe) or using startups as a bridge to broader markets (Gusto, AWS).
- Founders often target startups to avoid the perceived difficulty of enterprise sales, but this is a fallacy if the startup does not actually have the problem the product solves.
- Churn is a major risk when selling to startups; if the product does not scale with the company's growth, the customer will eventually leave.
- The 'civilization fallacy' occurs when founders treat their business like a game they can manage from a computer, avoiding the necessary, difficult work of active sales and customer acquisition.
- Bottom-up sales strategies (like AWS) still require building a formal enterprise sales team to close large deals, even if developers are already using the product.

## Topics

- [[topics/sales-strategy|Sales Strategy]]: The process of choosing between startup-focused or enterprise-focused customer acquisition.
- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: Ensuring the product solves a genuine problem for the target demographic rather than just being easy to sell to.
- [[topics/churn-management|Churn Management]]: The challenge of retaining customers as they grow and their needs evolve.
- [[topics/bottom-up-sales|Bottom-up Sales]]: A strategy where individual users/developers adopt a product, eventually leading to enterprise-wide adoption.

## Notable Claims

- Selling to startups is not inherently easier than selling to enterprises. Evidence: Startups can be just as demanding as large companies, and they often lack the budget to pay for the support they require.
- Some product categories are destined to churn as customers grow. Evidence: HR and recruiting tools often fail to scale with a company because the needs of a 5-person team are fundamentally different from a 500-person team.
- Self-serve flows are not a complete sales strategy. Evidence: Founders often rely on 'self-serve + ads + magic' without doing the hard work of active customer acquisition and messaging.
