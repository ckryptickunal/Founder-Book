# Parker Conrad's Billion Dollar Startup Lessons: Do unscalable things, then scale them (part 1)

## Summary

Parker Conrad, founder of Rippling and Zenefits, discusses the evolution of his startup philosophy. He emphasizes the importance of identifying real-world problems, the necessity of doing unscalable things early on, and the critical transition to building scalable, software-first infrastructure to avoid the operational pitfalls he encountered at Zenefits.

## Key Ideas

- Startups should solve genuine, painful problems rather than searching for applications for technology.
- The 'do things that don't scale' approach is essential for early validation, but it must eventually transition to full automation to remain viable.
- Scaling manual processes too quickly creates technical debt and operational complexity that becomes difficult to automate later.
- Gross margins are a critical metric that investors will scrutinize heavily as a company matures.
- Building a foundational system—like a single source of truth for employee data—provides a 10x advantage over fragmented solutions.
- Founders should recognize when growth metrics are bending negatively and address underlying issues rather than simply increasing input (e.g., hiring more sales staff).

## Notable Claims

- Scaling manual processes too early makes it significantly harder to automate them later. Evidence: Conrad notes that at Zenefits, the complexity of manual work became too high to easily replace with software.
- Human-driven processes have recurring error rates, whereas software errors are fixed once and eliminated. Evidence: Conrad contrasts the reliability of Rippling's software-first approach with the manual errors at his previous company.
- Gross margins become the primary focus for investors by the Series C stage. Evidence: Conrad shares that Zenefits faced intense scrutiny on gross margins during their Series C round.

## Quotes

> Do unscalable things, then scale them.
> Once things are being done manually at scale, it's incredibly difficult to go back and automate it.
> You have to go and find something that's happening that nobody's working on.
> Software errors, you make them once, and then you fix it, and they don't happen again. Human errors recur continuously.
