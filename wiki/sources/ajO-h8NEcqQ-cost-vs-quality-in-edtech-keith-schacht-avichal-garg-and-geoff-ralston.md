# Cost vs Quality in Edtech – Keith Schacht, Avichal Garg, and Geoff Ralston

## Summary

In this Y Combinator podcast, Avichal Garg and Keith Schacht discuss the evolution of the EdTech industry since 2011. They analyze the challenges of selling into the US education market, the tension between 'quality' and 'cost' in consumer psychology, and the shift from top-down institutional sales to bottom-up teacher-led adoption. The conversation highlights the potential for innovation in learning science and the long-term optimism for educational outcomes despite systemic inertia.

## Key Ideas

- The US education market often prioritizes cost over quality, creating a barrier for high-quality EdTech products.
- The 'push' model of selling to school districts is being replaced by a 'pull' model where teachers adopt tools directly.
- Learning science is still in its infancy; there is significant opportunity to build businesses based on deeper pedagogical understanding.
- The purpose of education is debated, with a shift toward focusing on student engagement and long-term fulfillment rather than just standardized test scores.
- The EdTech market is fragmented; selling to parents, schools, and higher education requires vastly different strategies.
- Disruption in education is likely to come from the 'fringes'—models that exist outside the traditional, tax-funded school system.

## Notable Claims

- The US education market is fundamentally a cost-driven market rather than a quality-driven one. Evidence: Early adopters saturate quickly, and mainstream consumers prioritize price over quality improvements.
- The 'push' model of selling to school districts is being disrupted by a 'pull' model. Evidence: Teachers are increasingly adopting software directly, forcing IT departments to support tools that gain traction.
- Learning science is currently at a primitive stage of development. Evidence: Our understanding of the brain and pedagogy is compared to the early, pre-scientific understanding of physics.
