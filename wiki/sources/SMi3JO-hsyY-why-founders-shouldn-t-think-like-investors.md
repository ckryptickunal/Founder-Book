# Why Founders Shouldn't Think Like Investors

## Summary

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel from Y Combinator discuss why early-stage startup founders should avoid adopting the 'investor mindset'—characterized by market sizing, competitive analysis, and exit strategy planning—which is better suited for later-stage finance roles than for building a company from zero to one.

## Key Ideas

- Founders often mistakenly apply VC, consulting, or banking frameworks to early-stage startups, which leads to contrived ideas and a lack of genuine passion.
- Early-stage startups require a focus on 'micro' (getting the first customer, building the product) rather than 'macro' (market trends, exit strategies).
- Investor-style analysis is often flawed for startups because it relies on predicting the future of a company that won't reach scale for a decade.
- The 'cure' for this mindset is to unlearn corporate/finance habits, stop consuming VC-focused media, and spend deep, direct time with potential users to understand their specific problems.
- Successful startups often start as 'bad' ideas that don't fit current VC trends, which is why having a unique, non-consensus filter is a competitive advantage.

## Notable Claims

- Founders who think like investors often fail to get their first customer because they are too focused on scaling metrics. Evidence: Founders often present slide decks about market size while having zero customers or product traction.
- Most successful startups start as ideas that investors initially think are bad or off-trend. Evidence: Historical YC success stories often defied the conventional wisdom of the investment community at the time of their founding.
