## Summary

This lecture from Y Combinator's 'How to Start a Startup' series features founders Stanley Tang (DoorDash), Walker Williams (Teespring), and Justin Kan (Justin.tv/Twitch) discussing the importance of doing things that don't scale, finding initial users, and managing public relations for early-stage startups.

## Key Ideas

- Early-stage startups should prioritize manual, non-scalable processes to test hypotheses and gain deep customer insights.
- Founders should personally handle customer support and operations to understand pain points and iterate quickly.
- Product-market fit is achieved through rapid iteration and direct feedback, not by building perfect, scalable systems from day one.
- Press is a tool for specific business goals (e.g., user acquisition, hiring, investor relations) rather than a vanity metric.
- Warm introductions are the most effective way to secure press coverage; cold emailing is generally ineffective.

## Topics

- [[topics/doing-things-that-don-t-scale|Doing Things That Don't Scale]]: The practice of performing manual, labor-intensive tasks to acquire early users and learn about the business before automating.
- [[topics/user-acquisition|User Acquisition]]: Strategies for finding the first 100-1000 users through personal effort, networking, and direct outreach.
- [[topics/public-relations|Public Relations]]: Tactical advice on how to pitch stories to journalists, build relationships, and use press to achieve specific business objectives.
- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The process of iterating on a product based on real user feedback to ensure it solves a genuine market need.

## Notable Claims

- Doing things that don't scale is a competitive advantage for early-stage startups. Evidence: Founders who manually handle operations gain deeper insights into customer needs and dispatch/logistics than those who automate too early.
- Press coverage is not a scalable user acquisition strategy. Evidence: News is inherently temporary; press should be used as a bootstrap for initial traction rather than a long-term growth engine.
- The product you launch with will not be the product that scales. Evidence: Early feedback forces pivots and feature changes that are necessary to reach true market fit.

## Quotes

> At the beginning, none of that is necessary. At the beginning, it's all about testing your idea, trying to get this thing off the ground.
> You want to do these things that don't scale as long as possible. There's not some magical moment... this is one of your biggest advantages as a company.
> Getting press is like a sales funnel. You're going to talk to a lot of people and not all of them are going to convert.
