# Be Good

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that 'being good' and focusing on user needs is not just a moral choice but a highly effective business strategy for startups, acting as a compass for decision-making and a source of resilience.

## Key Ideas

- The motto 'Make something people want' combined with ignoring early profit concerns creates a model similar to a charity, which is often a successful strategy for startups.
- Benevolence acts as an 'elixir of corporate youth,' preventing the laziness and customer-hostility that often plague large, established companies.
- The 'tamagotchi effect': having users who rely on your service provides the morale and motivation to survive difficult periods.
- Being good is a 'stateless' strategy; it simplifies decision-making by removing the need to track ulterior motives or complex lies.
- Benevolence attracts high-quality talent, investors, and community support, creating a competitive advantage.

## Notable Claims

- Startups that behave like nonprofits in their early stages are more likely to succeed. Evidence: Examples like Google and Craigslist, which prioritized user value over immediate revenue.
- Benevolence acts as a 'stateless' algorithm for decision-making. Evidence: By always doing what is best for the user, founders avoid the cognitive load of managing conflicting interests or past lies.
- The 'tamagotchi effect' helps startups survive. Evidence: When users rely on a service, founders feel a sense of responsibility that prevents them from quitting during low points.
