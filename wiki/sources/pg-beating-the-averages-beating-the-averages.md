# Beating the Averages

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-beating-the-averages`

## Summary

Paul Graham recounts the story of his startup, Viaweb, which gained a significant competitive advantage by using Lisp—a powerful, unconventional programming language—to build web-based software. He introduces the 'Blub Paradox' to explain why most programmers remain tethered to less powerful languages, arguing that startups can achieve a 'secret weapon' status by adopting superior technology that competitors fail to understand or value.

## Key Ideas

- Programming languages vary in power, and using the most powerful language provides a significant competitive advantage.
- The 'Blub Paradox' describes how programmers are generally satisfied with their current language because it dictates how they think, making them unable to perceive the benefits of more powerful languages.
- Startups can use unconventional technology as a 'secret weapon' to move faster and build features that competitors cannot easily replicate.
- Programming languages are both technology and habits of mind, which makes them slow to change and resistant to adoption of superior features.
- In a competitive market, doing what the 'average' company does leads to average results, which for a startup often means failure.

## Notable Claims

- Lisp allowed Viaweb to develop features significantly faster than competitors. Evidence: Viaweb could duplicate competitor features within a day or two of their announcement.
- Programming languages are not just tools but habits of mind. Evidence: Programmers rarely switch languages voluntarily and tend to view their current language as 'good enough'.
- Job listings are a reliable indicator of a competitor's technical capability. Evidence: Companies requesting 'IT-focused' skills or common languages like C++/Java are less dangerous than those seeking more advanced or niche language expertise.
