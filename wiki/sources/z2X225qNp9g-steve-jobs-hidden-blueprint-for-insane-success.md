## Summary

Garry Tan analyzes the early life and career of Steve Jobs, identifying three core 'tactical moves'—building what you desperately want, navigating the startup 'maze' through pivots, and integrating diverse interests—that served as the blueprint for his success at Apple and Pixar.

## Key Ideas

- Build products you personally want rather than relying on market research.
- View startups as a maze rather than a straight line; pivot when you hit walls.
- Combine disparate fields (like calligraphy and technology) to create unique competitive advantages.
- Treat failure as feedback rather than a permanent state.
- Integrate work and life to turn personal obsessions into product innovations.

## Notable Claims

- People do not know what they want until you show it to them. Evidence: Jobs and Wozniak built the 'blue box' and the first personal computer without market research, simply because they wanted them.
- The failure of NeXT hardware led to the success of macOS. Evidence: Apple bought NeXT for $400 million in 1997 specifically for its software, which became the foundation of the modern Mac operating system.
- Diverse interests provide a competitive advantage. Evidence: Jobs' random interest in calligraphy allowed him to introduce beautiful typography to the Macintosh, differentiating it from competitors.
