# Things That Don't Scale, The Software Edition – Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel

## Summary

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel discuss the 'do things that don't scale' philosophy applied to software engineering. They share historical anecdotes from companies like Google, Facebook, and Twitch, illustrating how early-stage startups often use 'dirty' hacks, manual processes, and non-scalable architectures to solve immediate problems, only building robust, scalable systems once they have proven product-market fit.

## Key Ideas

- Turn the water on: Don't spend time guessing where pipes leak; launch the product and fix the issues that actually emerge.
- The 90/10 solution: Aim for 90% of the benefit with 10% of the effort to avoid over-engineering.
- Don't let the perfect be the enemy of the good: Use manual or 'dirty' workarounds to deliver value before building automated systems.
- Earn the privilege to scale: Scalable infrastructure is a luxury earned by first building something people actually want.
- Duress drives innovation: Many foundational technologies (like MapReduce) were created only when existing systems broke under pressure.

## Notable Claims

- Gmail's invite system was a result of limited server capacity, not a growth hacking strategy. Evidence: The founders state they had no more hard drive space to add new users.
- Facebook initially scaled by copying and pasting code for each university. Evidence: Dalton Caldwell observed separate database instances and PHP code for individual schools in a data center.
- Google search results were stale for months during a period of infrastructure failure. Evidence: The batch indexing process failed, and the team had to build MapReduce to solve the scaling issue.
