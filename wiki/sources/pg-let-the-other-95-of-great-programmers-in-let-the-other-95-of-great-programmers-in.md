# Let the Other 95% of Great Programmers In

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that the US must liberalize immigration policies for high-skilled programmers to maintain its status as a technology superpower. He contends that exceptional programming talent is rare and globally distributed, meaning 95% of the world's best developers are born outside the US, and that startups are severely constrained by the inability to hire enough top-tier talent.

## Key Ideas

- Exceptional programming ability is an innate aptitude that cannot be created solely through training.
- Because the US represents less than 5% of the world's population, the vast majority of the world's elite talent is born abroad.
- Startups face a genuine talent shortage and are not seeking foreign labor to drive down wages, but to fill critical roles that cannot be filled locally.
- The global market for talent is becoming more liquid, creating a risk that top programmers will cluster in a single global hub outside the US if immigration remains restrictive.
- Attracting top talent creates a network effect where the presence of great programmers attracts more great programmers.

## Notable Claims

- Exceptional programmers are significantly more valuable than ordinary ones, potentially 100x to 1000x more productive. Evidence: Great programmers invent things that ordinary programmers would never conceive, creating value beyond mere speed.
- Technology companies do not hire foreign programmers to drive down salaries. Evidence: Startups pay foreign hires the same as Americans and go through significant legal effort to acquire them, proving the motive is talent acquisition, not cost-cutting.

## Quotes

> The US has less than 5% of the world's population. Which means if the qualities that make someone a great programmer are evenly distributed, 95% of great programmers are born outside the US.
> Exceptional performance implies immigration. A country with only a few percent of the world's population will be exceptional in some field only if there are a lot of immigrants working in it.
> The more of the world's great programmers are here, the more the rest will want to come here.
