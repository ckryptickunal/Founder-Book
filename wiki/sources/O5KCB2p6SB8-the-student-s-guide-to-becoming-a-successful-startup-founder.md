## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell from Y Combinator provide actionable advice for high school students interested in startups, emphasizing long-term skill development, the importance of 'grinding' to level up, and maintaining optimism despite societal cynicism.

## Key Ideas

- Treat skill acquisition like leveling up in a video game; defer immediate gratification to build long-term competence.
- Coding is a foundational 'power' skill for founders, regardless of their specific role.
- Building and releasing small, low-stakes side projects is essential for gaining experience.
- Social skills and the ability to talk to people are learnable, not fixed traits.
- The credential system is a tool to be understood and navigated, not necessarily a source of absolute truth.
- True ambition requires patience and the willingness to play a 'long game' over many years.
- Honesty, both with oneself and stakeholders, is a cornerstone of a high-trust startup environment.

## Notable Claims

- Coders are the most powerful people inside software companies. Evidence: They are highly compensated, hard to replace, and have the easiest path to implementing their own ideas.
- The average startup takes 7 to 10 years to exit or IPO. Evidence: Data from legal firms representing a large portion of tech companies.
- High school is a low-stakes sandbox for social experimentation. Evidence: Most high school social reputations do not follow individuals into their professional lives.
