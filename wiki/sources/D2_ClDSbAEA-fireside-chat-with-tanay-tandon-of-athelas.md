## Summary

Tanay Tandon, CEO and founder of Athelas, discusses the company's evolution from a YC-backed diagnostic hardware startup into a comprehensive digital health platform providing remote patient monitoring and revenue cycle management tools for healthcare systems.

## Key Ideas

- First-principles thinking is essential for entering and disrupting complex, regulated markets like healthcare.
- Startups should prioritize speed and aggressive milestones, even in hardware and biotech, to avoid stagnation.
- The FDA 510(k) process can be navigated by startups through direct submission rather than relying on lengthy, non-binding pre-submission meetings.
- Expanding product lines within existing customer bases (increasing share of patient) is a more effective growth strategy than relying on theoretical TAM.
- Hiring for 'slope' (grit, learning ability, and experience with fast shipping) is more valuable than hiring for 'intercept' (prestige or experience at large, slow-moving corporations).
- The real risk for entrepreneurs is not taking the risk at all; building a company requires a long-term commitment to a problem space.

## Notable Claims

- Pre-submission meetings with the FDA are often a waste of time for startups. Evidence: Tandon argues that feedback is non-binding and the process delays actual 510(k) submission by months.
- Total Addressable Market (TAM) is a fake number. Evidence: Tandon suggests TAM is merely a measure of a founding team's ambition and their ability to unlock new value for customers.
- Hiring from large, established tech companies is often a poor fit for early-stage startups. Evidence: Tandon notes that employees accustomed to 'coasting' or working in environments where products sell themselves lack the necessary grit for a startup.

## Quotes

> There's no market you can't actually build in, and if someone tells you you can't build in it, they probably run it currently and they don't want people to enter it.
> Tam is just a measure of the ambition of the founding team.
> Our belief has always been slopes versus intercepts: find people that will work really hard to learn the underlying thing.
