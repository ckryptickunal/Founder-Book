# Donate Unrestricted

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-donate-unrestricted`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: March 2021

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that restricted donations—where donors dictate how funds are spent—are generally counterproductive, suggesting that donors should trust nonprofits to allocate resources effectively unless the donor is a domain expert or the recipient is an umbrella organization.

## Key Ideas

- Restricted donations are inherently suboptimal because they imply either the nonprofit is incompetent or the donor is misallocating funds.
- Nonprofits are better positioned than donors to understand their own financial needs.
- Donors often restrict funds for personal motives like publicity or vanity rather than impact.
- The power asymmetry between donors and nonprofits prevents nonprofits from candidly requesting unrestricted funds.
- Exceptions to the rule include umbrella organizations like universities and expert donors like the Gates Foundation.

## Notable Claims

- Restricted donations are usually a bad idea. Evidence: If a nonprofit is competent, they know better than the donor where money is needed; if they are incompetent, they shouldn't receive donations at all.
- Restricted donations generate more publicity than unrestricted ones. Evidence: Specific projects like building a school provide a clearer narrative for the donor to claim credit than general operational support.

## Quotes

> If you trust them to spend your money, trust them to decide how.
> If a nonprofit doesn't understand better than its donors where money needs to be spent, then it's incompetent and you shouldn't be donating to it at all.
