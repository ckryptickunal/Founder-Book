# Modeling a Wealth Tax

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-modeling-a-wealth-tax`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: August 2020
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-modeling-a-wealth-tax

## Summary

Paul Graham models the long-term impact of wealth taxes on startup founders, demonstrating how compounding tax rates significantly erode equity over a 60-year period, even when accounting for high tax thresholds.

## Key Ideas

- Wealth taxes compound over time because the same asset is taxed repeatedly throughout the owner's life.
- Small percentage-based wealth taxes result in massive cumulative losses of equity over long time horizons.
- The presence of a high tax threshold provides only marginal relief for successful founders compared to the compounding effect of the tax rate itself.
- Wealth taxes function differently than income taxes, which only apply to annual gains rather than the total accumulated asset base.

## Topics

- [[topics/wealth-taxation|Wealth Taxation]]: The economic policy of taxing an individual's net worth rather than just annual income.
- [[topics/compounding-effects|Compounding Effects]]: The mathematical impact of applying a percentage-based tax to an asset base repeatedly over many years.
- [[topics/startup-equity|Startup Equity]]: The ownership stake founders hold in their companies and how it is affected by fiscal policy.

## Notable Claims

- A 2% wealth tax with a $50 million threshold takes about two-thirds of a successful founder's stock over 60 years. Evidence: Mathematical modeling based on a 60-year horizon and typical startup growth trajectories.
- Wealth taxes are more aggressive than income taxes because they are applied to the same money repeatedly. Evidence: Comparison of annual income tax application versus the cumulative nature of wealth tax on static assets.

## Quotes

> The reason wealth taxes have such dramatic effects is that they're applied over and over to the same money.
> It may at first seem surprising that such apparently small tax rates produce such dramatic effects.
