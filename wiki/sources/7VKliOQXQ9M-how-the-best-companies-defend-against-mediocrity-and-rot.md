# How The Best Companies Defend Against Mediocrity And Rot

## Summary

Eric Ries, author of 'The Lean Startup', discusses his new book 'Incorruptible', which argues that the modern 'best practice' of shareholder primacy is value-destroying and leads to corporate rot. He advocates for mission-controlled companies that use structural governance tools—such as Public Benefit Corporations (PBCs) and perpetual purpose trusts—to protect their core mission from short-term investor pressure and hostile takeovers.

## Key Ideas

- Shareholder primacy is a relatively recent, non-legislated normative consensus that prioritizes short-term financial returns over long-term value creation.
- The 'best practices' of corporate governance, including independent directors and standard Delaware C-Corp structures, often facilitate the removal of founders and the erosion of a company's mission.
- Companies should be structured to protect their mission through 'governance fortresses' like industrial foundations or perpetual purpose trusts.
- True long-term success is achieved by prioritizing human flourishing and mission integrity, which acts as a competitive advantage in talent recruitment and customer trust.
- Founders are often too naive about their legal charters and should proactively adopt structures like PBCs to ensure their company's purpose is legally protected.

## Topics

- [[topics/corporate-governance|Corporate Governance]]: The study of how companies are directed and controlled, specifically the conflict between shareholder primacy and mission-driven structures.
- [[topics/shareholder-primacy|Shareholder Primacy]]: The doctrine that a corporation's primary responsibility is to maximize value for its shareholders.
- [[topics/public-benefit-corporations-pbc|Public Benefit Corporations (PBC)]]: A legal corporate form that allows companies to pursue a mission beyond just profit maximization.
- [[topics/industrial-foundation-structure|Industrial Foundation Structure]]: A governance model where a nonprofit foundation controls a for-profit entity to ensure long-term mission stability.

## Notable Claims

- Shareholder primacy is not a legal requirement but a normative consensus. Evidence: Ries notes that no legislation or referendum ever established shareholder primacy as a legal duty, yet it is treated as such by boards and courts.
- Companies with industrial foundation structures are more likely to survive long-term. Evidence: Ries cites data showing such companies have a 60% probability of reaching 50 years, compared to 10% for standard corporations.
- Independent directors often have conflicts of interest. Evidence: Ries argues they are often recommended by investors and lack financial incentive to protect the mission, making them prone to pro-investor bias.
