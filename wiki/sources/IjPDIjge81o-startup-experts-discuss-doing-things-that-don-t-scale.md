# Startup Experts Discuss Doing Things That Don't Scale

## Summary

This video features Y Combinator partners discussing the enduring relevance of Paul Graham's 2013 essay, 'Do Things That Don't Scale.' The discussion emphasizes that early-stage startups should prioritize learning and customer acquisition over premature optimization or building complex, scalable infrastructure. By performing manual, one-off tasks—such as Airbnb's founders taking professional photos or DoorDash's manual dispatch—founders can validate product-market fit, build deep customer relationships, and avoid wasting time on features nobody wants.

## Key Ideas

- The primary goal of early-stage startups is to learn and validate if people want the product, not to build a perfectly scalable architecture.
- Premature obsession with scalability often leads to building products that no one wants.
- Manual, unscalable work (like consulting or hands-on service) is a powerful tool for understanding customer pain points and refining the product.
- Founders should embrace 'hair-on-fire' problems and solve them manually before automating processes.
- The transition from unscalable to scalable happens once you have validated the product and have the luxury of growth.
- Startups have a unique advantage over big companies because they can move fast, break things, and ignore corporate bureaucracy.

## Topics

- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The process of validating that a product solves a real problem for a specific group of users.
- [[topics/scalability|Scalability]]: The ability of a system or business model to handle increased load or growth without breaking.
- [[topics/mvp-minimum-viable-product|MVP (Minimum Viable Product)]]: The simplest version of a product that allows a team to collect the maximum amount of validated learning.
- [[topics/startup-culture|Startup Culture]]: The mindset of urgency, pragmatism, and customer-centricity required to survive the early stages.

## Notable Claims

- Google is indirectly responsible for warping the minds of founders regarding scalability. Evidence: Founders became obsessed with emulating Google's massive scale, leading to a focus on infrastructure over customer needs.
- Doing things that don't scale is the best way to learn. Evidence: Manual interaction with customers provides deeper insights than automated systems or web analytics.
- Consulting revenue can be a trap for startups. Evidence: While it validates demand, it is not scalable and can distract from building a high-growth software business.

## Quotes
