# How to apply the artistry of Death Stranding to creating as an auteur

## Metadata

- Video ID: `0a3eM7lcVTI`

## Summary

Garry Tan analyzes the creative philosophy behind Hideo Kojima's 'Death Stranding' to extract four core principles of auteurship: having a clear mission, prioritizing personal creative vision over market research, balancing delegation with hands-on involvement, and utilizing showmanship to define a project's identity.

## Key Ideas

- Auteurship requires a clear, mission-driven purpose that transcends standard entertainment.
- True innovation is stifled by market research and quantitative decision-making; creators should prioritize what they find interesting.
- Effective leaders know exactly which tasks to delegate and which critical elements to control personally.
- Collaboration is not about control but about the 'yes, and' approach to incorporating others' ideas.
- Showmanship and theatricality are essential tools for setting the tone and expectations for a creative brand.

- [[topics/creative-leadership|Creative Leadership]]: The balance between delegating tasks and maintaining hands-on control over critical creative decisions.
- [[topics/hikikomori|Hikikomori]]: A Japanese phenomenon of social withdrawal, which serves as a thematic inspiration for the game's focus on connection.

## Notable Claims

- Video games that rely on market research and surveys often result in safer, less original creative output. Evidence: Comparison between studios that follow market trends versus the independent, character-focused approach of Kojima Productions.
- Auteurship requires a specific level of showmanship to establish a project's unique identity. Evidence: The theatrical design of the Kojima Productions studio entrance as a way to set expectations for partners and employees.
