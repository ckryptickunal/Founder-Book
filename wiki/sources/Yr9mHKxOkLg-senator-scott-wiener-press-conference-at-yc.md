## Summary

This transcript announces and advocates for California's SB 1074, also known as the BASE Act, a pro-competition legislation aimed at banning the largest internet platforms (those with over $1 trillion market capitalization and 100 million monthly US users) from self-preferencing their own products and services. Speakers, including Senator Wiener, Y Combinator CEO Garry Tan, and Economic Security California Action VP Terry O'Lee, argue that dominant tech companies like Apple, Google, Amazon, and Meta rig the digital marketplace, stifling innovation, harming startups, and increasing costs for consumers. Several startup founders (Shane Gill of AltStore, Eric Migicovsky of Pebble/Beeper, Marvin von Hagen of Poke, and Peter Krog of Blue) share firsthand accounts of how these gatekeepers blocked their products or forced them to develop workarounds, often contrasting the restrictive US market with more open environments in Europe due to regulations like the Digital Markets Act. The bill seeks to restore fair competition by prohibiting practices such as manipulating search results, using non-public data to compete, imposing unreasonable costs, and denying interoperability, with enforcement mechanisms similar to the Cartwright Act.

## Key Ideas

- Large internet platforms engage in 'self-preferencing,' rigging the digital marketplace against startups and mid-sized companies.
- This anti-competitive behavior leads to higher prices, fewer choices, degraded user experience, fuels misinformation, and stifles innovation.
- Existing federal laws and past litigation have proven inadequate to rein in Big Tech's market dominance and anti-competitive practices.
- SB 1074 (BASE Act) is proposed California legislation to create a level playing field, specifically targeting platforms with over $1 trillion market cap and 100 million US users.
- The bill prohibits specific behaviors including manipulating search results, using non-public data to develop competing products, imposing anti-discount policies, and blocking interoperability.
- Startup founders face significant barriers to market access and user reach due to gatekeeper control, often losing on access rather than merit.
- European regulations, such as the Digital Markets Act (DMA), have successfully forced platforms to allow more competition and interoperability, demonstrating the effectiveness of such laws.
- California is uniquely positioned and obligated to lead on this issue, as the federal government has failed to act decisively.
- The BASE Act is anti-corruption work, not an attack on beneficial vertical integration, and aims to ensure that the best ideas, not just the best-connected ones, win in the market.
- The bill's enforcement mechanisms include private lawsuits and actions by the Attorney General under the Cartwright Act.

## Notable Claims

- The largest online platforms are rigging the internet to undermine and box out their competitors. Evidence: Examples provided include Apple blocking competing app stores, Google manipulating search results, Amazon setting anti-discount policies and using non-public data, and Meta demoting links from competing platforms.
- Anti-competitive practices cause consumer harm with higher prices, undermine competition, degrade user experience, fuel misinformation, and stifle innovation. Evidence: This is presented as a direct consequence of the platforms' behaviors, supported by the 'toll road' analogy and specific examples like Google's travel search results in the EU.
- Existing legal tools and past victories against Big Tech have proven incapable of reining in their anti-competitive behavior. Evidence: Mention of bipartisan legislation stalling in the Senate, Ninth Circuit ruling against Apple, FTC suing Amazon, and Google being found guilty of antitrust law, yet the problem persists.
- Apple would never have allowed emulators on its App Store if AltStore hadn't been ready to launch with Delta on an alternative store in the EU. Evidence: Apple's sudden change in worldwide App Store rules to allow emulators only after AltStore PAL was poised to launch with Delta in the EU, and Delta's subsequent success as a #1 app.
- Apple goes out of its way to make products like Pebble not work fully when connected to an iPhone, unlike its own Apple Watch. Evidence: Pebble users with iPhones cannot respond to notifications by voice, a feature available to Apple Watch users and European Pebble users due to DMA-mandated interoperability.
- Meta banned Poke, an AI assistant, from WhatsApp not because it was doing anything wrong, but because Meta introduced its own competing AI assistants. Evidence: Meta's defense of 'AI strain' was disproven by Poke paying for messages, and regulatory bodies in Italy and the EU ruled in Poke's favor, leading to Meta replacing the ban with prohibitive fees.
- Apple, despite its reputation for accessibility, makes it impossible for innovators like Blue to make their phones universally usable for everyone. Evidence: Blue, a voice assistant, had to develop a physical USB dongle to bypass Apple's restrictions on basic phone actions, which is a critical feature for users with motor or visual impairments.
- The federal government has completely failed to safeguard this crucial part of our economy, leaving small businesses and startups to fend for themselves. Evidence: Legislation addressing self-preferencing stalled in Congress, and antitrust enforcement has slowed to a trickle, with the current administration walking back enforcement.
