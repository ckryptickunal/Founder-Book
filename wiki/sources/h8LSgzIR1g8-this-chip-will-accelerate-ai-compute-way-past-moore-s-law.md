# This chip will accelerate AI compute way past Moore's Law

## Summary

Garry Tan interviews the founders of Extropic, Gom Verun and Trevor McCort, regarding their launch of a new physics-based computing paradigm. The company aims to overcome the limitations of Moore's Law by utilizing the stochastic nature of electrons in superconducting circuits to perform probabilistic machine learning, offering a more energy-efficient and faster alternative to traditional digital AI hardware.

## Key Ideas

- Moore's Law is reaching a physical limit where transistors become unreliable due to thermal fluctuations.
- Digital computers are inherently inefficient at generating the randomness required for probabilistic machine learning.
- Extropic's hardware uses analog stochastic circuits to harness electron movement as a native source of randomness.
- The approach accelerates sampling-based algorithms by orders of magnitude compared to traditional digital processors.
- Energy-based models are proposed as a superior, more efficient future for machine learning compared to current LLM architectures.
- Scaling AI to global levels requires moving beyond current digital hardware bottlenecks to avoid massive energy consumption.

## Topics

- [[topics/stochastic-computing|Stochastic Computing]]: A computing paradigm that uses probabilistic behavior of physical systems to perform calculations.
- [[topics/moore-s-law|Moore's Law]]: The observation that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles about every two years, now reaching physical limits.
- [[topics/probabilistic-machine-learning|Probabilistic Machine Learning]]: A framework for machine learning that incorporates uncertainty and randomness, often used in generative models.

## Notable Claims

- Digital computers are fundamentally inefficient at generating randomness. Evidence: Digital circuits require complex, high-power loops to simulate pseudo-randomness, whereas physical systems can generate it natively.
- Extropic's chips can perform sampling at 80 billion times per second. Evidence: Comparison to traditional Monte Carlo algorithms on digital computers which achieve roughly 100,000 steps per second.
