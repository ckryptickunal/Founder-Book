# Billion dollar startups are better faster cheaper— Lessons from Amazon, Instacart, Uber & Shopify

## Metadata

## Summary

Garry Tan explains that successful startups do not necessarily need to be first-to-market, but they must be 'better, faster, or cheaper' than existing alternatives to survive and scale in competitive markets.

## Key Ideas

- Being first is often a disadvantage; being better, faster, or cheaper is the key to long-term success.
- Technical excellence can drive a 'better' product experience (e.g., Google, Dropbox).
- Targeting underserved niches or specific customer segments can make a product 'better' (e.g., Discord for gamers, Airbnb for conference goers).
- Software can replace manual processes (paper and pencil) to make services significantly 'faster' (e.g., Flexport).
- Asset-light models and removing intermediaries allow startups to offer services 'cheaper' (e.g., Instacart, Shopify, Robinhood).
- Investors often favor novel approaches, but founders must balance this bias with the practical necessity of outperforming incumbents.

## Notable Claims

- Being first is often a disadvantage. Evidence: Examples like Webvan failing before Instacart and Friendster failing before Facebook.
- Speed is a critical factor for consumer retention. Evidence: Facebook's success over the slow, unoptimized Friendster platform.
- Customers are rational and will choose cheaper alternatives. Evidence: Shopify's growth by providing a cheaper alternative to Magento.

## Quotes

> You don't have to be first, but you do need to be different.
> If you can get the same or better product or service, for much less money, customers are smart, they're rational and they will vote with their feet.
> It's easy to be faster than your competitors, when your competitors are literally using paper and pencil.
