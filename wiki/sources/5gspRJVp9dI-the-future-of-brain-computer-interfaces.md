# The Future Of Brain-Computer Interfaces

## Summary

Max Hodak, co-founder of Neuralink and founder of Science, discusses the current state and future of Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI). He highlights Science's recent clinical success in restoring vision using a retinal implant and outlines a vision for BCI as a foundational shift in healthcare, moving from drug-based treatments to direct neural engineering.

## Key Ideas

- BCI is not a single product but a broad category of technologies, similar to pharmaceuticals.
- The brain is a computer that processes information via specific inputs/outputs (cranial and spinal nerves), making it amenable to engineering.
- Neuroplasticity allows the brain to adapt to new inputs, even in adulthood, provided there is feedback.
- Science's retinal implant (Prima) restores form vision by stimulating bipolar cells in the retina, bypassing damaged rods and cones.
- There is a significant unification between AI and neuroscience, as both fields deal with latent space representations.
- Bio-hybrid interfaces, which use cultured neurons to create new connections, represent a potential next frontier for high-bandwidth BCI.
- The 'takeoff era' of biotechnology and BCI is currently underway, moving beyond incremental progress.

## Topics

- [[topics/brain-computer-interfaces|Brain-Computer Interfaces]]: Technologies that connect the brain to external devices to restore function or enhance capabilities.
- [[topics/neuroplasticity|Neuroplasticity]]: The brain's ability to reorganize itself and learn to interpret new signals, even in adulthood.
- [[topics/retinal-prosthetics|Retinal Prosthetics]]: Devices designed to restore vision by bypassing damaged photoreceptors in the eye.
- [[topics/bio-hybrid-interfaces|Bio-hybrid Interfaces]]: An approach to BCI that integrates living, engineered neurons with hardware to create biological connections.
- [[topics/perfusion-technology|Perfusion Technology]]: Systems like ECMO used to support organ function, which Hodak aims to refine for better quality of life.

## Notable Claims

- The first people to live to 1,000 are likely alive today. Evidence: Based on the acceleration of biotech and the shift from incremental progress to a 'takeoff era'.
- The brain is a computer that processes information. Evidence: It receives inputs through cranial/spinal nerves and constructs a world model, similar to information processing in machines.
- Form vision can be restored in blind patients using retinal stimulation. Evidence: Science's clinical trial showed patients could read letters on an eye chart after receiving the Prima implant.
- AI research is currently teaching us more about neuroscience than vice versa. Evidence: The latent space representations in AI models mirror those found in the brain.
