# How Y Combinator Started

## Summary

Paul Graham recounts the founding of Y Combinator in 2005, detailing how he and Jessica Livingston transitioned from the idea of angel investing to creating a standardized, synchronous seed funding model for startups, initially inspired by his own experience with Viaweb.

## Key Ideas

- The VC industry in 2005 was inefficient, often taking too long to make decisions and lacking standardized terms.
- Y Combinator was originally conceived as a way to fund startups synchronously during the summer, a structure that proved highly effective.
- The 'Y Combinator effect' describes the shift in perception from viewing the program as a hobby to recognizing it as a legitimate startup accelerator.
- The decision to move to Silicon Valley was driven by the density of the startup ecosystem and a desire to establish YC as the primary player in the region.
- Successful startup models often start as 'toys' or projects that others initially dismiss.

## Notable Claims

- Y Combinator was initially intended to be a summer program for undergrads. Evidence: Graham notes they chose a summer program because the opportunity cost for students was low and it was a fast way to learn angel investing.
- The YC model of synchronous funding was discovered by accident. Evidence: Graham states they didn't realize the significance of synchronous funding until after the first summer program proved successful.
