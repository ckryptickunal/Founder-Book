## Summary

Carolynn Levy, Jon Levy, and Jason Kwon provide a comprehensive guide for early-stage startup founders on legal mechanics, covering incorporation, equity allocation, vesting, employment, and common pitfalls. They emphasize the importance of forming a Delaware C-Corp, maintaining proper documentation, and avoiding common mistakes like failing to file 83-b elections or neglecting to vest founder stock.

## Key Ideas

- Incorporate as a Delaware C-Corp early to protect against personal liability and facilitate future fundraising.
- Use online platforms like Clerke or Stripe Atlas for efficient formation and post-incorporation document management.
- Founder stock should be subject to four-year vesting to ensure long-term commitment and protect the company during founder departures.
- Always file an 83-b election within 30 days of purchasing stock to avoid significant tax liabilities.
- Treat the startup as a real company from day one: open a bank account, keep separate finances, and document all agreements.
- Avoid hiring employees or consultants without proper agreements (CIIA/PIIA) to ensure the company owns all intellectual property.
- Avoid working with strangers as co-founders; focus on trust and shared commitment.

## Notable Claims

- Most professional investors will not invest in an LLC. Evidence: The speakers note that LLCs are generally not suitable for startups planning to raise venture capital.
- Failing to file an 83-b election within 30 days can lead to massive tax liabilities. Evidence: The speakers explain that without the election, founders are taxed on the value of stock as it vests, which can be extremely expensive if the company grows.
- Employment agreements are often inappropriate for early-stage startups. Evidence: The speakers argue that at-will employment is the default and standard, and formal agreements can complicate termination.
