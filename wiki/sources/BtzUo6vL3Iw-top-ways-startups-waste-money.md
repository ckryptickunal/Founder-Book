# Top Ways Startups Waste Money

## Summary

Y Combinator group partners discuss common pitfalls where early-stage startup founders waste capital and equity, emphasizing that most of these expenditures are premature before achieving product-market fit.

## Key Ideas

- Founders often waste money on hiring, marketing, PR, legal, and advisors before they have product-market fit.
- Hiring expensive talent from large tech companies (FAANG) is often a mistake because they lack the necessary infrastructure and support systems at a startup.
- Contractors are rarely a viable substitute for in-house talent; they often lack 'skin in the game' and alignment with the company's long-term goals.
- Marketing spend on ads is dangerous early on because it can mask a lack of product-market fit and provide a false sense of growth.
- PR agencies are frequently ineffective for early-stage startups; founders should build direct relationships with journalists instead.
- Legal fees should be managed by avoiding unnecessary customization of standard agreements and utilizing payment plans.
- Advisors demanding equity are rarely worth the cost; if someone is truly valuable, they should be encouraged to invest cash instead.
- Founders should 'earn the right' to spend money by first attempting tasks themselves to gain context and understanding.

## Notable Claims

- Hiring expensive talent from large tech companies is usually a mistake for early-stage startups. Evidence: These employees are accustomed to massive support systems and infrastructure that do not exist in a startup environment.
- Paying for PR agencies is a waste of money for early-stage startups. Evidence: Founders can build better, more direct relationships with journalists themselves, and agencies often delegate work to junior staff who do not understand the product.
- Advisors asking for equity are rarely worth it. Evidence: There is no evidence that paid advisors are a primary driver of startup success; valuable advice is often given for free by domain experts or investors.
