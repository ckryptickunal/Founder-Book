# Haters

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the psychology of 'haters' as a byproduct of fame, arguing that they are essentially the inverse of 'fanboys'—obsessive, uncritical, and driven by personal identity rather than objective reality. He suggests that the best way to handle them is to recognize their irrationality and ignore them, rather than treating them as participants in a legitimate dispute.

## Key Ideas

- Haters are the mirror image of fanboys; both groups are obsessive and uncritical.
- Haters are driven by a sense of frustrated talent and the belief that the object of their hatred is a 'fraud'.
- Fame is inherently random, which is why truly successful people rarely become haters.
- Haters rarely produce great work themselves; there is a negative correlation between being a hater and being a creator.
- The most effective strategy for dealing with haters is to ignore them, as they are not interested in rational discourse.

## Notable Claims

- Haters are just fanboys with the sign switched. Evidence: Both groups are obsessive, uncritical, and define their identity through their relationship with the famous person.
- People who do great work never become haters. Evidence: Successful people understand the randomness of fame and are too focused on their own work to obsess over others.
- Haters are driven by a sense of frustrated talent. Evidence: The belief that it is unfair that someone else is famous and they are not.
