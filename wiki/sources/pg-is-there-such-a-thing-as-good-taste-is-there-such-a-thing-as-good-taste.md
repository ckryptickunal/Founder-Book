# Is There Such a Thing as Good Taste?

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-is-there-such-a-thing-as-good-taste`

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that 'good taste' is a real, objective concept by using a reductio ad absurdum argument. He posits that if good taste does not exist, then good art cannot exist, which would render the concept of artistic skill meaningless. He explains that while taste is often influenced by social factors and individual subjectivity, art functions as a tool designed to affect human psychology, making its effectiveness measurable through a partial order of quality.

## Key Ideas

- The denial of good taste necessitates the denial of good art and artistic skill.
- Artistic quality is not purely subjective; it is a measure of how effectively a work operates on the human audience.
- The 'objective' vs 'subjective' distinction is a spectrum based on how much human observers have in common.
- Comparing one's own creative efforts to those of masters (like Bellini or Leonardo) reveals the reality of artistic quality.
- Disagreement about art often stems from extraneous social factors rather than the art itself.
- Good taste is a partial order, not a total one, making it difficult but possible to cultivate.

## Notable Claims

- If there is no such thing as good taste, there is no such thing as good art. Evidence: If taste is purely arbitrary, then no artist can be objectively better than another, making the concept of 'good art' meaningless.
- Art functions similarly to a vaccine. Evidence: Both are designed to produce a specific, measurable effect on a human subject, even if individual responses vary.

## Quotes

> If you want to discard the concept of good taste, you also have to discard the concept of good art.
> The purpose of art is to work on its human audience, and humans have a lot in common.
> While it's not possible to have perfect taste, it is possible to have good taste.
