## Summary

Y Combinator partners share personal anecdotes of disastrous investor meetings to help founders set realistic expectations, maintain professional boundaries, and avoid common pitfalls during the fundraising process.

## Key Ideas

- Fundraising is a high-stakes game that requires preparation, but it should not be the primary barometer for a startup's success or validity.
- Investors are not always experts in your specific business; they can be unprofessional, distracted, or prone to playing status games.
- Founders should avoid 'Frankenstein' pivots where they change their product to match investor trends rather than solving real user problems.
- A great investor is respectful of time, makes decisions quickly, and provides genuine value; avoid those who are net negatives to the business.
- The power dynamic is shifting; founders should treat investor meetings as a two-way street where they also evaluate the investor's fit.
- Professionalism is a two-way street; founders must be organized, prepared, and avoid desperate or disorganized behavior like 'cash crunch' panic pitches.

## Notable Claims

- Investors often do not understand that their process is their product. Evidence: Founders' memories of investors are defined by how they were treated during the pitch process, regardless of the outcome.
- Asking for advice is a common but often ineffective tactic to get money. Evidence: Founders often use it as a 'clever' ruse, but investors see through it, and it can lead to wasted time.
- The best founders will eventually have their pick of the best builder-focused VCs. Evidence: The market is shifting away from status-driven VC games toward value-add partnerships.
