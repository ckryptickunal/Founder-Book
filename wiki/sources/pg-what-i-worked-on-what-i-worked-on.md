# What I Worked On

## Summary

Paul Graham reflects on his career path, detailing his transition from programming and AI research to art, and eventually to co-founding Viaweb and Y Combinator. He explores the patterns of his life, emphasizing the value of working on non-prestigious projects, the importance of independence, and the evolution of his technical and creative pursuits.

## Key Ideas

- The 'low end' of technology often disrupts and eventually replaces the 'high end'.
- Prestige is often a danger sign; working on non-prestigious projects can be a sign of genuine interest and independence.
- Growth rate is the ultimate test of a startup's success.
- Customs and conventions often persist long after the constraints that created them have disappeared.
- The best way to learn about a field is to build things within it.
- Founders should focus on doing things that don't scale initially to gain users and insights.

## Notable Claims

- The low end of software eats the high end. Evidence: Interleaf's high-end document software was eventually displaced by cheaper, more accessible alternatives like Microsoft Word.
- Prestige is a danger sign in choosing what to work on. Evidence: Graham notes that his most successful projects, like Viaweb and YC, were initially viewed as 'lame' or non-prestigious.
- Customs often outlive their utility. Evidence: Venture capital practices and essay publishing norms remained constrained by old-world limitations long after technology removed those constraints.
