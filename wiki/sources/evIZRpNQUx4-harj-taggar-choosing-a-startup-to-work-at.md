## Summary

Harj Taggar, a partner at Y Combinator, provides a framework for evaluating whether to join a startup and how to select the right one, advising candidates to think like investors by prioritizing growth trajectory, founder quality, and user passion over traditional metrics or market size.

## Key Ideas

- Working at a startup requires a different mindset than working at a stable company, often involving lower pay and higher effort.
- Candidates should evaluate startups like investors, as they are investing their time and career energy.
- Growth trajectory is more important than absolute current revenue or user numbers.
- Founder quality should be judged by their relative achievements and 'outlying' traits rather than traditional credentials.
- Market size is difficult to predict; instead, look for a small group of users who are fanatical about the product.
- The best way to accelerate a career is to join a startup that is already succeeding.

## Notable Claims

- If you are happy in a stable job and dislike the idea of working more for less pay, you should not join a startup. Evidence: Citing advice from Paul Buhay regarding the inherent trade-offs of startup life.
- Growth trajectory is a better indicator of success than absolute current metrics. Evidence: A startup with $10k revenue growing from $5k is more promising than a stagnant $1M revenue startup.
- Market size is a poor predictor of startup success in the early stages. Evidence: Many top investors rejected Airbnb because they underestimated the market for home-sharing.
