# The Two Kinds of Moderate

## Metadata

## Summary

Paul Graham distinguishes between two types of political moderates: 'intentional' moderates who deliberately position themselves between extremes, and 'accidental' moderates who arrive at a centrist average by independently evaluating each issue on its own merits.

## Key Ideas

- Intentional moderates acquire their positions in bulk to maintain a middle-ground status, similar to how ideologues acquire their beliefs.
- Accidental moderates form opinions independently, leading to a scattered distribution of views that happens to average out to the center.
- Accidental moderates are more likely to be found among people who work with ideas, as independent thinking is a prerequisite for high-quality intellectual work.
- Being an accidental moderate requires courage because it invites criticism from both political extremes without the support of an orthodox group.
- Ideological conformity is often incompatible with high-level intellectual work when that work intersects with political topics.

## Notable Claims

- Intentional moderates are similar to ideologues because their opinions are acquired in bulk rather than chosen individually. Evidence: If the median opinion shifts, the intentional moderate must shift with it to maintain their status as a moderate.
- Accidental moderates are more likely to be found among people who work with ideas. Evidence: Independent-mindedness is a requirement for doing intellectual work well, whereas it is not required for physical or performance-based tasks.

## Quotes

> The defining quality of an ideologue, whether on the left or the right, is to acquire one's opinions in bulk.
> Accidental moderates, on the other hand, not only choose their own answers, but choose their own questions.
> If the ideas you use in your work intersect with the politics of your time, you have two choices: be an accidental moderate, or be mediocre.
