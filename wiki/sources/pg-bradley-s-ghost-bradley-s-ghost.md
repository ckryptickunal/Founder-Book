## Summary

Paul Graham analyzes the discrepancy between 2004 US presidential election exit polls and actual results, proposing that a modern 'Bradley Effect' caused by social pressure led conservative voters to withhold their true preferences from pollsters.

## Key Ideas

- Exit polls in the 2004 election consistently overestimated support for John Kerry.
- The 'Bradley Effect' describes a phenomenon where voters lie to pollsters to avoid social stigma.
- Pollsters attempt to compensate for non-responses by guessing based on demographics, which fails if there is a correlation between voting choice and willingness to disclose.
- Cultural elites (described as 'NPR values') create an environment where conservative voters feel social pressure to hide their true beliefs.
- Voters who feel their beliefs contradict elite norms are less likely to be honest with strangers or pollsters.

## Notable Claims

- Exit polls in the 2004 election were skewed because conservative voters were less likely to disclose their vote to pollsters. Evidence: The author argues that because elite values favor liberal positions, conservative voters feel social pressure to hide their preferences to avoid being judged.
- Pollsters' methods of adjusting for non-response are flawed when there is a correlation between voting choice and willingness to answer. Evidence: The author notes that pollsters guess the votes of non-responders based on demographics, which fails if the non-responders are not a representative sample of those demographics.
