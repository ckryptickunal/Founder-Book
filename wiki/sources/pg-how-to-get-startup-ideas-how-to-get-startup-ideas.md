# How to Get Startup Ideas

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-how-to-get-startup-ideas`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: November 2012
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-how-to-get-startup-ideas

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that the best startup ideas are not 'thought up' but 'noticed' by founders who live at the leading edge of rapidly changing fields. He advises founders to solve problems they personally experience, build things that are missing, and prioritize depth of user need over broad, shallow market appeal.

## Key Ideas

- Don't try to think of startup ideas; look for problems you have yourself.
- The best startup ideas are 'organic'—they grow out of the founder's own experience.
- Avoid 'made-up' or 'sitcom' startup ideas that sound plausible but lack urgent user demand.
- Choose between building something a large number of people want a little, or a small number of people want a lot; choose the latter.
- Live in the future and build what is missing.
- Turn off filters like 'is this a big company?', 'is this sexy?', and 'is this a schlep?' to see real opportunities.
- Competition is rarely the cause of startup failure; focus on users instead.
- If you need an idea on demand, look for gaps in your own expertise or work as a consultant for a specific user group.

## Topics

- [[topics/organic-startup-ideas|Organic Startup Ideas]]: Ideas that emerge naturally from a founder's personal experience and needs.
- [[topics/schlep-blindness|Schlep Blindness]]: The tendency to avoid startup ideas because they involve tedious, messy, or unglamorous work.
- [[topics/the-well-model|The Well Model]]: The strategy of finding a small group of users who need a solution urgently, rather than a large group who are only mildly interested.

## Notable Claims

- The most common mistake startups make is solving problems no one has. Evidence: Founders often invent a model of the world that doesn't correspond to reality, only realizing it when they try to get users to pay.
- Startups are rarely killed by competitors. Evidence: Success depends far more on the founder's execution and the urgency of user need than on market competition.
- Learning to hack is a sufficient condition for being at the leading edge of a changing field. Evidence: Software is 'eating the world,' and technical skills allow founders to build and test versions of their ideas rapidly.

## Quotes

> Live in the future, then build what's missing.
> The verb you want to be using with respect to startup ideas is not 'think up' but 'notice.'
> If you're at the leading edge of a field that's changing fast, when you have a hunch that something is worth doing, you're more likely to be right.
> Better a good idea with competitors than a bad one without.
