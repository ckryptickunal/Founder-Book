## Summary

Kat Mañalac and Eric Migicovsky from Y Combinator host a Q&A session addressing startup founders' questions regarding product launches, user interviews, finding product-market fit, and navigating early-stage growth challenges.

## Key Ideas

- Avoid over-perfecting launches; treat them as iterative experiments to maximize surface area.
- Separate user interviews into two groups: current users (for feedback) and non-users (for problem discovery).
- Focus on solving a burning problem; if you have to pay people to talk to you, they may not have a real problem.
- Avoid 'whale hunting' (targeting massive enterprise clients) early on; focus on smaller, faster-moving customers.
- Use 'X for Y' analogies only if X is a household name and the comparison clarifies your value proposition.
- Prioritize organic growth and revenue from early customers over seeking immediate investor funding.

- [[topics/product-launching|Product Launching]]: The philosophy of launching early and often rather than waiting for a perfect 1.0 release.
- [[topics/sales-and-marketing|Sales and Marketing]]: Techniques for customer acquisition, including cold outreach and leveraging niche communities.
- [[topics/enterprise-sales|Enterprise Sales]]: The risks of targeting large enterprise clients too early and the importance of finding smaller, faster-deciding customers.

## Notable Claims

- Every launch reaches a new audience. Evidence: Only a tiny fraction of the world knows about a startup at any given time, making repeated launches effective.
- If you have to pay for feedback, the problem isn't painful enough. Evidence: If a problem is truly burning, users will be motivated to discuss it without financial incentives.
- Don't save your big launch for Demo Day. Evidence: Launching early allows for traction and iteration based on real user feedback before the major event.
