## Summary

This lecture covers two critical startup functions: early-stage sales and investor relations. Tyler Bosmeny (Clever) details how founders should handle sales by focusing on prospecting, listening, and persistence. Michael Seibel, Dalton Caldwell, and Qasar Younis (YC Partners) then provide a framework for pitching investors, emphasizing clarity, traction, and the importance of building the company over fundraising.

## Key Ideas

- Founders are the best salespeople in the early stage because of their passion and industry knowledge.
- Sales is a numbers game; focus on the 2.5% of innovators who will buy from an unproven startup.
- The goal of a sales call is to listen 70% of the time and talk 30% of the time.
- Avoid 'maybe' responses; push for a 'yes' or 'no' to save time.
- Avoid the 'free trial' trap; use opt-out clauses in annual agreements instead.
- A pitch should be simple enough for your parents to understand (the 'Mom Test').
- Fundraising is a sprint; schedule all investor meetings in the same week to create deal heat.
- Fundraising is not success; building the company is the only true metric of success.

## Topics

- [[topics/sales-prospecting|Sales Prospecting]]: The process of identifying potential customers using personal networks, conferences, and cold emails.
- [[topics/the-sales-funnel|The Sales Funnel]]: Moving customers from initial contact to closing, emphasizing persistence and follow-up.
- [[topics/investor-pitching|Investor Pitching]]: Structuring a 30-second and 2-minute pitch to clearly explain the business, market size, and traction.
- [[topics/deal-heat|Deal Heat]]: Creating demand for a funding round by scheduling all investor meetings simultaneously.

## Notable Claims

- Founders should not hire salespeople until they have figured out the sales process themselves. Evidence: Founders possess unique passion and industry knowledge that trumps generic sales experience.
- If a customer asks for a specific feature before buying, it is usually a 'pass'. Evidence: Customers often use feature requests as an excuse; it is better to wait for broader demand before building.
- The more you talk during a pitch, the more likely you are to say something the investor dislikes. Evidence: Concise, simple pitches are more effective than long, complex presentations.
