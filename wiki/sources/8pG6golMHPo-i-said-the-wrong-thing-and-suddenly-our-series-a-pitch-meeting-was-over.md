# I said the wrong thing and suddenly our Series A pitch meeting was over

## Summary

Garry Tan recounts a pivotal Series A pitch meeting for his startup, Posterous, where he failed to distinguish between being a 'platform' or a 'network.' He explains why choosing a clear business model is essential for startup success and advises founders to ask clarifying questions when they don't understand an investor's inquiry.

## Key Ideas

- Founders must clearly define their business model as either a platform or a network rather than trying to be both.
- Investors ask questions based on their past experiences and potential failures; understanding the 'why' behind a question is crucial.
- A network relies on scale and user growth to monetize via ads, while a platform charges customers directly for value.
- If you don't understand a question during a pitch, it is better to ask for clarification than to provide a vague or incorrect answer.
- Startups are like elements on a periodic table; builders must identify the next logical step in the market.

## Notable Claims

- Saying 'both' when asked if you are a platform or a network is a fatal mistake for a founder. Evidence: The author's personal experience of losing a Series A investment from Benchmark Capital after giving this answer.
- A platform is a more viable business model when you can charge customers directly. Evidence: The success of companies like Squarespace and Weebly compared to the struggle of network-based models that rely solely on scale.
