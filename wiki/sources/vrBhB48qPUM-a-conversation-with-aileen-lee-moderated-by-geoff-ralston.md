## Summary

Aileen Lee, founder of Cowboy Ventures, discusses her career path, the realities of venture capital, and the essential components of startup fundraising in a conversation moderated by Geoff Ralston at Y Combinator's Startup School.

## Key Ideas

- Venture capital is a game of outliers; investors are looking for companies with the potential to become multi-billion dollar 'unicorns' to return their funds.
- Founders should treat fundraising like a sales process: qualify leads, practice the pitch, and iterate based on feedback.
- Storytelling is a critical skill for founders to recruit talent, win customers, and secure investment.
- Empathy and human connection are vital in the investor-founder relationship, though some successful investors operate with a more mercenary approach.
- Non-traditional founders have more opportunities today than in the past, and diversity in the ecosystem is increasingly recognized as a strength.
- The most successful startups often have co-founders who have pre-existing relationships, and founders are often in their 30s, contrary to the 'college dropout' myth.

## Notable Claims

- Investors are looking for companies that can become worth over a billion dollars to return their funds. Evidence: VC fund economics require a few outsized winners to compensate for the many failures in a portfolio.
- Women-founded companies are often undervalued compared to male-founded companies. Evidence: Studies show bias in investment decisions and a lack of female representation in the 'unicorn' list.
- The best founders often have pre-existing relationships with their co-founders. Evidence: Analysis of successful companies shows they rarely meet on a 'blind date'.
