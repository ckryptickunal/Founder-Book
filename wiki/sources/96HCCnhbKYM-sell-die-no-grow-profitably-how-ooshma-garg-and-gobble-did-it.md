# Sell? Die? No. Grow profitably. How Ooshma Garg and Gobble did it

## Summary

Garry Tan interviews Ooshma Garg, founder of Gobble, about the company's evolution from a failed peer-to-peer marketplace to a highly profitable, direct-to-consumer meal kit service. The discussion highlights the importance of proprietary IP (sauces and recipes), the shift from growth-at-all-costs to sustainable profitability, and the value of cohort retention analysis.

## Key Ideas

- Innovation often happens at the intersection of two industries; for Gobble, it was combining food expertise with tech.
- Proprietary IP (like custom sauces) creates defensibility that generic 'onion-in-a-box' meal kits lack.
- Startups should test if they can be profitable by turning off marketing spend to understand true customer retention.
- The 'growth knob' allows founders to choose between rapid, cash-burning growth and sustainable, profitable growth.
- Founders should seek out 'real talk' communities to discuss the 'unknown unknowns' rather than maintaining a 'killing it' facade.

## Notable Claims

- Meal kits that rely on generic ingredients lack defensibility. Evidence: Competitors like Blue Apron faced challenges because customers could easily replicate recipes by buying ingredients at a grocery store.
- Turning off marketing spend is a critical test for startup health. Evidence: Gobble discovered their true retention rates and profitability by cutting marketing spend when fundraising became difficult.
