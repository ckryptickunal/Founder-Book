# YC SUS: Michael Seibel and Eric Migicovsky discuss How to Launch an MVP

## Summary

Michael Seibel and Eric Migicovsky from Y Combinator provide tactical advice to early-stage founders on building, launching, and iterating on Minimum Viable Products (MVPs), emphasizing the importance of solving real pain points and avoiding common pitfalls like over-engineering or premature scaling.

## Key Ideas

- An MVP should be the smallest possible version of a product that solves a real problem for a specific group of users.
- If you cannot find users willing to use a 'scrappy' MVP, it is a sign that the problem is not severe enough or that competitors are already doing a great job.
- Founders should charge for their product from day one if there are real costs involved, as it validates demand and filters for serious users.
- Avoid 'build it and they will come' fallacies; focus on existing workflows and pain points rather than just digitizing paper processes.
- When building on top of existing platforms (like YouTube), recognize the risk of platform dependency and consider it a potential threat to long-term viability.
- Do things that don't scale in the early days to maintain high-bandwidth feedback loops with your first customers.
- Time-bound your experiments and use objective metrics to decide whether to pivot or continue.

## Notable Claims

- If you cannot find 10-100 users who love your product despite it being 'bad', you likely do not have a viable business. Evidence: Founders' experience with early-stage startups and the reality of market competition.
- Charging more than competitors can be a valid strategy to identify the most desperate users. Evidence: If users are willing to pay a premium for a new, unproven solution, it proves the pain point is severe.
- Patents are generally not a critical step for early-stage startups. Evidence: Focusing on product-market fit and revenue is more important than legal protection for unproven ideas.

## Quotes

> The goal of any good MVP is to test your hypothesis: is what you're making important enough for people to care about?
> If you're not providing any value to your users, go figure out who is in so much pain around this problem that they would use your MVP.
> Do things that don't scale.
> If you're starting a startup... the number one thing that you need to do is find something that people want and sell it to them and then iterate and grow.
