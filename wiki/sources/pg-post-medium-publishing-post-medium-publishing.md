# Post-Medium Publishing

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-post-medium-publishing`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: September 2009
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-post-medium-publishing

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that traditional publishers were never in the business of selling 'content,' but rather in the business of marking up physical media. As digital technology renders the physical medium obsolete, publishers are struggling because they lack a viable model for selling information alone, suggesting that the future of media lies in indirect monetization or creating physical experiences that cannot be replicated digitally.

## Key Ideas

- Publishers historically sold physical media (paper, discs) rather than the information itself.
- Consumers have rarely shown a willingness to pay for 'content' unless it provides a direct financial return (e.g., stock tips).
- Digital distribution makes the 'tollbooth' model (like iTunes) difficult to sustain as consumers find ways around painful costs.
- Software is a distinct category from content because it is functional and often legally mandated for business use.
- The future of publishing involves either giving content away to monetize indirectly (ads, concerts, merchandise) or creating high-quality physical objects.
- New media winners will use technology to provide new value, while losers will focus on preserving legacy revenue streams.

## Notable Claims

- Publishers were never in the content business; they were in the business of marking up paper. Evidence: The price of books and magazines has historically been tied to the format and page count rather than the quality of the journalism or writing.
- iTunes is a tollbooth, not a store. Evidence: It makes money by controlling the default path to the device and charging small, ignorable amounts rather than selling content at its intrinsic value.
- Software is fundamentally different from content. Evidence: Software is often a business necessity, and users face legal or functional consequences for not using it, unlike articles or songs.
