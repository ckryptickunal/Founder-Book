# Why It's Safe for Founders to Be Nice

## Metadata

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that startup founders do not need to be ruthless or 'rapacious' to succeed. He demonstrates mathematically that focusing on high growth rates through product excellence is far more impactful than maximizing short-term revenue extraction, allowing founders to remain 'nice' without sacrificing long-term success.

## Key Ideas

- Startup success is driven by exponential growth, not by aggressive extraction of money from customers.
- The 'rapacious' businessman stereotype is largely inapplicable to successful startup founders.
- A founder's 'niceness' is a safe trait because the compounding effect of a high growth rate far outweighs the minor revenue gains from aggressive pricing.
- Growth rate is the most important metric to optimize; it compensates for being less aggressive in monetization.
- The most successful founders are driven by the desire to build something great, not just by money.

## Notable Claims

- Being 'nice' does not hinder startup success. Evidence: Mathematical modeling shows that even if a founder extracts half the revenue of a 'rapacious' competitor, a slightly higher growth rate allows them to surpass the competitor in total revenue within a few years.
- Growth rate is the primary driver of startup success. Evidence: Compounding growth creates massive differences in long-term outcomes compared to constant multiples of revenue extraction.

## Quotes

> The reason startup founders can safely be nice is that making great things is compounded, and rapacity isn't.
> If you're going to optimize a number, the one to choose is your growth rate.
> What drives the most successful founders is the same thing that drives most people who make things: the company is their project.
