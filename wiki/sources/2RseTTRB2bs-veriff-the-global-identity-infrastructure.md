## Summary

Kyle Kotkas, CEO and founder of Verif, discusses the journey of building his online identity verification platform. Starting straight out of high school in 2015, Kyle was inspired by his own experience of easily circumventing ID checks. Verif evolved from a compliance tool for companies like Wise and Uber into a robust fraud prevention infrastructure, leveraging video and thousands of data points to ensure accurate identity verification. He recounts overcoming significant challenges, including a 'messed up' cap table (selling 95% of the company for €60k) and a lack of co-founders, which he rectified in two intense weeks after a Y Combinator rejection, taking a €1 million personal loan. Verif experienced rapid growth during the crypto boom and the COVID-19 pandemic, as online services surged. Kyle emphasizes the company's independence and its mission to build a 'trust infrastructure' that goes beyond government IDs, incorporating a user's historical trust across platforms, especially crucial in the age of AI and deepfakes. He advises aspiring founders to prioritize conviction in solving a problem over merely deploying technology.

## Key Ideas

- Verif's origin stemmed from Kyle Kotkas's personal experience of easily bypassing existing identity verification systems.
- Identity verification has evolved from a compliance-driven necessity to a critical fraud prevention infrastructure.
- Verif's technology utilizes video and thousands of data points for superior accuracy and scalability, contrasting with older, easily spoofed picture-based methods.
- Overcoming significant early challenges, including a highly diluted cap table and the need to onboard a co-founder, was crucial for Verif's survival and YC acceptance.
- Major growth drivers for Verif included the crypto boom (due to increased fraud in promotions) and the COVID-19 pandemic (as services rapidly shifted online).
- The concept of a 'trust infrastructure' is paramount, extending beyond government-issued IDs to include a user's historical behavior and trustworthiness across various platforms.
- Combating AI and deepfakes in identity verification requires a multi-layered approach, analyzing user behavior, device data, and real-time camera input.
- Founders should focus on having a strong conviction in the problem they are solving, rather than just applying popular technologies.
- The identity verification industry is in an 'arms race' against sophisticated, well-funded fraudsters.

## Notable Claims

- Verif is one of the leading ID verification companies in the world, with over 600 employees, growing almost 100% year-over-year, and is profitable. Evidence: Stated by the interviewer at the beginning of the transcript, and reinforced by Kyle's later discussion of growth.
- Identity verification in 2015 was inaccurate, didn't scale, killed conversion, and had a very low cost for fraud. Evidence: Kyle's analysis for TransferWise and his personal experience hacking PayPal's system as a kid.
- Complying with regulations alone does not prevent fraud; deeper measures are needed. Evidence: Kyle states, 'because complying with regulations don't prevent fraud and eventually you need to go so much more in depth'.
- Online identity verification by Verif can be more accurate than physical face-to-face authentication. Evidence: Traditional banks in the Nordics were early customers who needed this proven before adopting Verif.
- The US Social Security Number (SSN) is a poor form of identity verification, treated as a 'public secret'. Evidence: Both Kyle and the interviewer agree on this point, highlighting its vulnerability.
- Deepfakes have been around for 10 years but are now accessible to everyone at a very low cost. Evidence: Kyle explains, 'over the past 10 years it was at the beginning more of a Hollywood production. Now it's in the hands with very low cost for everybody'.
- Single-factor identity verification methods (e.g., relying only on media or biometrics) are insufficient against modern fraud and deepfakes. Evidence: Kyle states that if a company relies on a 'single credit verification method... then the cost of doing fraud pretending to be somebody else is now lower than ever before'.
- Fraudsters are highly funded and have access to advanced AI tools, creating an 'arms race' in identity verification. Evidence: Kyle mentions 'those fraudsters who are unfortunately pretty highly funded who can actually outsmart' and agrees with the 'arms race' analogy.
- A new identity system should incorporate a user's trust history across various platforms, not just government-issued IDs. Evidence: Kyle's vision for 'trust infrastructure' where 'you've been a good actor at Uber, Airbnb, PayPal, Stripe, Google, you name it and everybody benefits'.
