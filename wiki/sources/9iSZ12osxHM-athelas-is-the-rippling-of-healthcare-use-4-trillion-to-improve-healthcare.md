# Athelas is the Rippling of Healthcare: Use $4 Trillion to improve healthcare

## Summary

Athelas, founded by T Tandon and Deepa, has evolved from a blood diagnostic hardware company into a comprehensive healthcare technology platform. By automating administrative 'work tax' through software and AI, the company aims to modernize the inefficient $4 trillion US healthcare industry, positioning itself as the 'Rippling for healthcare' by building an integrated operating system for medical providers.

## Key Ideas

- Healthcare is plagued by 'work tax'—excessive administrative, billing, and credentialing friction that consumes 15-30% of industry spending.
- The most effective way to improve healthcare is by building tools for providers, who act as the primary conduit for care and financial transactions.
- Athelas transitioned from a niche hardware diagnostic company to a broad software platform by leveraging the operational insights gained from their initial product.
- Large language models and AI can significantly reduce administrative burden by automating clinical documentation, coding, and claims processing.
- Healthcare, real estate, and education are identified as 'black holes' for wealth that require technological intervention to stop inflationary growth.
- The goal is to reduce healthcare spending by $2 trillion over 20 years while simultaneously improving patient outcomes.

## Notable Claims

- Administrative spending accounts for 15% to 30% of all healthcare costs. Evidence: Cited as a consensus among researchers regarding the inefficiency of the current US healthcare system.
- Athelas Scribe eliminates hours of daily work for clinicians. Evidence: The tool automatically transcribes patient conversations and generates clinical notes and billing codes.
- Reducing healthcare spending by $2 trillion over 20 years would act as a massive non-inflationary stimulus. Evidence: Based on the premise that current healthcare spending is highly inefficient and redirecting those funds would improve the standard of living.
