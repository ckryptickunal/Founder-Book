# When To Do What You Love

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the nuanced decision of whether to 'follow your passion' in one's career. He argues that while working for money often requires sacrificing personal interest, pursuing one's genuine interests is a necessary condition for doing 'great work' and, paradoxically, the most effective strategy for building massive wealth through startups.

## Key Ideas

- The decision to follow one's passion is not binary; it depends on one's goals (e.g., moderate wealth vs. extreme wealth vs. great work).
- There is a 'midwit peak' for making money: working for interest is viable for low-income needs, difficult for moderate wealth, but highly effective for extreme wealth via startups.
- Startup ideas are often discovered as side projects when founders work on what they find interesting, as these ideas are often outliers that money-focused individuals overlook.
- Indecision about career paths is usually a result of ignorance regarding one's own happiness, the nature of the work, or one's own capabilities.
- The concept of 'staying upwind' involves choosing paths that preserve future options when one is uncertain.
- Working on what you love surrounds you with like-minded, passionate people, whereas working solely for money surrounds you with others driven by the same extrinsic motivation.

## Notable Claims

- Working on what you are most interested in is a necessary condition for doing great work. Evidence: The author notes that great work requires an 'ambitious curiosity' that cannot be manufactured through external incentives.
- The best startup ideas are often discovered by accident while working on personal interests. Evidence: Historical examples like Apple, Google, and Facebook began as projects done for fun rather than as conscious attempts to make money.
- Economic inequality is a natural result of different people having different interests. Evidence: Some interests (like enterprise software) have higher market value than others (like studio pottery).

## Quotes

> The root of great work is a sort of ambitious curiosity, and you can't manufacture that.
> The less sure you are about what to do, the more important it is to choose options that give you more options in the future. I call this 'staying upwind.'
> If you choose a kind of work mainly for how well it pays, you'll be surrounded by other people who chose it for the same reason, and that will make it even more soul-sucking than it seems from the outside.
