# How To Deal With Setbacks

## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell of Y Combinator discuss the inevitability of setbacks in the startup journey, emphasizing that founders must learn to 'take a punch' rather than expecting a smooth, movie-like path to success. They provide tactical advice on managing reactions to fundraising failures, co-founder disputes, and negative public feedback.

## Key Ideas

- Setbacks are an inherent, unavoidable part of the startup game, not an anomaly.
- Founders often hold unrealistic expectations shaped by movies and media, which portray success as a linear, dramatic arc.
- The 'meta-skill' of being a founder is learning how to manage one's own reaction to bad news.
- A founder's reaction to adversity sets the tone for the entire organization.
- Performing a 'worst-case analysis' helps ground founders in reality and reduces irrational fear.
- Resilience and the ability to remain calm during crises are valuable 'superpowers' that persist even if a specific startup fails.

## Topics

- [[topics/fundraising|Fundraising]]: The process of raising capital is often fraught with rejection and 'bloody noses,' which are normal parts of the experience.
- [[topics/co-founder-dynamics|Co-founder Dynamics]]: Friction is inevitable; a strong pre-existing relationship is necessary to survive the stress of a startup.
- [[topics/the-magical-deal-fallacy|The 'Magical Deal' Fallacy]]: The dangerous belief that a single contract or event will solve all company problems and lead to an easy exit.
- [[topics/public-perception-vs-reality|Public Perception vs. Reality]]: The contrast between the 'movie version' of startup success and the boring, repetitive, and difficult reality of building a company.

## Notable Claims

- Most Series A funding rounds fail, but because failures are not publicized, founders feel isolated in their struggles. Evidence: The speakers note that no one issues press releases for failed funding rounds, creating a survivor bias in public perception.
- You can get better at dealing with setbacks through practice. Evidence: The speakers argue that emotional regulation is a skill that can be developed, similar to any other professional competency.
