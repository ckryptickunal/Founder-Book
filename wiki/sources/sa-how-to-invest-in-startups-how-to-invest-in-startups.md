# How To Invest In Startups

## Summary

Sam Altman outlines the core principles of successful startup investing, emphasizing that in a competitive market, investors must excel at sourcing opportunities, making high-conviction decisions, and winning over top-tier founders through genuine support and peer-level relationships.

## Key Ideas

- Investors must focus on three pillars: access, decision-making, and closing.
- The best way to gain access is by working hard to help founders and other investors, rather than just networking.
- Founder quality is the primary indicator of success; look for rapid improvement, high conviction, and speed of action.
- Investors should prioritize markets with high growth potential and rapid technological change rather than current market size.
- The power law dictates that returns come from a few massive successes, so focus on companies with $10B+ potential.
- Treat founders as peers; they have a sixth sense for investors who act like bosses.
- Emotional support and specific, tactical advice are more valuable to founders than generic strategic input.

## Notable Claims

- Investors who do not respond quickly to important emails rarely become successful founders. Evidence: Altman notes he has almost never made money investing in founders who lack this trait.
- The best investment opportunities are found by helping founders, not just networking. Evidence: Altman suggests spending a day per week helping a company to build a sourcing network.

## Quotes

> It’s much easier to get LPs to give you money for your seed fund than it is to get a meaningful allocation in a ‘hot deal’.
> The rate of improvement is often more important than the current absolute ability.
> Founders have a sixth sense for who is going to treat them like a peer and who is going to treat them like a boss.
> The failures don’t matter much, the small successes don’t matter much, and the giant returns are where everything happens.
