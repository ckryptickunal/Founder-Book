# Upside risk

## Summary

Sam Altman argues that angel investors often miscalculate risk by focusing on downside protection rather than the 'upside risk' of missing out on high-growth, power-law-driven investments.

## Key Ideas

- Angel investing returns are driven by the power law, where one outlier investment often outperforms the entire portfolio combined.
- Focusing on downside protection through onerous terms alienates top-tier founders and reduces access to the best deals.
- Founders often make the mistake of prioritizing high valuation over securing high-quality, long-term investors.
- Human intuition is naturally biased toward fearing downside risk, leading to poor decision-making in both private and public markets.

## Topics

- [[topics/power-law-in-investing|Power Law in Investing]]: The principle that a small number of investments account for the vast majority of total portfolio returns.
- [[topics/angel-investing-strategy|Angel Investing Strategy]]: The practice of investing in early-stage startups and the importance of founder-investor alignment.
- [[topics/risk-perception|Risk Perception]]: The psychological tendency for humans to over-index on downside risk while ignoring the opportunity cost of missing out on massive upside.

## Notable Claims

- The real risk in angel investing is missing out on the single best investment, not losing capital on smaller deals. Evidence: The power law dictates that one 300x or 3000x return will likely exceed the returns of all other investments combined.
- Founders who obsess over high valuations often hurt their companies. Evidence: High valuations can price out good investors and lead to difficult down rounds in the future.

## Quotes

> The consequence of this is that the real risk is missing out on that outstanding investment, and not failing to get your money back.
> Instead of downside risk, more investors should think about upside risk—not getting to invest in the company that will provide the return everyone is looking for.
> The end of the world only happens once; it’s very unlikely to be Monday morning.
