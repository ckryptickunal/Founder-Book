# The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-the-18-mistakes-that-kill-startups`

## Summary

Paul Graham outlines 18 common pitfalls that lead to startup failure, arguing that almost all failures stem from the fundamental mistake of not creating something users actually want. He provides practical advice on team dynamics, location, product development, funding, and the necessity of full-time commitment.

## Key Ideas

- The primary cause of startup failure is failing to make something users want.
- Single-founder startups are at a significant disadvantage due to lack of support and peer pressure.
- Startups should avoid 'marginal niches' and instead solve real, significant problems.
- Founders should be open to pivoting based on user feedback rather than being obstinate about an initial vision.
- Launching quickly is essential to understand the problem and force progress.
- Raising too much money can lead to premature scaling, office politics, and a loss of focus.
- Founders must be willing to do 'unpleasant' work like sales and marketing, not just coding.
- A half-hearted effort, often manifested by keeping a day job, is a major predictor of failure.

## Notable Claims

- Startups founded by a single person are statistically less likely to succeed. Evidence: Observation of successful companies and the psychological need for peer support and shared commitment.
- Choosing a marginal niche to avoid competition is a mistake. Evidence: Good ideas naturally attract competition; avoiding it usually means avoiding good ideas.
- Raising too much money can kill a startup. Evidence: It forces premature scaling, introduces office politics, and makes it harder to pivot.
