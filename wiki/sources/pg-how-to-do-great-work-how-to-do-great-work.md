# How to Do Great Work

## Summary

Paul Graham outlines a practical, non-systematic approach to achieving great work, emphasizing the importance of curiosity, ambition, and the iterative process of discovery over rigid planning.

## Key Ideas

- Great work requires a combination of natural aptitude, deep interest, and the scope for greatness.
- The process of doing great work involves four steps: choosing a field, learning to the frontier, noticing gaps, and exploring them.
- Curiosity is the primary engine and rudder for great work; it guides you to what is worth paying attention to.
- Avoid over-planning; instead, 'stay upwind' by doing what is most interesting and keeps your options open.
- Consistency and compounding effort are more important than daily bursts of productivity.
- Originality is a habit of mind, not a process; it is best cultivated by trying to build or understand things slightly too difficult.
- The young have advantages in energy and fresh perspectives, while the old have advantages in knowledge and efficiency; both should be leveraged.
- Intellectual honesty and earnestness are essential to avoid the distortions of affectation and fashion.

## Topics

- [[topics/the-nature-of-ambition|The Nature of Ambition]]: Ambition is a driving force that can either precede or grow out of interest; it is best when aligned with genuine curiosity.
- [[topics/the-role-of-luck|The Role of Luck]]: Luck is a significant factor in discovery; one should make themselves a 'big target' for luck by being curious and trying many things.
- [[topics/educational-systems|Educational Systems]]: Schools often induce passivity and provide a misleading view of work by focusing on soluble problems and 'hacking the test'.
- [[topics/the-importance-of-morale|The Importance of Morale]]: Morale is the foundation of ambitious work and must be nurtured through physical health, optimism, and choosing the right colleagues.

## Notable Claims

- The best way to figure out what to work on is by working. Evidence: You cannot know what a field is like until you engage with it; working allows for the discovery of aptitude and interest.
- Great work is often a result of exponential growth. Evidence: Learning and building an audience compound over time, though the initial phase feels flat and unrewarding.
- Trying to be the best is often easier than trying to be merely good. Evidence: Aiming for the best simplifies decision-making and provides a clearer, more exciting standard.
