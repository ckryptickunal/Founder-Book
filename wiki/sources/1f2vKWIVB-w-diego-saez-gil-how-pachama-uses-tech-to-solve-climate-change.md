# Diego Saez Gil - How Pachama Uses Tech to Solve Climate Change

## Summary

Diego Saez Gil, founder of Pachama, discusses how his company uses artificial intelligence and remote sensing to bring transparency and efficiency to the carbon credit market, specifically focusing on forest conservation and reforestation as a scalable solution to climate change.

## Key Ideas

- Carbon markets are currently inefficient and lack transparency, hindering investment in forest-based climate solutions.
- Pachama uses satellite imagery, lidar, and machine learning to verify carbon capture in forests, reducing the cost and time of certification.
- There is a massive, untapped potential for reforestation and forest conservation to sequester significant amounts of carbon.
- Climate change is both a major global challenge and a significant opportunity for value creation through technology-driven startups.
- The transition to a sustainable economy requires a combination of voluntary corporate action, consumer demand, and government regulation.

## Topics

- [[topics/carbon-markets|Carbon Markets]]: Mechanisms for companies to offset emissions by purchasing credits from projects that reduce or capture carbon.
- [[topics/remote-sensing-and-ai|Remote Sensing and AI]]: Using satellite data and machine learning to monitor and verify forest biomass and carbon storage.
- [[topics/reforestation-and-conservation|Reforestation and Conservation]]: Nature-based solutions to climate change that involve protecting existing forests or planting new ones.
- [[topics/climate-tech-entrepreneurship|Climate Tech Entrepreneurship]]: Applying the Silicon Valley startup playbook to solve large-scale environmental problems.

## Notable Claims

- Reforestation and forest conservation have the potential to draw down approximately 200 gigatons of carbon. Evidence: Cited a recent research paper from a university in Switzerland.
- Pachama's machine learning model has less than 1.5% error in predicting carbon storage in forests. Evidence: Demonstrated by their team using lidar data and satellite imagery.
- The carbon market is growing, with over 80 billion dollars invested in initiatives globally last year. Evidence: Referenced World Bank data.
