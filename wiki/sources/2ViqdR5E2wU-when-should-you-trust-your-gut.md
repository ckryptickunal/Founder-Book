# When Should You Trust Your Gut?

## Summary

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel from Y Combinator discuss the nuance of when startup founders should trust their intuition versus when they should seek external validation, emphasizing that the correct approach depends heavily on whether the founder possesses deep domain expertise.

## Key Ideas

- Founders with deep domain expertise should trust their gut more, as they have developed 'taste' and a unique understanding of the problem space.
- Founders without domain expertise should prioritize learning, talking to customers, and building simple MVPs rather than relying on unvalidated assumptions.
- A common mistake is for experts to ignore their intuition due to fear or external pressure (e.g., chasing trends like AI), while novices often act with overconfidence.
- The 'Mom Test' or extreme customer-led development is a strategy for those without expertise, whereas experts should focus on building products that impress themselves.
- Startup advice is not one-size-fits-all; it must be tailored to the founder's specific background and knowledge level.

## Notable Claims

- Founders with deep expertise in a specific industry should trust their gut more than those without. Evidence: Experts have already validated their taste through past work and understand the specific pain points of the industry.
- Founders without expertise should avoid having prescriptive ideas about what a market needs. Evidence: Without experience, initial assumptions are likely to be incorrect; learning on the fly is more effective.
