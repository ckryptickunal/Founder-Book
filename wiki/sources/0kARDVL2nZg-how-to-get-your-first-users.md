# How To Get Your First Users

## Summary

This video provides a strategic framework for early-stage startup founders to acquire their first users, emphasizing that finding customers is a search problem rather than a persuasion problem. It introduces the concept of a 'minimum evolvable product' and argues that early adopters shape the long-term trajectory of a company's product evolution.

## Key Ideas

- Focus on finding 'early adopters' who have a burning need rather than trying to persuade the general public.
- Adopt a 'minimum evolvable product' mindset instead of just a minimum viable product.
- Charge real money early to ensure high-quality feedback and validate the problem's severity.
- Use targeted personal outreach (cold emails, direct contact) rather than broad marketing.
- Treat early users like an anthropologist studying a civilization to understand their decision-making.
- Product evolution is path-dependent; the preferences of your first users will dictate the features and identity of your mature product.

## Notable Claims

- Finding your first users is a search problem, not a persuasion problem. Evidence: You are looking for people who already have a burning need or a natural inclination to try new things, rather than convincing uninterested people.
- Early adopters are rarely price sensitive. Evidence: People with a burning problem prioritize solving that problem over the cost of the tool.
- Product evolution is path-dependent. Evidence: Tesla's current vehicle characteristics (acceleration vs. comfort) are a direct result of the specific preferences of their early Roadster customers.
