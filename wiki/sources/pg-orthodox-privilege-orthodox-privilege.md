# Orthodox Privilege

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-orthodox-privilege`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the concept of 'orthodox privilege,' a form of blindness where conventional-minded individuals fail to recognize that certain true ideas are socially dangerous to express, leading them to falsely believe that free speech is universally safe.

## Key Ideas

- Orthodox privilege is the inability of conventional-minded people to perceive that some true statements are socially prohibited.
- Historical precedent suggests that every era has had 'unspeakable' truths, making it highly improbable that the present day is an exception.
- People with orthodox privilege often demand evidence for 'unspeakable' truths, failing to realize that the nature of such truths makes them impossible to voice safely.
- The disagreement over 'cancel culture' stems from this privilege; those who possess it assume that anyone punished for their speech must deserve it.
- Appealing to politeness—taking others at their word regarding their perception of social constraints—is a more effective strategy than direct confrontation.

## Topics

- [[topics/orthodox-privilege|Orthodox Privilege]]: The cognitive bias where conventional thinkers assume that because their own opinions are safe to express, all opinions are safe.
- [[topics/cancel-culture|Cancel Culture]]: A social phenomenon where individuals face consequences for their speech, which is perceived differently depending on one's level of orthodox privilege.
- [[topics/epistemology|Epistemology]]: The study of how we form beliefs and the limitations imposed by our social environment on what we consider 'true' or 'sayable'.

## Notable Claims

- It is historically improbable that the current era is the first in which all true statements can be spoken without consequence. Evidence: Historical consistency suggests that every era has had social taboos that punished the expression of certain truths.
- Conventional-minded people cannot overcome orthodox privilege through learning alone. Evidence: The privilege is rooted in a lack of independent-mindedness, which cannot be corrected on the time scale of a single conversation.

## Quotes

> The more conventional-minded someone is, the more it seems to them that it's safe for everyone to express their opinions.
> If someone says they can think of things that are true but that cannot be said, it's only polite to take them at their word, even if you can't think of any yourself.
> If you believe there's nothing true that you can't say, then anyone who gets in trouble for something they say must deserve it.
