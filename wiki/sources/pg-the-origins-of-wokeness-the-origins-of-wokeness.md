## Summary

Paul Graham explores the historical and sociological roots of 'wokeness,' defining it as a second, more aggressive wave of political correctness. He argues that wokeness is a form of performative moralism driven by 'prigs'—people naturally inclined to enforce shallow moral purity—who have found a new outlet for their behavior within universities and professionalized bureaucratic roles.

## Key Ideas

- Wokeness is a modern iteration of an ancient human phenomenon: the 'prig' who enforces moral purity to signal superiority.
- Political correctness and wokeness are defined as an aggressively performative focus on social justice.
- The movement originated in humanities and social science departments in the 1980s as 1960s radicals gained tenure and institutional power.
- Social media and group chat apps acted as catalysts for the second wave of wokeness, enabling the formation of 'cancel mobs'.
- The rise of professionalized DEI administrators created a class of people whose job security depends on finding and enforcing new moral improprieties.
- Wokeness functions like a religion, complete with doctrines, original sin (privilege), and heretics.
- The most effective way to counter future outbreaks is to develop strong cultural antibodies against the concept of heresy and to demand proof of harm before accepting new speech restrictions.

## Notable Claims

- Wokeness peaked in 2020-2021 and is currently in a state of retreat. Evidence: Corporate rejection of DEI, university commitments to free speech, and consumer backlash against brands like Bud Light.
- The rise of social media created a feedback loop that incentivizes outrage. Evidence: Data from the author's own forum showing users were three times more likely to upvote content that outraged them.
- Wokeness is a religion without a God. Evidence: It possesses concepts of original sin (privilege), heresy, and requires abject repentance for salvation.
