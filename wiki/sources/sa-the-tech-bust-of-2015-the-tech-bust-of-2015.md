# The Tech Bust of 2015

## Summary

Sam Altman argues that the 2015 tech market is not in a bubble, but rather experiencing a shift where late-stage private equity is being structured as debt. He contends that public markets and early-to-mid-stage private companies are reasonably valued, while the 'bubble' narrative in late-stage private tech stems from a misunderstanding of complex financial instruments like liquidation preferences.

## Key Ideas

- Public tech valuations are generally reasonable, with some mega-caps trading at low P/E ratios.
- Early and mid-stage private valuations have remained relatively stable and are not in a bubble.
- Late-stage private 'equity' rounds are increasingly functioning as debt instruments due to aggressive liquidation preferences.
- The 'tech bubble' narrative is driven by a few struggling unicorns, while the majority of companies are performing well.
- Easy access to capital in late-stage rounds can harm companies by masking poor unit economics and distorting internal decision-making.
- Long-term, technology remains a superior asset class despite potential short-term market corrections.

## Notable Claims

- Late-stage private tech investments are often structured as debt rather than equity. Evidence: The observation of 2x liquidation preferences and 3x liquidation caps in recent deal terms.
- Public tech companies are not currently in a bubble. Evidence: S&P Tech P/E ratios are lower than the overall S&P P/E, and IPO volume is at a seven-year low.
- Easy capital harms companies. Evidence: It allows companies to operate with bad unit economics and masks internal operational problems.
