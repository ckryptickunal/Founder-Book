# What You Can't Say

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the concept of 'moral fashions'—the invisible, socially enforced taboos of any given era that prevent people from expressing certain opinions. He argues that just as historical figures like Galileo were persecuted for ideas later proven true, modern society likely harbors its own ridiculous and restrictive taboos. Graham provides strategies for identifying these 'unthinkable' thoughts and suggests that maintaining intellectual independence requires questioning conventional wisdom, recognizing the power dynamics behind censorship, and practicing 'closed thoughts and an open face' to navigate social pressures.

## Key Ideas

- Moral fashions are invisible to those living within them, similar to how fashion trends or the earth's rotation are perceived.
- Taboos are often enforced by groups that are powerful enough to suppress dissent but nervous enough to feel threatened by it.
- The most dangerous taboos are those that might actually be true, as they threaten the existing power structure.
- Labels like 'divisive', 'inappropriate', or 'heretical' are often used to shut down debate without addressing the truth of an argument.
- Intellectual growth requires the ability to think thoughts that are socially unacceptable, even if one chooses not to express them publicly.
- Humor and abstraction are effective tools for countering dogmatic censorship.

## Topics

- [[topics/moral-fashions|Moral Fashions]]: The shifting, arbitrary, and often invisible social norms that dictate what is acceptable to say or believe in a specific culture and time.
- [[topics/intellectual-independence|Intellectual Independence]]: The practice of questioning conventional wisdom and maintaining the ability to think critically despite social pressure to conform.
- [[topics/censorship-and-power|Censorship and Power]]: The mechanism by which groups in power use labels and social ostracization to suppress ideas that threaten their status or ideology.

## Notable Claims

- If you have no opinions that you are reluctant to express in front of peers, you are likely just repeating what you have been told. Evidence: The statistical improbability of independently arriving at the exact same set of beliefs as the majority, including their potential errors.
- The most effective way to identify taboos is to look at what people get in trouble for saying. Evidence: Historical patterns where statements that were once considered dangerous or heretical (e.g., heliocentrism) were later accepted as fact.
- There is a correlation between high intelligence and the willingness to consider shocking or heretical ideas. Evidence: The tendency of scientists and innovative thinkers to actively seek out cracks in conventional wisdom.
