# Vinod Khosla : How to Build the Future

## Summary

Vinod Khosla, founder of Sun Microsystems and Khosla Ventures, discusses the philosophy of building ambitious, billion-dollar companies. He emphasizes that a company is defined by its people rather than its plan, advocates for 'gene pool engineering' in hiring, and stresses the importance of long-term vision over short-term revenue metrics.

## Key Ideas

- A company becomes the people it hires, not the plan it makes.
- Distinguish between a $0 million company and a $0 billion company mindset from day one.
- Vision should be fixed (like climbing Mount Everest), but tactics must be flexible and iterative.
- The 'kitchen cabinet' of early hires is the most critical factor in a company's success.
- Investors should be treated as permanent employees who cannot be fired; most add negative value by prioritizing short-term liquidity over long-term vision.
- Equity should be distributed generously to attract high-quality, 'unreasonable' talent who could otherwise start their own companies.
- First-principles thinking and the rate of learning are more important than established industry experience.

## Notable Claims

- 90% of investors add no value, and 70% add negative value. Evidence: Khosla argues that many investors lack the experience of building a company and provide stress-inducing, short-term advice.
- The best employees are those who have great opportunities elsewhere. Evidence: Khosla notes that if someone doesn't have other great options, they are likely not the caliber of person needed to build a billion-dollar company.
- A business plan is irrelevant except to judge how a founder thinks. Evidence: Khosla asserts that plans change constantly, so the plan itself is less important than the founder's ability to reason from first principles.
