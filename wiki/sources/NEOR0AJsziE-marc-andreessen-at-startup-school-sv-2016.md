# Marc Andreessen at Startup School SV 2016

## Summary

In this 2016 fireside chat at Y Combinator's Startup School, Marc Andreessen discusses the founding philosophy of Andreessen Horowitz (a16z), the importance of founder-CEOs, the mechanics of institutional fundraising, and how to evaluate venture capital firms. He emphasizes the 'struggle' of building companies, the value of historical perspective, and identifies AI, biology-CS intersection, and cryptocurrency as the most transformative fields for the next two decades.

## Key Ideas

- The best companies are often run by their founders for long periods, contrary to the 'professional CEO' trend.
- Venture capital firms should provide 'superpowers' to founders through operational support and extensive networks.
- Fundraising is a test of a founder's ability to network and sell, skills that are essential for hiring and customer acquisition.
- Warm referrals are a necessary first test of a founder's networking capability.
- There are no silver bullets in startups; success requires grinding labor and navigating the 'struggle'.
- Founders often feel they are too late to a market, but the greater risk is being too early.
- History provides a vital mental toolkit for entrepreneurs to understand that building transformative things is always difficult.

## Topics

- [[topics/founder-ceo-model|Founder-CEO Model]]: The belief that founders are uniquely positioned to lead their companies through long-term growth.
- [[topics/institutional-fundraising|Institutional Fundraising]]: The formal process of raising capital from professional venture firms, involving warm referrals and rigorous pitching.
- [[topics/the-struggle|The Struggle]]: The inevitable, difficult, and messy reality of building a company that requires persistence rather than silver bullets.
- [[topics/market-timing|Market Timing]]: The challenge of entering a market when infrastructure and consumer behavior are ready, rather than being too early.

## Notable Claims

- The best venture firms are those that provide operational support and networks to founders. Evidence: a16z's model of employing 85 professionals to build networks for portfolio companies.
- Warm referrals are a valid test for founders. Evidence: If a founder cannot network their way to a VC, they will likely fail at networking to hire talent or sell to customers.
- The biggest risk for startups is being too early to market. Evidence: The failure of the Apple Newton 20 years before the successful iPad.

## Quotes

> There are no silver bullets. There are only lead bullets.
> The great Founders almost always feel like you're too late and you're almost always too early.
> If you can't figure out a way to network your way to a VC firm, which is of course in the business of meeting Founders, then you're unlikely to be able to network your way into hiring a great team.
