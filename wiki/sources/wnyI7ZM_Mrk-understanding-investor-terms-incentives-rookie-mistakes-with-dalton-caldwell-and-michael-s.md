## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell from Y Combinator discuss common fundraising pitfalls for first-time founders, emphasizing that investors have their own incentives, agendas, and professional goals that may not always align with the founder's best interests.

## Key Ideas

- Founders should prioritize deal terms and rights over just valuation and total capital raised.
- Investors often use 'jargon-filled' terms like participating preferred or super pro-rata to gain leverage.
- Investors who have a track record of backing billion-dollar companies are generally better partners than those optimizing for small exits.
- The phrase 'come back when you find a lead' is often a polite way for an investor to say 'no' while keeping a free option on the company.
- Professionalization of seed funds has created new misalignments, such as investors pushing for higher capital raises to meet their own ownership targets.
- Founders must recognize that investors are negotiating from a position of experience and should use standard paperwork and experienced legal counsel to protect themselves.

## Notable Claims

- The phrase 'come back when you find a lead' usually means 'no'. Evidence: Investors use this to maintain a free option on the company without committing capital, while avoiding a direct rejection.
- Investors may push founders to raise more money than needed to satisfy their own ownership targets. Evidence: Professional seed funds have obligations to their LPs and internal goals regarding ownership percentages.

## Quotes
