## Summary

In this lecture from the 'How to Start a Startup' series, Sam Altman discusses the critical importance of team building and execution. He emphasizes that founders should prioritize hiring only when necessary, focus on finding 'relentlessly resourceful' co-founders with a shared history, and maintain a high bar for talent. The lecture also covers the necessity of maintaining momentum, the role of the CEO in setting an execution rhythm, and the importance of focusing on metrics and product growth over external distractions like PR or competitors.

## Key Ideas

- Co-founder relationships are the most common cause of early startup failure; choose partners you have a long history with.
- Hire slowly and only when absolutely necessary; the cost of a bad early hire can be fatal.
- The CEO's primary roles are setting the vision, fundraising, evangelizing, hiring/managing, and setting the execution bar.
- Startups should prioritize momentum and growth above all else; if growth stalls, focus on small wins to regain it.
- Avoid remote co-founding teams; physical proximity is essential for the communication speed required in early-stage startups.
- Equity should be split near-equally among co-founders and include a four-year vesting schedule.
- Ignore competitors until they are actually beating you with a superior shipped product.

## Notable Claims

- The number one cause of early death for startups is co-founder blowups. Evidence: Observations from Y Combinator's portfolio of startups.
- Remote co-founding teams are a non-starter for early-stage startups. Evidence: Lack of successful examples among the top 30 software startups of all time.
- Mediocre engineers do not build great companies. Evidence: The compounding effect of early hires on company culture and product quality.
