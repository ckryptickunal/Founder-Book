## Summary

Author Robin Sloan discusses his novel 'Sourdough,' his creative use of machine learning to generate fictional music for the audiobook, and his perspective on the intersection of technology, food, and storytelling in the Bay Area.

## Key Ideas

- Machine learning can be used for creative, non-practical applications like generating fictional languages and music.
- The 'old' and 'new' in technology and craft are not adversarial but rather pile up in a 'glorious crash' that creates new possibilities.
- Digital projects face unique challenges regarding long-term maintenance and preservation compared to printed books.
- Social media, specifically Twitter, has changed the nature of public discourse and blogging, leading to the decline of long-form blog discussions.
- The 'egg problem' in robotics—the difficulty of automating chaotic, organic tasks like cooking—remains a significant hurdle for AGI.
- Sloan advocates for a 'positive satire' or 'puzzle fiction' genre that avoids pure dystopia while remaining grounded in reality.

## Topics

- [[topics/machine-learning-in-art|Machine Learning in Art]]: Using neural networks to generate creative outputs like music and text rather than just practical economic applications.
- [[topics/digital-preservation|Digital Preservation]]: The difficulty of maintaining digital art and apps over time compared to the permanence of physical books.
- [[topics/tech-culture-in-fiction|Tech Culture in Fiction]]: The shift from utopian to dystopian narratives in Silicon Valley-themed literature and media.

## Notable Claims

- The Sourdough audiobook is the first to include creative output from a machine learning system. Evidence: Sloan notes this is a 'tiny distinction' but believes it to be the first instance of ML-generated content in an audiobook.
- Twitter killed the blogging era. Evidence: Sloan argues that the shift in audience attention toward social media platforms led to the decline of independent, long-form blog discussions.
- Digital works are inherently more fragile than physical books. Evidence: Sloan cites the loss of his 'Fish' app due to OS updates and the general instability of digital platforms.
