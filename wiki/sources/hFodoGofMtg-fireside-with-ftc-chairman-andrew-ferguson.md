# Fireside with FTC Chairman Andrew Ferguson

## Summary

FTC Chairman Andrew Ferguson discusses his approach to antitrust enforcement, emphasizing a shift toward protecting innovation and individual liberty from both government overreach and corporate monopoly power. He outlines a strategy of vigorous enforcement of existing laws rather than ex-ante regulation, expresses skepticism toward the European Union's Digital Markets Act, and encourages startup founders to report anti-competitive behavior directly to the FTC.

## Key Ideas

- Antitrust enforcement should prioritize protecting innovation and individual liberty, not just price and output metrics.
- The FTC aims to avoid both 'hardcore' anti-M&A ideology and the 'laissez-faire' view that monopoly concerns are overstated.
- Vigorous antitrust enforcement is the preferred alternative to heavy-handed ex-ante regulation.
- The Digital Markets Act (DMA) is viewed with suspicion due to concerns over due process, its potential as a tax on American firms, and its extraterritorial reach.
- The FTC remains open to hearing from builders and startup founders about anti-competitive conduct they encounter in the market.
- Executive branch agencies should be accountable to the President, supporting the recent removal of independent commissioners.

## Topics

- [[topics/antitrust-policy|Antitrust Policy]]: The shift from consumer welfare (price/output) to a broader focus on innovation, market structure, and individual liberty.
- [[topics/platform-gatekeeping|Platform Gatekeeping]]: The concern that dominant tech platforms use 'walled gardens' to extract rents and suppress competitive innovation.
- [[topics/regulatory-philosophy|Regulatory Philosophy]]: The debate between ex-ante regulation (like the DMA) versus ex-post antitrust enforcement through the courts.

## Notable Claims

- The 2013 decision by the Obama administration not to pursue antitrust enforcement against Google was a major mistake. Evidence: Ferguson argues this period allowed Google's search and ad tech monopolies to solidify.
- The Digital Markets Act functions as a form of taxation on American companies. Evidence: Ferguson points to the fact that fines are based on worldwide revenue and that few European companies qualify as gatekeepers.
- Independent agencies are detrimental to democracy. Evidence: Ferguson argues that all executive branch officials should be accountable to the elected President.
