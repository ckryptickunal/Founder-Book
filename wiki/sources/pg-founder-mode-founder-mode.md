## Summary

Paul Graham explores the concept of 'Founder Mode,' a management style that contrasts with the conventional 'Manager Mode.' He argues that the standard advice given to scaling founders—to hire good people and stay out of their way—is often disastrous, and that founders should instead maintain deeper, more direct involvement in their companies, similar to how Steve Jobs operated.

## Key Ideas

- Conventional wisdom for scaling companies (Manager Mode) is often detrimental to founders.
- Founder Mode involves breaking the traditional org chart hierarchy, such as conducting skip-level meetings.
- Founders are often gaslit by professional managers and VCs into adopting a management style that is ill-suited for them.
- Founder Mode is not about micromanagement, but about maintaining the founder's unique ability to influence the company's direction.
- There is a lack of formal literature or education on Founder Mode, leaving founders to experiment on their own.

## Notable Claims

- The advice to 'hire good people and give them room' often leads to hiring professional fakers who damage the company. Evidence: Anecdotal reports from successful founders who experienced failure after following this advice.
- Founder Mode is more effective than Manager Mode for scaling startups. Evidence: The success of companies like Airbnb after shifting away from traditional management advice.
