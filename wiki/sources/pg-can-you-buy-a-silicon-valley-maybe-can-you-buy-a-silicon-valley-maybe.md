# Can You Buy a Silicon Valley?  Maybe.

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-can-you-buy-a-silicon-valley-maybe`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: February 2009
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-can-you-buy-a-silicon-valley-maybe

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the feasibility of cities attempting to manufacture a 'Silicon Valley' startup ecosystem by funding startups directly, arguing that while traditional methods like building universities are slow, a large-scale, high-capital investment strategy could theoretically work if executed correctly.

## Key Ideas

- Seed funding is not a regional business because early-stage startups are highly mobile.
- To make startups stay in a city, they must be funded at a level where they are rooted or successful enough to resist relocation pressure from Silicon Valley VCs.
- A city could potentially become a major startup hub by investing roughly $1 million per startup to attract 1,000 companies, costing about the same as a football stadium.
- The primary challenge is the 'picking problem'—identifying high-potential startups—which is a rare skill that governments are ill-equipped to perform.
- A viable strategy involves piggybacking on the due diligence of eminent Silicon Valley angel investors by offering their portfolio companies incentives to relocate.
- The success of such a program depends heavily on the city's existing culture, walkability, and appeal to 'nerds'.

## Notable Claims

- Small-scale local seed funds have near-zero effect on a city's startup scene. Evidence: Y Combinator's experience in Boston showed that funded startups remain mobile and gravitate toward Silicon Valley regardless of where they received initial funding.
- A city can jumpstart a startup hub for the price of a football stadium. Evidence: By investing $1 million each into 1,000 startups, a city could create a critical mass of companies that would eventually attract local VC offices and create a self-sustaining cycle.
- Governments are poor at picking startups. Evidence: The skill of identifying high-potential startups is rare and difficult to measure, leading to a high likelihood that government-appointed committees would select poor-quality companies.
