# How To Design Products That Truly Stand Out

## Summary

Kari Sarnin, co-founder and CEO of Linear, shares insights on prioritizing high-quality design and building a standout product. Drawing from his experiences at Y Combinator, Coinbase, and Airbnb, he emphasizes that design and brand are crucial for building trust, differentiating in competitive markets, and accelerating company growth. Linear, an issue tracking tool, exemplifies this philosophy by focusing on craft, quality, and empowering small, autonomous teams of engineers and designers. Sarnin also discusses the transformative impact of AI, suggesting it will lower the barrier to entry for design while raising the ceiling for top-tier companies, and will shift designers' roles towards managing AI output and focusing on strategic problem-solving rather than making them obsolete.

## Key Ideas

- High-quality design and a strong brand are critical for product differentiation, building trust, and accelerating company growth, especially in early startup stages.
- A company's brand encompasses every customer touchpoint, from product visuals to sales interactions, and should reflect core values like quality and authenticity.
- Empowering small, autonomous teams of engineers and designers to drive projects and take ownership is key to achieving both quality and speed, avoiding 'design by committee.'
- Hiring individuals with product sensibilities, curiosity, and a track record of building things independently is more important than rigid processes for fostering a culture of quality.
- Designers should broaden their focus beyond aesthetic tasks to understand and solve core business problems, collaborating with sales and leadership.
- AI will lower the barrier for basic design and product development but will also raise the bar for truly exceptional work, shifting designers' roles towards strategic oversight and managing AI-generated output.
- The ease of AI-generated output poses a risk of reduced critical thinking and attention to detail; 'suffering' through difficult problems can clarify design intent.

## Notable Claims

- Great design can make or break your product. Evidence: Kari Sarnin's career experience at Coinbase and Airbnb, and Linear's success, are presented as evidence of design's critical impact.
- Linear has about 15,000 companies as customers, including growth companies like Mercury, Ramp, Bretool, and larger companies like OpenAI and Block. Evidence: Stated directly by Kari Sarnin as current company statistics.
- YC helped clarify that building companies doesn't have to be complicated, especially in the beginning, requiring singular focus on making progress for customers. Evidence: Kari Sarnin's personal reflection on lessons learned from YC.
- At Coinbase, the job of a designer was to change the company to look more trustworthy and professional to go mainstream, simplifying crypto for a broader audience. Evidence: Kari Sarnin's description of his design challenges and solutions at Coinbase, including fixing the logo and website visuals.
- Silicon Valley still often misunderstands brand or design, logically acknowledging its importance but lacking the language or experience to fully grasp it. Evidence: Kari Sarnin's observation from his time at Airbnb and broader industry experience.
- Quality in product design doesn't mean perfection or endless polishing, but rather a consistent direction and idea to provide a great user experience. Evidence: Kari Sarnin's explanation of Linear's philosophy on quality, balancing it with shipping frequently and iterating.
- Having more people working on things doesn't necessarily make it better; it can often make it worse due to loss of focus and 'design by committee.' Evidence: Kari Sarnin's experience in other companies and Linear's small team structure as a counter-approach.
- It's hard to spec quality or the right solution; empowering engineers and designers to drive projects allows them to tweak and improve execution. Evidence: Kari Sarnin's observation that designs often don't work in practice as spec'd, leading to delays if ownership isn't given to builders.
- AI will lower the floor on design, enabling companies without designers to achieve decent design, and helping designers with ideas, but it will also raise the ceiling for top-tier design. Evidence: Kari Sarnin's analysis of AI's dual impact on design accessibility and excellence.
- The danger with AI making things easy is that you stop paying attention to them; 'suffering' through hard problems can clarify design and reveal if it's the right thing to do. Evidence: Kari Sarnin's philosophical take on the potential pitfalls of over-reliance on AI for creative output.
- AI will not make designers obsolete; instead, it will shift their roles towards managing AI output, focusing on narrative, storytelling, and ensuring the AI's work is good and fits the company's goals. Evidence: Kari Sarnin's optimistic outlook on the evolution of the designer's role in an AI-driven world, predicting industry growth and more software.
