# AI Is Eating Logistics

## Summary

Ryan Petersen, CEO of Flexport, discusses how his company is leveraging AI to transform the logistics industry. By automating manual, human-intensive tasks like document parsing, shipment planning, and warehouse communication, Flexport aims to reduce ocean freight costs by 8-10% while improving transit times. Petersen emphasizes the importance of 'founder mode,' the strategic use of hackathons for bottom-up innovation, and the goal of making global logistics a seamless utility by 2035.

## Key Ideas

- Logistics is a scale-driven industry where automation acts as a force multiplier for efficiency.
- Incumbent companies have distinct advantages in AI adoption: massive data sets, domain expertise, and existing distribution.
- AI can bridge the gap between 'fast' and 'cheap' in logistics, which are traditionally seen as a trade-off.
- Founder mode involves being hands-on and directive while still fostering bottom-up innovation through mechanisms like hackathons.
- The goal of logistics should be to become a 'utility'—like the electrical grid—where customers don't have to think about the underlying complexity.
- Capital should be used to scale, but founders must avoid the trap of using money to solve problems that should be solved by better processes or automation.

## Notable Claims

- Flexport can reduce ocean freight costs by 8-10% over the next few years using AI. Evidence: Internal AI models already saved 2% of ocean freight spend while improving transit times by 20%.
- AI agents can effectively perform tasks that were previously too expensive for humans to do. Evidence: Automated AI agents now handle warehouse appointment confirmations via voice and email.
- The role of a company is to deliver goods and services efficiently, not just to employ people. Evidence: Lowering costs through automation makes goods more available and accessible to society.
