# The separation of advice and money

## Metadata

- Video ID: `sa-the-separation-of-advice-and-money`
- Channel: Sam Altman Essays
- Published: 2013-10-08 17:57:54
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sa-the-separation-of-advice-and-money

## Summary

Sam Altman discusses the shifting landscape of venture capital, specifically the decoupling of financial investment from strategic mentorship, driven by the rise of crowdfunding and increased founder leverage.

## Key Ideas

- The traditional model of venture capital as a package deal of money and advice is breaking down.
- Founders are gaining more leverage over investors due to lower startup costs and increased cultural interest in startups.
- Crowdfunding allows founders to raise capital without being forced to accept advice from institutional investors.
- The separation of advice and money will likely lead to a market correction where mediocre VCs struggle to survive.
- There is a risk that the democratization of startup investing will lead to significant financial losses for inexperienced investors.

## Entities

- [[entities/sam-altman|Sam Altman]] (person): Entrepreneur, investor, and author of the essay.

## Topics

- [[topics/venture-capital-evolution|Venture Capital Evolution]]: The transition from institutional gatekeeping to a more fragmented market where money and advice are sourced separately.
- [[topics/crowdfunding|Crowdfunding]]: A mechanism allowing founders to bypass traditional VC gatekeepers to raise capital directly from the public.
- [[topics/founder-leverage|Founder Leverage]]: The increasing power of founders to dictate terms due to lower capital requirements and increased competition among investors.

## Notable Claims

- The separation of advice and money will lead to more competition and better behavior from investors. Evidence: Increased transparency and the availability of alternative funding sources like crowdfunding reduce the monopolistic power of traditional VCs.
- Startup investing is at risk of becoming the 'new daytrading'. Evidence: The accessibility of crowdfunding appeals to gambler's instincts and attracts investors who may lack the expertise to evaluate startup risk.

## Quotes

> In a sense, VCs sell advice, but founders want to buy money.
> The mediocre and bad VCs will have to adapt or die.
> The bigger force at work is the long-term trend towards founders having more leverage than investors.
