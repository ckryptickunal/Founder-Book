# Investors Said No, Now What?

## Summary

Y Combinator partners discuss how founders should handle investor rejections, emphasizing that founders are the true experts on their businesses and should prioritize progress over the specific reasons given for a 'no'.

## Key Ideas

- Believe the 'no', but do not believe the 'why' provided by investors.
- Investors often lack deep expertise in a specific startup's niche and may provide superficial or inaccurate reasons for rejection.
- Founders should not pivot their entire business based on a single investor's email feedback.
- The best way to change an investor's mind is through tangible business progress (e.g., acquiring new customers) rather than debating the rejection.
- Investors often reject based on pattern matching or stack ranking against other opportunities, which are not necessarily reflections of the startup's actual potential.
- Consistency in rejection reasons across many investors is the only time a founder should consider the feedback as a potential data point.

## Notable Claims

- Investors are rarely experts in the specific domain of the startup they are evaluating. Evidence: Y Combinator partners note that they rarely feel qualified to judge a startup's idea even with their experience, and investors often have high failure rates.
- Changing the facts of the business is the only effective way to change an investor's mind. Evidence: Y Combinator frequently sees companies get rejected, apply again after making progress, and get accepted by the same investors who previously said no.
