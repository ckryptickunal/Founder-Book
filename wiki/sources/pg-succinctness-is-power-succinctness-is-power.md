# Succinctness is Power

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that the power of a programming language is directly proportional to its succinctness. He posits that high-level languages exist to provide leverage, allowing programmers to express complex ideas with fewer elements, and that 'readability' is often a misleading metric used to justify verbose, less powerful languages.

## Key Ideas

- Succinctness is the primary measure of a programming language's power.
- The purpose of high-level languages is to reduce the conceptual load by minimizing the number of elements required to express a program.
- Lines of code is a poor metric for program size; a better metric is the number of distinct nodes in a program's abstract syntax tree.
- Restrictiveness in a language is primarily a symptom of a lack of succinctness.
- Readability-per-line is often a marketing tactic that obscures the total effort required to read a larger, more verbose program.
- The true test of a language is its ability to help a programmer discover and solve new problems, rather than solving predefined ones.

## Notable Claims

- Succinctness is equivalent to power in programming languages. Evidence: High-level languages are designed to provide leverage, and power is defined by how well a tool achieves its purpose.
- Programmer productivity is roughly constant regardless of the language used. Evidence: Cites Fred Brooks' observations and Ericsson internal project data.
- Restrictiveness is primarily a lack of succinctness. Evidence: Restrictive languages force programmers to take long detours to express ideas that could be stated more concisely.
