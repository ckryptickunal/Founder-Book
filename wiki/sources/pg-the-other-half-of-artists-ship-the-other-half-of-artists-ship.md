# The Other Half of "Artists Ship"

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-the-other-half-of-artists-ship`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: November 2008
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-the-other-half-of-artists-ship

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the hidden costs of organizational bureaucracy, arguing that while checks and procedures are designed to prevent disasters, they often create significant inefficiencies, drive away top talent, and prevent companies from shipping products effectively.

## Key Ideas

- Organizations accumulate checks and procedures to mitigate past disasters, but often fail to account for the hidden costs of these checks.
- The cost of a check includes not just the administrative time, but the loss of potential suppliers and the discouragement of high-performing talent.
- Bureaucratic purchasing processes can force companies to pay significantly higher prices by excluding smaller, more efficient vendors.
- Government-imposed regulations, such as Sarbanes-Oxley, can have unintended consequences like stifling the IPO market.
- For programmers, the ability to ship code quickly is a primary motivator; excessive release checks lead to decreased productivity and talent attrition.
- The 'Artists Ship' principle implies that if an organization prevents people from shipping, it will inevitably lose its best talent.

## Notable Claims

- The cost of a check is often higher than the benefit it provides. Evidence: The example of corporate software purchasing committees forcing companies to pay $50,000 for software that should cost $5,000.
- Excessive checks drive away the best talent. Evidence: Programmers would sacrifice significant portions of their compensation just to regain the ability to ship code immediately.
- Government regulations can cripple entire economies. Evidence: The historical example of China restricting long trading voyages and the impact of Sarbanes-Oxley on the US IPO market.
