## Summary

A discussion between Mike Davis and Doha Mekki regarding the emergence of a bipartisan consensus on antitrust enforcement against Big Tech, the challenges of maintaining this coalition amidst political factionalism, and the necessity of protecting market competition for startups and national security.

## Key Ideas

- A bipartisan 'horseshoe' coalition has formed between the populist left and right to challenge Big Tech monopolies.
- The primary driver for conservative interest in antitrust was the perceived censorship and deplatforming of conservative voices by tech platforms.
- Antitrust enforcement is framed as a tool to restore free market competition rather than as government regulation.
- Big Tech companies use their gatekeeping power to stifle innovation, harm startups, and potentially compromise national security.
- Internal political friction, such as the firing of FTC commissioners, threatens the continuity of aggressive antitrust efforts.
- The future of antitrust must extend beyond just search and advertising to address AI, healthcare, and agriculture.

## Topics

- [[topics/antitrust-law|Antitrust Law]]: The use of legal frameworks like the Sherman and Clayton Acts to break up monopolies and ensure market competition.
- [[topics/big-tech-gatekeeping|Big Tech Gatekeeping]]: The power of trillion-dollar platforms to control information, commerce, and stifle startup growth.
- [[topics/political-realignment|Political Realignment]]: The convergence of left and right-wing political factions on the issue of corporate power and antitrust.
- [[topics/national-security|National Security]]: The concern that Big Tech's influence and potential ties to foreign powers like China pose a threat to the United States.

## Notable Claims

- Big Tech companies intentionally degrade their own products to prioritize advertising revenue. Evidence: Reference to reports regarding Google search quality.
- Antitrust enforcement is the opposite of regulation. Evidence: Argument that antitrust removes barriers to entry for startups, whereas regulation often protects incumbents.
- The president has the legal authority to fire FTC commissioners at will. Evidence: Mike Davis's interpretation of Article 2 executive power, challenging the Humphrey's Executor precedent.
