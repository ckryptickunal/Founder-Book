# Good and Bad Procrastination

## Metadata

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that procrastination is not inherently bad; rather, it is a matter of what one chooses to do instead of their primary work. He categorizes procrastination into three types, advocating for 'Type-C' procrastination—avoiding trivial errands to focus on significant, ambitious projects—as a necessary trait for high-impact productivity.

## Key Ideas

- Procrastination is inevitable because there are infinite things to do; the goal is to procrastinate well.
- Type-C procrastination involves putting off small, unimportant tasks to work on big, meaningful ones.
- Errands and interruptions are the enemies of deep work, which requires long, uninterrupted stretches of time.
- To-do lists can be a form of 'Type-B' procrastination, where one stays busy with trivial tasks to avoid facing difficult, important problems.
- Big problems are intimidating; one must approach them obliquely and use 'tricks' to maintain momentum.
- True productivity is driven by delight and ambition rather than the dutiful completion of scheduled tasks.

## Notable Claims

- The most impressive people are often terrible procrastinators. Evidence: They prioritize 'big' work over 'small' errands, often neglecting daily chores to focus on significant projects.
- Errands are often used as a subconscious tool to avoid working on hard problems. Evidence: People often fill their time with cleaning or minor tasks to avoid the psychological discomfort of facing a difficult, ambitious project.
- Startups are most productive when they are small and isolated. Evidence: Lack of interruptions allows founders to maintain the focus required for high-level problem solving.

## Quotes

> The question is not how to avoid procrastination, but how to procrastinate well.
> Good procrastination is avoiding errands to do real work.
> The most dangerous form of procrastination is unacknowledged type-B procrastination, because it doesn't feel like procrastination.
> You can't look a big problem too directly in the eye. You have to approach it somewhat obliquely.
