# Before the Startup with Paul Graham (How to Start a Startup 2014: Lecture 3)

## Summary

In this lecture, Paul Graham provides counterintuitive advice for aspiring startup founders, emphasizing that success comes from solving real problems for users rather than 'gaming the system' or following traditional business school tropes. He argues that startups are all-consuming, require genuine curiosity, and are best pursued after gaining domain expertise rather than during college.

## Key Ideas

- Startups are counterintuitive; following standard instincts often leads to failure.
- The primary goal of a startup is to make something people want, not to master fundraising or business mechanics.
- Founders should avoid 'playing house'—imitating the outward appearance of a startup without solving a real problem.
- Startups are all-consuming and require total commitment, making them difficult to balance with being a student.
- The best startup ideas often emerge unconsciously from side projects or deep curiosity in a specific domain.
- Founders should hire people they genuinely like and respect, as early team members act more like co-founders than employees.

## Notable Claims

- Startups are counterintuitive, similar to learning to ski. Evidence: The speaker notes that initial impulses in both domains lead to failure, requiring a conscious effort to override natural instincts.
- Gaming the system does not work in startups. Evidence: Unlike large corporations where one can trick a boss, startups only have users who care exclusively about whether the product works.
- The best way to convince investors is to grow fast. Evidence: Investors prioritize growth metrics over fundraising tricks or pitch decks.

## Quotes

> The way to make your startup grow is to make something that users really love, and then tell them about it.
> Starting a startup is where gaming the system stops working.
> The best startup ideas almost have to start as side projects because they're always such outliers that your conscious mind would reject them as ideas for companies.
> At its best, starting a startup is merely an ulterior motive for curiosity.
