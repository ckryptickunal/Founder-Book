# Hiring is Obsolete

## Metadata

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that the traditional path of graduating college and seeking employment is becoming obsolete for talented young people. He posits that the falling costs of starting a technology company allow young founders to bypass traditional hiring, create products directly for users, and leverage the trend of large companies acquiring startups as a form of 'hiring' talent.

## Key Ideas

- The cost of starting a software startup has dropped so low that it is accessible to undergraduates.
- Young people are often undervalued by large organizations due to the difficulty of measuring their potential.
- Large companies are inherently bad at product development and often acquire startups to gain both technology and talent.
- Starting a startup is a better career risk for young people than seeking traditional employment, even if the startup fails.
- The primary goal of a startup should be to make something users want, rather than just writing cool code.
- Investors and employers are losing their traditional leverage over young hackers as the barrier to entry for entrepreneurship decreases.

## Notable Claims

- The cost of starting a web-based startup is now low enough that it is comparable to the cost of living as a student. Evidence: The primary costs are limited to food and rent, with minimal infrastructure requirements.
- A failed startup is more valuable to employers than two years of experience in a large company. Evidence: Feedback from managers at major tech firms like Yahoo and Microsoft indicates a preference for the initiative shown by founders.
- Large companies are bad at product development because they are risk-averse and lack direct access to user feedback. Evidence: Internal politics, turf protection, and the lack of direct sales/support interaction hinder innovation.
