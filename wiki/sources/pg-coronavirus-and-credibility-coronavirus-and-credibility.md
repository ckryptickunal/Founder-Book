# Coronavirus and Credibility

## Summary

Paul Graham reflects on how the COVID-19 pandemic serves as a unique stress test for the credibility of public figures, exposing those who habitually make confident but false predictions.

## Key Ideas

- Public figures often make false predictions without consequence because outcomes are usually ambiguous or long-term.
- Epidemics provide rapid, unequivocal feedback that exposes inaccurate forecasting.
- The pandemic acts as a 'tide going out' moment, revealing the true competence and credibility of journalists and politicians.
- The public should remember who made incorrect claims during the crisis as a metric for future credibility.

## Notable Claims

- Journalists and politicians frequently make false predictions without facing professional consequences. Evidence: The author notes that these figures often rely on 'mushy' outcomes or long time horizons to avoid accountability.
- Epidemics are uniquely effective at testing the credibility of public figures. Evidence: Unlike other political or social issues, epidemics provide rapid and unequivocal falsification of incorrect predictions.

## Quotes

> It's only when the tide goes out that you learn who's been swimming naked.
> An epidemic is different. It falsifies your predictions rapidly and unequivocally.
> This is the most accurate test of credibility we're ever likely to have.
