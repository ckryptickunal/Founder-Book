# What Basic Game Theory Teaches Us About Startups

## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell of Y Combinator discuss the distinction between zero-sum games (where one party's gain is another's loss) and positive-sum games (where value is created). They argue that while zero-sum activities like gambling or predatory financial practices may offer short-term rewards, they are unsustainable, lack long-term value, and often lead to personal unhappiness, whereas building startups and solving real problems creates enduring value and a positive legacy.

## Key Ideas

- Zero-sum games involve wealth transfer without value creation, often relying on manipulation or luck.
- Positive-sum games create lasting value, such as building a house or solving a customer's problem.
- Human psychology is prone to the 'gambler's fallacy' and the desire for disproportionate wins without proportional effort.
- Zero-sum participants often face sudden, catastrophic failure because they lack the incremental learning experiences found in building real businesses.
- FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out) often drives people toward zero-sum games when they see others getting rich quickly.
- Legacy and personal fulfillment are tied to building things that improve society, rather than extracting wealth from others.
- The 'one plus one equals three' principle applies to positive-sum relationships and collaborations.

## Notable Claims

- Zero-sum games lack the incremental learning opportunities found in positive-sum endeavors. Evidence: Founders building real companies learn from failures, whereas gamblers or speculators often face sudden, total loss without prior warning.
- The world becomes unstable when zero-sum participants gain too much influence. Evidence: Historical financial crises are cited as examples where predatory practices led to systemic collapse.
