# Most Important Lifestyle Habits Of Successful Founders

## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell from Y Combinator discuss the importance of 'adulting' and building sustainable lifestyle habits to help founders navigate the inevitable highs and lows of startup life. They categorize advice into preventative measures—such as auditing one's living environment, information diet, and co-founder communication—and reactive treatments for when setbacks occur, emphasizing that founders should not sacrifice their long-term well-being or personal integrity to sustain a failing venture.

## Key Ideas

- Founders must 'adult harder' than their peers to build extraordinary companies, which often requires opting out of typical social activities.
- Preventative health (sleep, diet, exercise) is the 80/20 solution for maintaining the resilience needed to handle startup crises.
- Information diet is as critical as physical diet; founders should aggressively limit exposure to toxic social media and investor gossip.
- Compensation should be viewed as a tool for sustainability—enough to avoid debt and maintain focus, but not necessarily for wealth accumulation during the early stages.
- Conflict with co-founders is inevitable; the key is mastering the 'meta-conversation' about how to handle frustration rather than arguing about the specific issue at hand.
- When a crisis hits, 'no action' is a valid and often underutilized strategic choice.
- A failed startup does not mean the founder is a failure; the experience gained is valuable for future endeavors.

## Notable Claims

- Founders who do not have their physical health (sleep, diet, exercise) in order are not well-situated to handle hard challenges. Evidence: Clinician perspective on how physical health serves as the foundation for mental resilience.
- The best founders use 'no action' as a strategic play 20-40% of the time when dealing with crises. Evidence: Observation of successful founders who avoid impulsive responses to bad news.
- Founders often fall victim to predatory service providers when their startup is failing. Evidence: Anecdotal evidence of non-technical founders spending life savings on ineffective dev shops.
