# Hard Tech is Back

## Metadata

- Video ID: `sa-hard-tech-is-back`
- Channel: Sam Altman Essays
- Published: 2016-03-11 16:19:09
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sa-hard-tech-is-back

## Summary

Sam Altman addresses the criticism that Silicon Valley ignores 'hard technology,' highlighting Y Combinator's commitment to funding deep tech startups and citing the success of companies like Cruise as evidence of the model's viability.

## Key Ideas

- Hard technology companies are viable and successful within the Y Combinator model.
- The YC model is effective for supporting long-term, capital-intensive technology projects.
- Founding a 'hard' company can be easier than an 'easy' one because the mission-driven nature attracts talent.
- Altman identifies AI, biotech, and energy as high-potential sectors for future innovation.

## Topics

- [[topics/hard-technology|Hard Technology]]: Startups focused on complex, long-term engineering challenges rather than purely software-based consumer applications.
- [[topics/startup-funding|Startup Funding]]: The challenges and strategies involved in securing capital for high-risk, high-reward technology ventures.
- [[topics/future-technologies|Future Technologies]]: Emerging fields like AI, biotech, and energy that offer significant opportunities for innovation.

## Notable Claims

- It is often easier to start a hard company than an easy company. Evidence: Hard companies attract more people who are motivated by the mission.
- The YC model works effectively for hard tech companies. Evidence: Three of the four companies that raised the most money from the Summer 2014 batch were hard tech companies.

## Quotes

> There has never been a better time to take a long-term view and use technology to solve major problems.
> In many ways, it’s easier to start a hard company than an easy company—more people want to join the mission.
