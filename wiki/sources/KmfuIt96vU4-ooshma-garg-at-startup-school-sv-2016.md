## Summary

Ushma Garg, founder of Gobble, shares her journey of building a food-tech startup, emphasizing the 'trough of sorrow' and the critical importance of finding a core mission beyond mere ambition to achieve product-market fit.

## Key Ideas

- Startups often follow a curve from launch to a 'trough of sorrow' before reaching the 'promised land'.
- Ambition is singular and can lead to burnout, while a shared mission sustains teams through difficult times.
- Doing things that don't scale is a necessary phase for discovering distribution and product-market fit.
- The concept of 'mise en place' (everything in its place) was the key innovation that allowed Gobble to pivot to 10-minute dinner kits.
- Persistence in fundraising pays off; investors who reject a company early may invest later once the business model is proven.
- Talking to customers is essential not just for product feedback, but for understanding the emotional 'why' behind the business.

## Notable Claims

- Most great startups follow a specific curve involving a 'trough of sorrow' and 'wiggles of false hope'. Evidence: Referenced Paul Graham's startup curve theory.
- Recruiting based on mission rather than ambition leads to higher employee retention during tough times. Evidence: Personal experience with team members staying through pivots and financial instability.
- The 'mise en place' culinary concept was the catalyst for Gobble's success. Evidence: The shift to 10-minute dinner kits led to rapid growth from zero to millions in ARR.

> Have grit in your core, have experiments in your head, and have your mission in your heart.
