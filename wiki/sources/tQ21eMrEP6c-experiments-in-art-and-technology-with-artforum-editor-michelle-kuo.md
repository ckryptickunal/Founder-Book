# Experiments in Art and Technology with Artforum Editor Michelle Kuo

## Summary

Michelle Kuo, editor of Artforum, discusses the history and legacy of 'Experiments in Art and Technology' (E.A.T.), a group founded in 1966 by Robert Rauschenberg and Billy Klüver. The organization aimed to foster collaborations between artists and engineers to explore the intersection of art, technology, and social change, often challenging traditional notions of authorship and institutional boundaries.

## Key Ideas

- E.A.T. sought to democratize access to technology for artists by pairing them with engineers from Bell Labs.
- The collaboration between artists and engineers was often tumultuous, ad-hoc, and experimental, leading to both groundbreaking performances and logistical failures.
- E.A.T. projects, such as the Pepsi Pavilion at the 1970 World's Fair, demonstrated the potential for global, large-scale collaborative art.
- The group faced criticism for its perceived complicity with the military-industrial complex during the anti-Vietnam War era.
- Modern art continues to grapple with the legacy of technological obsolescence, the role of the 'ready-made,' and the challenge of maintaining ephemeral or machine-based works.
- There is a need for a public sphere for technological knowledge, similar to the role museums play for art, to bridge the gap between specialized domains.

## Notable Claims

- E.A.T. was a precursor to the modern integration of technology in art, but faced significant backlash due to the political climate of the 1960s. Evidence: The discussion of anti-Vietnam protests and accusations of complicity with the military-industrial complex.
- The 'ready-made' concept can be updated to address technological obsolescence. Evidence: Rauschenberg's willingness to allow his transistor radio sculpture to be updated with modern digital components.
- The current art world is experiencing a shift where technology is ubiquitous but often less foregrounded as the primary 'raison d'être' of an artwork. Evidence: Observations regarding the Venice Biennale and the focus on nature, craft, and spirituality.
