# How to Raise Money

## Summary

This essay provides a strategic guide for early-stage startup founders on navigating 'phase 2' fundraising, emphasizing that fundraising is a means to an end—growth—rather than a measure of success. It outlines a disciplined, process-oriented approach to minimize distraction and maximize the probability of securing capital.

## Key Ideas

- Fundraising is a distraction; founders should either be in 'fundraising mode' or 'company-building mode,' not both.
- Treat fundraising as a breadth-first search, prioritizing investors by expected value.
- Never assume an investor is interested until they provide a definite, written commitment; treat all other signals as 'no'.
- The best way to raise money is to reach a point where you don't need it (ramen profitability).
- Valuation is secondary to securing the capital needed to reach the next milestone; don't optimize for it.
- Always have one founder handle fundraising to insulate the others and maintain company growth.

## Notable Claims

- Fundraising is a distraction that causes startup growth to drop. Evidence: Founders who focus on fundraising lose their 'top idea' focus on building the company.
- Investors often deliberately mislead founders to preserve optionality. Evidence: Investors feign interest to keep a 'free option' on a deal without committing.
- Valuation is not the most important metric in early-stage fundraising. Evidence: Highly successful companies like Dropbox and Airbnb raised at relatively low valuations.
