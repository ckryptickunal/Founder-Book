## Summary

Paul Graham provides a corrective account of Y Combinator's origins, arguing that co-founder Jessica Livingston was the central, often overlooked architect of the organization's culture, founder selection process, and alumni network.

## Key Ideas

- Jessica Livingston was a co-founder of Y Combinator and the primary architect of its culture.
- The 'Social Radar'—Jessica's ability to judge character—was the most critical factor in YC's success in picking founders.
- YC's culture was its product; it was built on authenticity and a family-like atmosphere.
- The YC alumni network's high level of trust is a direct result of Jessica's insistence on funding 'good people' over just successful ones.
- Jessica's aversion to the spotlight, dislike of conflict, and modesty have led to her being written out of YC's public history.
- The media's tendency to focus on conflict and sensationalism silences thoughtful, non-confrontational voices like Jessica's.

## Notable Claims

- Jessica Livingston's 'Social Radar' was the primary reason YC succeeded in picking successful founders. Evidence: The founders relied on her judgment during interviews to assess character, which proved to be a better predictor of success than technical questions.
- The most successful founders are almost always good people. Evidence: Observation that bad founders who do succeed tend to sell early, while long-term success correlates with high character.
- Jessica Livingston is the primary reason for the high level of trust in the YC alumni network. Evidence: Her practice of rejecting founders with questionable character ensured the network was composed of trustworthy individuals.
