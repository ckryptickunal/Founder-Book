# Masterclass: Fighting scammers online (How founders can build Trust and Safety teams)

## Summary

Garry Tan interviews Steve Kirkham and Eric Levine, former members of the Airbnb Trust and Safety team and founders of Berbix. They discuss the challenges of building trust in online marketplaces, the necessity of proactive identity verification, and how to mitigate risks from premeditated, opportunistic, and accidental bad actors.

## Key Ideas

- Trust and safety is a foundational element for any online marketplace, not an afterthought.
- Bad actors are often financially motivated and operate like businesses; the goal is to increase their cost of operation to make their ROI negative.
- Identity verification should be instant and automated to avoid user friction and 'purgatory' states.
- Enforcing uniqueness (Sybil resistance) is critical to preventing bad actors from returning to a platform.
- Founders should think about trust and safety from day one by putting themselves in the shoes of potential attackers.
- Imposter syndrome is common; founders must learn to own their impact and act without waiting for permission.

## Topics

- [[topics/trust-and-safety|Trust and Safety]]: The practice of protecting users and platforms from fraudulent or malicious activity.
- [[topics/identity-verification|Identity Verification]]: The process of confirming that an online user is the same person as their physical-world identity.
- [[topics/fraud-mitigation|Fraud Mitigation]]: Strategies to identify and stop bad actors, including account takeovers and financial fraud.
- [[topics/sybil-resistance|Sybil Resistance]]: Ensuring one-to-one relationships between virtual accounts and real-world individuals.

## Notable Claims

- Most online fraud is financially motivated and driven by actors operating like businesses. Evidence: The speakers observed that bad actors focus on maximizing ROI by extracting money from systems like Airbnb.
- Automated, instant ID verification is superior to human-in-the-loop systems. Evidence: Legacy systems relying on manual review create delays, user frustration, and are prone to human error.
- Making a platform difficult to exploit causes bad actors to move to easier targets. Evidence: The 'path of least resistance' principle; similar to how slow servers discourage trolls on Hacker News.
