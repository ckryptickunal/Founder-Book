## Summary

Patrick Collison, co-founder and CEO of Stripe, discusses the company's early development, the challenges of scaling, and the philosophy behind running a startup. He emphasizes the importance of rapid iteration, deep user engagement, and building an organization that can handle global infrastructure.

## Key Ideas

- The 'Cullison Installation' method involves founders personally visiting users to install APIs, which serves both as user research and a way to overcome customer inertia.
- Pre-product-market fit, the primary goal is rapid iteration based on high-throughput qualitative feedback rather than metrics.
- Post-product-market fit, the CEO's role shifts to building an organization capable of serving the entire market.
- Decision-making in co-founding teams should be efficient and based on passion or expertise rather than consensus, which can lead to stagnation.
- Stripe's culture encourages 'yes and' thinking, exemplified by their 'crazy ideas' process to foster innovation.
- The transition from a small, centralized team to a global organization requires moving away from consensus-based decision-making toward more explicit, hierarchical communication.

## Topics

- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The transition point where a company shifts from struggling to find users to being bottlenecked by user demand.
- [[topics/organizational-scaling|Organizational Scaling]]: The challenges of maintaining speed, agility, and clear communication as a company grows from a small team to a global entity.
- [[topics/user-feedback-loops|User Feedback Loops]]: The necessity of hyper-attentiveness to user behavior and errors to improve product quality in the early stages.

## Notable Claims

- Consensus-based decision-making is a recipe for failure in startups. Evidence: Collison notes that it is inefficient and that successful co-founding teams need mechanisms to make decisions even when they disagree.
- B2B software is easier to build than consumer software. Evidence: Businesses are more rational and know what they want, whereas consumer needs are often amorphous and difficult to predict.
- The growth curve of a startup is under the founder's control. Evidence: It is a function of the go-to-market apparatus and the organization built to serve the market, rather than a cosmic trajectory.
