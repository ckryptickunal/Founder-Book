# How to Convince Investors

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-how-to-convince-investors`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: August 2013
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-how-to-convince-investors

## Summary

Paul Graham explains that the most effective way to convince investors is not through performative pitching, but by ensuring the startup is a genuinely good bet and then clearly communicating that truth. He emphasizes that founders must first convince themselves through rigorous evaluation and domain expertise, rather than imitating the 'swagger' of experienced entrepreneurs.

## Key Ideas

- Investors treat startup success as a binary outcome due to the power law distribution of returns.
- The most important factor for investors is the perceived 'formidability' of the founders.
- Founders should avoid 'pitching' and instead focus on understanding why their startup is a good investment.
- Investors are skilled at detecting insincerity; if a founder is not convinced of their own business, they will fail to convince others.
- A startup is a bet on a market; founders must demonstrate a plausible path to dominating a large market.
- Rejection is common and should be handled by candidly addressing the concerns raised by previous investors.

## Notable Claims

- Investors decide within the first few minutes whether a founder is a winner or a loser. Evidence: Investors often check messages or lose interest quickly if the initial impression is negative.
- The best way to seem formidable is to stick to the truth. Evidence: Confidence is natural when speaking about facts one knows to be true.
- Investors view startups as markets, not just ideas. Evidence: Investors calculate TAM (Total Addressable Market) to determine the upper bound of potential success.

## Quotes

> The foundation of convincing investors is to seem formidable.
> Convince yourself that your startup is worth investing in, and then when you explain this to investors they'll believe you.
> The time to raise money is not when you need it, or when you reach some artificial deadline like a Demo Day. It's when you can convince investors, and not before.
> Make the truth good, then just tell it.
