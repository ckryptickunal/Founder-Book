# Save Your Startup During an Economic Downturn

## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell discuss the concept of 'default alive' versus 'default dead' for startups, a framework created by Paul Graham. They emphasize that founders must be honest about their financial sustainability, arguing that being 'default alive'—where revenue growth ensures profitability before cash runs out—is essential for maintaining control and leverage, especially during economic downturns.

## Key Ideas

- Default alive means your growth rate is high enough to reach profitability before your cash runs out, without needing further investment.
- Default dead means you will run out of money if you do not raise more capital.
- Founders often avoid this math because it is uncomfortable, they feel pressure to appear confident, or they assume future fundraising will be easy.
- Investors and founders have misaligned incentives; investors may prefer high-risk, high-burn strategies that lead to either massive success or total failure.
- Taking the 'L' (cutting costs, reducing ad spend, or raising prices) to reach sustainability is a strategic move that allows a company to survive and pivot.
- Founders are the pilots of their companies; they must resist the urge to 'blitz scale' based on external pressure or market hype.

## Notable Claims

- Most fundraising rounds fail, but the media only highlights the successful ones, creating a false perception of ease. Evidence: Anecdotal evidence from YC's internal data and observation of market trends.
- A company that is hemorrhaging cash is often worth less than nothing to an acquirer due to liabilities. Evidence: The example of the company 'Fast' being acquired for zero dollars.
- Founders often wait until it is too late to seek advice on financial sustainability. Evidence: Observations from YC office hours where founders ask for help only when they are weeks away from bankruptcy.
