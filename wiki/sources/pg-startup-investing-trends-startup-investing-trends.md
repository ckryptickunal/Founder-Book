# Startup Investing Trends

## Summary

Paul Graham analyzes the evolving landscape of startup investing, noting that startups are becoming cheaper to launch and more socially acceptable, which shifts power toward founders. He argues that while investors may face lower equity percentages and less control, the increase in the number of high-quality startups and the potential for faster, more efficient investment models will create new opportunities for those who adapt to founder needs.

## Key Ideas

- The cost of starting a startup is decreasing, making entrepreneurship a viable career path alongside traditional employment.
- Founders are gaining leverage over investors, leading to lower equity dilution and more founder control.
- The number of 'big hit' startups is not a fixed constant but is limited by the number of good founders, which is increasing.
- Investors should prioritize speed and founder-friendly terms to gain access to the best deals.
- The VC model of forcing large, unnecessary Series A investments is becoming obsolete and creates an opportunity for firms that offer more flexible terms.
- The startup ecosystem is shifting from an 'obelisk' shape to a 'pyramid' with a much wider base of early-stage activity.

## Notable Claims

- The number of successful startups per year is not a fixed constant. Evidence: The limiting factor is the number of good founders, which is increasing as starting a company becomes more accessible.
- Investors make more money as 'founders' bitches' than as their bosses. Evidence: Empirical evidence suggests that founders who retain control are more effective, and micromanagement is a poor use of investor time.
- The current Series A funding model is broken. Evidence: VCs often force companies to take more money than they need to justify board seats and ownership percentages, creating misalignment.
