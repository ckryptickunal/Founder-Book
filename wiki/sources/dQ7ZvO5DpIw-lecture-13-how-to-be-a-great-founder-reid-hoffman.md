## Summary

Reid Hoffman, co-founder of LinkedIn and partner at Greylock, discusses the realities of being a great founder, debunking the 'superperson' myth and emphasizing the importance of team composition, strategic location, contrarian thinking, and navigating paradoxes.

## Key Ideas

- Great founders are not 'superhumans' with every skill; they are individuals who build teams to compensate for their weaknesses.
- Founding teams of two or three are generally superior to solo founders because they offer diverse skill sets and mutual support.
- Location matters because founders must seek out the specific networks essential to their problem, not just move to Silicon Valley by default.
- Contrarianism is only valuable if it is both contrarian and correct; founders must know something others do not.
- Founders must navigate paradoxes, such as being both persistent and flexible, and balancing long-term vision with short-term execution.
- An investment thesis is a critical tool for minimizing risk and determining when to pivot based on increasing or decreasing confidence.
- Product distribution is often more fundamental to success than the product itself.

## Topics

- [[topics/founder-mindset|Founder Mindset]]: The psychological and strategic approach required to lead a startup through uncertainty.
- [[topics/investment-thesis|Investment Thesis]]: A framework for articulating why a startup will succeed and using it to measure progress and risk.
- [[topics/startup-paradoxes|Startup Paradoxes]]: The necessity of balancing opposing forces like persistence vs. flexibility and internal focus vs. external networking.
- [[topics/network-effects|Network Effects]]: The importance of building and leveraging networks for talent, distribution, and growth.

## Notable Claims

- Most successful startups have two or three co-founders rather than one. Evidence: Observation of successful ventures and the need for diverse skill sets to handle broad challenges.
- Silicon Valley is not the optimal location for every type of startup. Evidence: Groupon's success in Chicago due to its specific need for a massive sales force, which was not supported by Silicon Valley's culture.
- Founders should not seek 'balance' in their lives. Evidence: The extreme difficulty of building a startup requires total commitment to winning, at least for a period of time.

## Quotes

> If you're confidence is unmeasured for a fairly long time or is decreasing... that's a seriously good time to think about pivoting.
