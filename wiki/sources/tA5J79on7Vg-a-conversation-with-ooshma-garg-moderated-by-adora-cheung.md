## Summary

Ooshma Garg, founder and CEO of Gobble, discusses her entrepreneurial journey, the evolution of her business models, and the grit required to navigate the challenges of building a startup. She details the transition from a student-led recruiting platform to a successful 15-minute dinner kit service, emphasizing the importance of iteration, customer-centricity, and resilience.

## Key Ideas

- Product-market fit is a summation of many small experiments and iterations rather than a single 'aha' moment.
- Founders must be willing to kill profitable business lines if they do not align with the long-term vision.
- Grit and determination are essential, often requiring founders to ignore external skepticism and trust their gut.
- Innovation often happens at the intersection of diverse industries; bringing in domain experts (like professional chefs) is crucial for product quality.
- Fundraising is consistently difficult and requires a compelling, high-level vision rather than just a promise of safety or return on investment.
- Building a support network of other founders is vital for solo entrepreneurs to manage the loneliness of the early stages.

- [[topics/business-iteration|Business Iteration]]: The process of evolving a business model from a marketplace to a catering service, then to a personalized dinner service, and finally to meal kits.
- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The intersection of growth potential and high customer retention.
- [[topics/fundraising|Fundraising]]: Strategies for pitching to investors and maintaining conviction during difficult market conditions.

## Notable Claims

- Whoever pays you owns you and determines how your product evolves. Evidence: Ooshma's experience with her first recruiting company where banks dictated hiring criteria.
- The most successful overnight successes are actually ten years in the making. Evidence: The long, iterative process required to reach the current success of Gobble.
- Investors do not care about you just getting their money back. Evidence: Investors are looking for massive, multi-billion dollar potential, not risk-averse protection.

## Quotes

> Product market fit is not just an aha moment; it's a summation of lots of broad experiments and micro learnings over many years.
> I have found that I cannot listen to anything; there's nothing more loud than my gut.
> If you want to change the world or change your industry, you have to do something that is materially better—like iPhone level better—than the market.
> I'm always enamored by these real overnight successes. I think the most overnight successes are like ten years in the making.
