# Schlep Blindness

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-schlep-blindness`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: January 2012
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-schlep-blindness

## Summary

Paul Graham defines 'schlep blindness' as the unconscious tendency of founders to avoid startup ideas that require tedious, unpleasant, or difficult tasks (schleps), leading them to pursue trivial projects instead of solving high-value, complex problems.

## Key Ideas

- Schleps are inevitable in business and often define the value of a company.
- Schlep blindness is an unconscious filter that prevents founders from even considering difficult but necessary business ideas.
- Ambitious ideas involving difficult tasks are undervalued because they face less competition from founders who are intimidated by the work.
- Ignorance of future obstacles can be an advantage for young founders, allowing them to start projects they might otherwise avoid.
- To overcome schlep blindness, founders should ask, 'What problem do I wish someone else would solve for me?'

## Notable Claims

- Ambitious ideas are less competitive because they are intimidating. Evidence: Founders naturally gravitate toward easier, less impactful projects like recipe sites rather than complex infrastructure problems.
- Younger founders have an advantage in starting difficult companies. Evidence: They are often ignorant of the scale of the challenges they will face, which allows them to start before they realize how hard it will be.

## Quotes

> A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake.
> The most dangerous thing about our dislike of schleps is that much of it is unconscious.
> Instead of asking 'what problem should I solve?' ask 'what problem do I wish someone else would solve for me?'
