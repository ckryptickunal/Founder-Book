# Copy What You Like

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-copy-what-you-like`

## Summary

Paul Graham reflects on his history of imitating the wrong influences in literature, philosophy, and computer science, arguing that one should ignore external prestige and instead copy only what they genuinely enjoy to develop authentic taste and work.

## Key Ideas

- Imitating prestigious but uninteresting work is a common trap for ambitious people.
- The distinction between what you are impressed by and what you actually like is crucial.
- Presentation and social status often mask a lack of substance in academic and artistic fields.
- Guilty pleasures are often more indicative of genuine taste than 'virtuous' reading or study.
- Imitators often copy the flaws of their idols (like the brown varnish on old paintings) rather than the core quality.
- One must develop personal taste independently of authorities, who often prioritize status over quality.

## Notable Claims

- Philosophy papers in the author's experience were often empty of definite meaning. Evidence: The author notes that no philosopher ever refuted another because the claims were too vague to be falsifiable.
- Imitators often copy the flaws of masters rather than their virtues. Evidence: Painters in the 18th and 19th centuries imitated the brown tones of Renaissance masters, not realizing the color was caused by dirt and aging varnish.
