## Summary

Apoorva Mehta, founder and CEO of Instacart, shares his entrepreneurial journey at Y Combinator's Startup School 2014. He details his transition from a logistics engineer at Amazon to a startup founder, highlighting the importance of solving problems one truly cares about, the necessity of resilience through repeated failure, and the value of 'doing things that don't scale' in the early stages of a business.

## Key Ideas

- The motivation for starting a company should be to solve a problem you personally care about, not just to be a founder.
- Early-stage startups have a competitive advantage by doing things that don't scale, which larger companies cannot replicate.
- Resilience is critical; founders must be able to move from failure to failure without losing momentum.
- Validating a product through direct customer feedback and iteration is more important than perfect planning.
- Complex logistics problems can be solved through software without owning physical infrastructure like warehouses or trucks.

## Notable Claims

- Instacart is a software company that does not own warehouses, trucks, or inventory. Evidence: Mehta explains that they utilize a network of personal shoppers to pick up items from existing retail stores.
- Doing things that don't scale is a competitive advantage for startups. Evidence: Mehta cites examples like manually fulfilling orders himself and taking photos of every item at Trader Joe's to build their initial catalog.

## Quotes

> The reason to start a company should never be to start a company. The reason to start a company should be to solve a problem that you truly, truly care about.
> You have to go from failure to failure without losing any steam, because the next step, the next product, the next iteration that you build could make the difference.
> I find that this is one of the biggest competitive advantages that a start-up has over a larger company because there's no way that the larger company would be doing those things unscalably.
