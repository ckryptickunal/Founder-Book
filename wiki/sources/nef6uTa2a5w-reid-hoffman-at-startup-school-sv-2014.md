## Summary

Reid Hoffman discusses the founding of LinkedIn, his investment philosophy, and common myths in Silicon Valley entrepreneurship during a Y Combinator Startup School session.

## Key Ideas

- Self-delusion is a necessary trait for entrepreneurs, but it must be balanced with constant reality testing.
- The best time to start a company is often during a downturn when talent is more accessible and competition is lower.
- Raising too much capital early can lead to operational inefficiency and a lack of focus.
- Network effects are not inherent to all networks; they require superlinear value growth as nodes are added.
- Founders should view government as a platform and an essential part of the ecosystem rather than just an obstacle.
- The 'address book upload' feature was the critical growth hack that scaled LinkedIn.

## Notable Claims

- Open capital markets are not always ideal for entrepreneurs. Evidence: Increased capital leads to more competition for talent and market relevance, making it harder to break through the noise.
- Raising too much capital in early rounds is often detrimental. Evidence: It reduces the sharpness of decision-making and allows teams to become complacent when metrics don't improve.
- Government can be a positive force for entrepreneurship. Evidence: Government provides the legal, educational, and infrastructure 'aquarium' that allows businesses to operate.
