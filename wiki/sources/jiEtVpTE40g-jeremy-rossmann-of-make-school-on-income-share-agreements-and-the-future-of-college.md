# Jeremy Rossmann of Make School on Income Share Agreements and the Future of College

## Summary

Jeremy Rossmann, co-founder of Make School, discusses the institution's innovative model for higher education, which utilizes project-based learning, accelerated timelines, and Income Share Agreements (ISAs) to align college incentives with student career success.

## Key Ideas

- Make School structures the academic week like a professional work week to prepare students for industry.
- Income Share Agreements (ISAs) shift the financial risk from the student to the institution, incentivizing the school to ensure student employability.
- Traditional liberal arts education is often a 'luxury good' for the elite, whereas career-focused education is essential for low-to-mid income students.
- Most students are not natural autodidacts; they require structured mentorship and curriculum to succeed in complex fields like computer science.
- The 'degree' as a requirement is often a barrier to equity, and Make School aims to provide an accredited path that combines theoretical CS with practical, project-based skills.

## Topics

- [[topics/income-share-agreements-isas|Income Share Agreements (ISAs)]]: A financing model where students pay tuition only after securing a job, aligning school and student incentives.
- [[topics/project-based-learning|Project-Based Learning]]: An educational approach where students learn by building real-world products rather than through traditional lectures and problem sets.
- [[topics/higher-education-reform|Higher Education Reform]]: The movement to make college more affordable, relevant, and outcome-oriented in the face of the student debt crisis.
- [[topics/autodidacticism|Autodidacticism]]: The ability to teach oneself; the guest argues that relying on this is a form of survivorship bias in the tech industry.

## Notable Claims

- The majority of students are not autodidacts and require structure to succeed in software engineering. Evidence: Low completion rates in MOOCs and the guest's personal experience with needing mentorship to deploy code.
- Elite universities primarily serve the top 20% of income earners. Evidence: Statistical data regarding the socioeconomic background of students at schools like Stanford and MIT.
- Income Share Agreements are becoming a standard tool for higher education to align incentives. Evidence: Purdue University and other institutions adopting ISA models based on early documentation.
