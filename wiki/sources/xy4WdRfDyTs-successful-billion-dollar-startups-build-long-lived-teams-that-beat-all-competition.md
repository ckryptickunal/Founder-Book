# Successful billion dollar startups build long-lived teams that beat all competition

## Metadata

- Video ID: `xy4WdRfDyTs`
- Channel: Garry Tan

## Summary

Garry Tan explains that building long-lived, high-trust teams is the primary driver for startup success, drawing parallels between professional sports championships and the necessity of founder cohesion during periods of adversity.

## Key Ideas

- Long-lived teams are essential for winning in business, similar to how pro sports teams require years to maximize championship odds.
- Founders need to have a history or deep relationship before starting a company to survive inevitable startup crises.
- Trust is the foundation of a high-performing culture, described as a 'seamless web of deserved trust'.
- 'Givers' who prioritize the group over personal interests are more likely to build sustainable, successful organizations than 'takers'.
- Organizational alignment is a competitive advantage because most companies suffer from internal conflict and lack of direction.

## Notable Claims

- It takes an average of four and a half years for a pro-level basketball team to maximize their odds of winning a championship. Evidence: Cited as a benchmark for the time required to build high-performing, cohesive teams.
- The best way to find trustworthy people is to be trustworthy yourself. Evidence: Based on the principle that 'like attracts like' in organizational culture.
- Most organizations fail because they are not rowing in the same direction. Evidence: Observation of internal conflict and lack of alignment in the average corporate environment.

## Quotes

> The right culture, the highest and best culture is a seamless web of deserved trust.
> If you could get all the people in an organization rowing in the same direction, you could dominate any industry in any market against any competition at any time.
> Integrity means standing for something meaningful beyond oneself rather than being motivated by narrow personal interests.
