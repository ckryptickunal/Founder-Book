## Summary

Bob McGrew, former Chief Research Officer at OpenAI, discusses the evolution of AI from early research projects like Dota 2 and robotics to the current era of reasoning models and AGI. He highlights the shift from pre-training scaling to test-time compute and reasoning, the importance of 'forward-deployed' engineering in AI adoption, and the future role of humans as managers of AI-driven systems.

## Key Ideas

- The transition from pre-training scaling to reasoning and test-time compute is the new mechanism for unlocking AGI.
- Scaling laws are now being applied to robotics, which is currently at a stage similar to where LLMs were five years ago.
- AI adoption is currently bottlenecked by a lack of specialized software (UI/UX) that bridges the gap between raw intelligence and specific user workflows.
- The future of work will likely bifurcate into two roles: the 'lone genius' leveraging AI and the 'manager' overseeing AI-driven firms.
- Distillation is becoming a critical strategy for startups to achieve high performance at lower costs after initial development on frontier models.

## Topics

- [[topics/scaling-laws|Scaling Laws]]: The principle that increasing compute, data, and model size leads to predictable performance improvements.
- [[topics/reasoning-models|Reasoning Models]]: Models like o1/o3 that use test-time compute to 'think' through problems, enabling higher reliability.
- [[topics/forward-deployed-engineering|Forward-Deployed Engineering]]: The practice of embedding engineers with customers to build highly specific, usable software solutions.
- [[topics/robotics|Robotics]]: The next frontier for AI, currently in the 'zero-to-one' phase of development.

## Notable Claims

- The 'data wall' for pre-training is being bypassed by reasoning and test-time compute. Evidence: The success of models like o1 and o3 in using more compute at inference time to improve performance.
- Robotics will have its 'ChatGPT moment' within the next five years. Evidence: The application of foundation models to physical environments and the progress of companies like Physical Intelligence.
- AI adoption is slow because of a lack of specialized software, not a lack of intelligence. Evidence: Comparison to Palantir's success in government by building software that solves specific user workflows rather than just providing raw data.
