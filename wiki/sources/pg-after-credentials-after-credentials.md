# After Credentials

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-after-credentials`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: December 2008
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-after-credentials

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the shift from a credential-based society to a performance-based one, arguing that the decline of large, monolithic organizations in favor of smaller, market-driven entities allows for direct measurement of individual performance, thereby reducing the influence of legacy and academic pedigree.

## Key Ideas

- Credentials were an historical advancement over nepotism and bribery but are inherently flawed and 'hackable' via cram schools.
- The primary purpose of credentials is to predict performance in large organizations where individual output is difficult to measure.
- Small organizations and startups naturally prioritize performance over credentials because they cannot afford to carry non-performers.
- The rise of startups and the shift toward paying market rates for young talent have accelerated the decline of seniority-based compensation.
- A society composed of many small, competitive organizations is more meritocratic than one dominated by a few large, credential-obsessed institutions.

## Topics

- [[topics/credentialism|Credentialism]]: The reliance on academic or professional degrees as the primary indicator of a person's value or future performance.
- [[topics/performance-based-economy|Performance-based Economy]]: An economic model where individuals are rewarded based on their actual output rather than their pedigree or tenure.
- [[topics/intergenerational-power-transmission|Intergenerational Power Transmission]]: The tendency for wealthy or powerful parents to use their influence to secure advantages for their children, often through elite credentials.

## Notable Claims

- Societies prosper when they prevent parents from directly influencing their children's success. Evidence: Societies that prioritize meritocratic performance over inherited status ensure the 'best man for the job' is selected.
- Small organizations are better at measuring performance than large ones. Evidence: In small companies, poor performance leads to business failure, forcing a focus on actual output rather than credentials.

## Quotes
