# Hans Tung funded 16 unicorns: Billion dollar war stories and hard lessons learned

## Summary

Garry Tan interviews Hans Tung, a prominent venture capitalist at GGV Capital, about his career, investment philosophy, and the universal principles of building successful companies. They discuss lessons from historical crises, the importance of founder-market fit, and how global trends in technology, particularly in China, influence innovation worldwide.

## Key Ideas

- Successful founders possess deep systematic thinking, the ability to learn and adjust quickly, strong leadership, and objective decision-making.
- Crisis periods often force companies to adopt more sustainable business models and clearer missions.
- Many principles of building successful companies are universal, regardless of geography.
- Innovation often moves from the US to China and back, with China leading in highly dense, fast-urbanizing consumer internet applications.
- Software is increasingly moving from a tool to an essential armament for traditional industries.
- Founders should focus on building something deeply loved by a small group of people rather than being liked by a mass audience initially.
- Patience is crucial; success is a long-term game of learning from mistakes and finding one's own path.

## Notable Claims

- The best founders build something that is absolutely loved by 100 people rather than being liked by a million. Evidence: Attributed to Brian Chesky; emphasizes the importance of early evangelists for organic growth.
- Food delivery in China is profitable, unlike in many other regions. Evidence: Hans Tung notes that high density and lack of government subsidies forced Meituan to develop a sustainable model.
- 5G will enable new growth opportunities for startups, particularly in AR/VR and autonomous systems. Evidence: Increased computing power and broadband availability will shift how software interacts with the physical world.
