## Summary

This lecture from Y Combinator's 'How to Start a Startup' series features founders Stanley Tang (DoorDash), Walker Williams (Teespring), and Justin Kan (Justin.tv/Twitch) discussing the importance of 'doing things that don't scale' in the early stages of a startup, followed by a tactical guide on managing public relations.

## Key Ideas

- Early-stage startups should prioritize manual, non-scalable processes to gain deep customer insights and validate product-market fit.
- Founders should launch quickly with a 'hacky' MVP rather than waiting for a perfect, scalable infrastructure.
- Customer service and direct interaction are critical for learning what to build and turning early users into brand champions.
- Press coverage is a sales funnel; it requires a clear business goal, warm introductions, and a compelling, novel story.
- Avoid hiring expensive PR firms in the early days; founders should learn to pitch their own stories to build relationships.

## Topics

- [[topics/doing-things-that-don-t-scale|Doing Things That Don't Scale]]: The strategy of performing manual tasks to acquire initial users and understand business operations before automating.
- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The process of iterating quickly based on user feedback to reach a product that satisfies a clear market need.
- [[topics/public-relations|Public Relations]]: Tactical advice on how to pitch stories to journalists, build relationships, and use press to achieve specific business goals.

## Notable Claims

- Doing things that don't scale is a competitive advantage for early-stage startups. Evidence: Founders gain intimate knowledge of customer pain points and operational bottlenecks that automated systems would obscure.
- Press coverage is not a scalable user acquisition strategy. Evidence: News is inherently temporary; once the novelty fades, coverage stops, making it a tool for initial traction rather than long-term growth.
- The product you launch with will not be the product you scale with. Evidence: Early feedback loops force pivots and feature changes that align the product with actual market demands.
