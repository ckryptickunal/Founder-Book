# Write Simply

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that simple, clear writing is superior because it minimizes cognitive friction for the reader, allowing them to focus entirely on the ideas rather than the prose. He posits that complex writing is often used to mask a lack of substance and that simplicity is a sign of craftsmanship that ensures longevity.

## Key Ideas

- Simple writing reduces reader energy expenditure, increasing engagement and completion rates.
- The goal of writing should be 'saltintesta'—where ideas transfer directly into the reader's mind with minimal notice of the words.
- Complex writing is often a selfish act intended to impress, which burdens the reader.
- Simple writing is more inclusive for non-native speakers.
- Complexity is frequently used to hide a lack of original thought.
- Editing, particularly cutting, is the primary mechanism for achieving simplicity.

## Notable Claims

- Fancy writing is often used to conceal a lack of ideas. Evidence: The author notes that simple writing keeps the writer honest, as it makes the absence of substance immediately obvious.
- Simple writing has better longevity. Evidence: Future readers will face language and cultural shifts; simple language remains accessible across time, similar to well-crafted furniture.

## Quotes

> The less energy they expend on your prose, the more they'll have left for your ideas.
> My goal when writing might be called saltintesta: the ideas leap into your head and you barely notice the words that got them there.
> When you write in a fancy way to impress people, you're making them do extra work just so you can seem cool.
> The main reason I write simply is that it offends me not to.
