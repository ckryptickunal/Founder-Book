# How to Run a User Interview with Emmett Shear (How to Start a Startup 2014: Lecture 16)

## Summary

Emmett Shear, co-founder of Twitch, explains the critical importance of conducting user interviews to identify real problems rather than just building features. He emphasizes that startups often fail by building for themselves or by asking users for feature requests, which leads to 'faster horses' rather than innovative solutions. The lecture covers how to find the right users, how to conduct effective interviews, and how to synthesize feedback to drive product strategy.

## Key Ideas

- Don't build for yourself unless you are the target market; most startups need to solve problems for others.
- Avoid asking users for feature requests; instead, ask about their current problems and habits.
- Who you talk to is as important as what you ask; segment your audience into current users, competitor users, and non-users.
- The 'Horseless Carriage' effect: users often ask for incremental improvements to existing workflows rather than transformative solutions.
- Record interviews to allow for deep listening and to share insights with the rest of the team.
- The most valuable insights often come from non-users who are blocked from using your product due to specific pain points.
- User research is an ongoing process; the target audience for a startup will shift as the company grows.

## Topics

- [[topics/user-research|User Research]]: The methodology of interviewing potential or current users to identify pain points and market needs.
- [[topics/product-strategy|Product Strategy]]: Using qualitative data from interviews to inform long-term feature development and market expansion.
- [[topics/customer-segmentation|Customer Segmentation]]: Categorizing users into current, competitor, and non-user groups to gain diverse perspectives.
- [[topics/the-pivot|The Pivot]]: Changing a product's direction based on insights gained from talking to users, as seen in the transition from Justin TV to Twitch.

## Notable Claims

- Asking users 'is this feature good?' is a trap. Evidence: Users will often say yes to be polite, but that does not mean they will actually use or pay for the feature.
- The best way to validate a product idea is to get users to pay for it. Evidence: The 'money test' forces users to demonstrate genuine interest rather than just expressing theoretical approval.
- Non-users are the most important group for market expansion. Evidence: They represent the majority of the potential market and their feedback reveals the blockers preventing adoption.

## Quotes

> Who you talk to is as important as what questions you ask.
> You're going to get the 'Horseless Carriage' effect where you're getting asked for a faster horse instead of trying to design the actual real solution to the problem.
> If you just talk to who's easy to talk to, you're not really getting the best data.
> The people who get you started... are not who will be using it 3 years later.
