# Two Kinds of Judgement

## Metadata

## Summary

Paul Graham distinguishes between two types of judgment: those where accuracy is the goal (like court cases or grades) and those where judgment is merely a means to an end (like hiring or college admissions). He argues that understanding the latter helps individuals take rejection less personally and become more proactive in influencing outcomes.

## Key Ideas

- People mistakenly treat all judgments as if they are intended to be accurate assessments of their character or ability.
- Judgments in competitive selection processes are about building an optimal group, not about evaluating the individual.
- The 'selector' in a process is more like a customer choosing a product than a judge presiding over a case.
- Recognizing the impersonal nature of selection processes allows individuals to stop being passive and start actively influencing the outcome.
- Self-centeredness and early childhood conditioning lead people to believe that every judgment is about them.

## Topics

- [[topics/psychology-of-judgment|Psychology of Judgment]]: The cognitive bias that leads individuals to perceive all external evaluations as personal assessments.
- [[topics/selection-processes|Selection Processes]]: The mechanics of how institutions like colleges and companies select individuals for a group, often prioritizing efficiency over absolute accuracy.

## Notable Claims

- Most judgments in life are not about the individual being judged. Evidence: Selection processes like hiring or college admissions are designed to build an optimal set, not to provide an accurate assessment of every applicant.
- Taking rejection less personally can lead to being rejected less often. Evidence: Realizing that selectors are not 'wise magistrates' but rather 'fickle customers' encourages applicants to actively sell themselves rather than remaining passive.

## Quotes
