## Summary

Ammon Bartram, co-founder of Triplebyte, discusses the complexities of engineering hiring, emphasizing that most traditional interview processes are plagued by noise and inconsistency. He advocates for standardized, data-driven evaluation methods that prioritize actual problem-solving skills over credentials or subjective 'brain teasers'.

## Key Ideas

- Hiring processes are often noisy and inconsistent; companies should define exactly what they are looking for rather than relying on generic 'good engineer' metrics.
- Interviews should be standardized to reduce bias and noise, ensuring every candidate is evaluated on the same criteria.
- There is a tension between measuring 'small problem' solving (interviews) and 'large project' execution (real-world work).
- Experience and credentials (degrees, bootcamps) are often used as crude filters for screening, but they are poor predictors of actual job performance.
- The best interview questions are often simple, practical tasks rather than complex, math-heavy brain teasers.
- Interviewing should not be a form of hazing; interviewers should switch to a 'teaching mode' if a candidate is clearly failing to maintain a positive experience.

## Notable Claims

- There is no correlation between an engineer's performance in an interview and their actual job performance at a different company. Evidence: Triplebyte's data shows that inter-rater agreement between companies is equivalent to the agreement between film critics reviewing movies.
- Simple programming tasks are better predictors of skill than complex brain teasers. Evidence: Data shows that 'easy' problems have a lower false-negative rate for identifying capable engineers.
- Seniority is often used as a proxy for architectural skill, but it makes passing interviews easier regardless of actual performance. Evidence: Experienced engineers often pass interviews with lower performance scores than junior candidates due to resume bias.
