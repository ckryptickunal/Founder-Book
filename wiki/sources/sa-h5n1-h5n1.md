## Summary

Sam Altman discusses the human tendency to miscalculate risk, favoring dramatic but unlikely threats over mundane but probable ones, while highlighting the existential danger posed by advancements in biotechnology, specifically the potential for engineered pathogens.

## Key Ideas

- Humans are evolutionarily wired to fear dramatic, rare events rather than common, lethal ones.
- Biotechnology represents a significant existential risk that is currently underestimated.
- The democratization of biotech tools allows for the creation of dangerous pathogens outside of state-controlled environments.
- Proactive defense against bio-attacks is a critical priority for global security.
- The societal impact of 'hacking biology' will likely exceed the impact of the computer revolution.

## Topics

- [[topics/risk-assessment|Risk Assessment]]: The psychological bias that leads humans to fear rare events like terrorism over common causes of death like heart disease.
- [[topics/biosecurity|Biosecurity]]: The risks associated with the accessibility of biotechnology and the potential for engineered viruses to cause global catastrophes.
- [[topics/technological-singularity|Technological Singularity]]: The comparison between the software revolution and the coming 'biology revolution' where hacking bodies becomes more impactful than hacking bits.

## Notable Claims

- Humans are 35,000 times more likely to die from heart disease than a terrorist attack. Evidence: Comparison of 2011 mortality data for heart disease (600,000) versus terrorism (17).
- Biotechnology is becoming accessible enough to be developed in private settings, unlike nuclear weapons. Evidence: Contrast between the resource-heavy requirements of uranium enrichment and the private funding of biotech development.

## Quotes

> We seem to be wired to overweight the risk of the dramatic, scary, but very unlikely and underweight the risk of the mundane, familiar, and probable.
> As we begin to be able to create biology programs in garages, we should remember that bigger changes are likely coming--hacking our bodies will likely be more powerful than hacking bits.
