# Alien Truth

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the concept of 'alien truth'—a heuristic for identifying universal principles that intelligent beings, regardless of their origin, would likely share. He suggests that beyond the objective constants of math and physics, there exist logical and philosophical truths that serve as a target for human inquiry and a potential definition for the true purpose of philosophy.

## Key Ideas

- Intelligent life across the universe likely shares fundamental truths beyond just math and physics.
- The concept of 'alien truth' serves as a heuristic to identify the most general and universal ideas.
- Principles like Occam's razor, the value of practice, and the scientific method are likely universal.
- The search for alien truth provides a potential definition for the field of philosophy.
- Artificial Intelligence may eventually help us define the threshold of what constitutes universal intelligent thought.
- The idea of alien truth is an extension of Paul Erdos's concept of 'God's book'.

## Notable Claims

- The search for alien truth is a valid and perhaps ideal definition of philosophy. Evidence: Graham argues that if philosophy is to have a core purpose, it should be the pursuit of truths that are not merely human-centric.
- The idea that the search for alien truth is a worthwhile undertaking is itself likely an alien truth. Evidence: Graham posits this as a self-referential meta-truth that would be recognized by any sufficiently intelligent entity.

## Quotes

> If there were intelligent beings elsewhere in the universe, they'd share certain truths in common with us.
> The point of the idea of alien truth is not that it gives us a way to speculate about what forms intelligent life might take, but that it gives us a threshold, or more precisely a target, for truth.
> Whatever we call it, the attempt to discover alien truths would be a worthwhile undertaking. And curiously enough, that is itself probably an alien truth.
