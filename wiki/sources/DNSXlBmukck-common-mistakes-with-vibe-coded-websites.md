# Common Mistakes With Vibe Coded Websites

## Metadata

## Summary

In this Y Combinator design review, host and guest Raphael Shad analyze several startup websites built using AI-assisted coding tools. They discuss the 'vibe coding' phenomenon, where AI-generated designs often result in repetitive, unoriginal patterns—such as ubiquitous purple gradients, distracting scroll-jacking, and unnecessary hover effects—that can harm user experience and brand identity.

## Key Ideas

- AI design tools make it easy to build professional-looking sites, but often lead to 'AI design slop' where sites lack originality.
- Founders should act as editors, not just passive recipients of AI-generated code.
- Common AI-driven anti-patterns include: excessive purple gradients, scroll-jacking, distracting hover effects, and hidden functionality that is difficult to discover.
- A landing page is a customer acquisition channel; design choices should prioritize clarity, conversion, and brand identity over 'cool' but useless animations.
- Designers must be intentional about brand colors and hierarchy rather than accepting default AI outputs.
- Mobile usability is often overlooked in AI-generated designs, particularly regarding hover-based interactions.

## Topics

- [[topics/vibe-coding|Vibe Coding]]: The practice of using AI to generate code and design elements rapidly, often without deep technical or design scrutiny.
- [[topics/ai-design-slop|AI Design Slop]]: The proliferation of generic, repetitive design patterns (like purple gradients) caused by LLMs being trained on similar, popular web design examples.
- [[topics/user-experience-ux-anti-patterns|User Experience (UX) Anti-patterns]]: Design choices like scroll-jacking, hidden hover-based navigation, and inconsistent button styles that frustrate users.
- [[topics/information-hierarchy|Information Hierarchy]]: The importance of clear messaging and layout that guides users to understand the product's value proposition immediately.

## Notable Claims

- AI-generated websites often suffer from 'hallucinations' in design, such as inconsistent button styles or broken navigation. Evidence: Observed during the review of the Sphinx website where buttons shifted and reoriented unpredictably.
- Scroll-jacking and forced animations often distract users from the core value proposition of a landing page. Evidence: Observed during the review of Get Crocs and Zarna, where users were forced to wait for animations or fight against hijacked scrolling.
- Hover effects are increasingly used as an anti-pattern to hide critical information, which is inaccessible on mobile devices. Evidence: Discussion regarding the trend of hiding functionality behind hover states, which lacks a direct equivalent on mobile.
