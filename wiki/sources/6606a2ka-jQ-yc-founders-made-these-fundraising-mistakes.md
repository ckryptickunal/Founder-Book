# YC Founders Made These Fundraising Mistakes

## Summary

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell of Y Combinator discuss common fundraising mistakes made by early-stage founders, emphasizing the importance of customer-driven growth over investor validation and the strategic benefits of maintaining leverage by not being desperate for capital.

## Key Ideas

- Fundraising is significantly easier and more favorable when you have growing metrics.
- Founders often use fundraising as a way to avoid the fear of building a product that customers don't want.
- Investors should not be treated as teachers or authority figures; the customer is the only entity that matters.
- Founders should aim to spend 80-90% of their time talking to customers and building product.
- Raising too much money can be detrimental; revenue is the true 'oxygen' for a company.
- Founders who maintain control and ownership at exit are typically those who were never desperate for cash in the early stages.
- Founders should emulate the strategies of multi-billion dollar companies rather than local peers or recent unicorn valuations.

## Topics

- [[topics/fundraising-strategy|Fundraising Strategy]]: The timing and necessity of raising capital based on metrics and leverage.
- [[topics/customer-obsession|Customer Obsession]]: The practice of prioritizing customer feedback and problem-solving over investor approval.
- [[topics/founder-psychology|Founder Psychology]]: Overcoming the fear of failure and the tendency to seek validation from authority figures.

## Notable Claims

- Raising money before having metrics is often a form of fear-based decision-making. Evidence: Founders who suspect their product is bad try to raise money before the market confirms their failure.
- Founders who own more of their company at exit were never desperate for cash. Evidence: Historical examples like Google and Facebook show that early profitability and traction provided the leverage to avoid bad fundraising terms.
