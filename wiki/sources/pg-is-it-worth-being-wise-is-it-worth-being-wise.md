# Is It Worth Being Wise?

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the distinction between wisdom and intelligence, proposing that they represent different performance curves: wisdom is a high average outcome across many situations, while intelligence is characterized by high peaks of performance in specific, often complex, scenarios. He argues that modern society increasingly favors intelligence due to the rise of specialized, creative work, and that the two traits often require conflicting developmental paths.

## Key Ideas

- Wisdom is defined as a high average of success across many situations, whereas intelligence is defined by high-performance spikes in specific, difficult tasks.
- The distinction between the two is becoming sharper as knowledge becomes more specialized and the number of situations (points on the curve) increases.
- Wisdom is often associated with remedial qualities like self-discipline and experience, while intelligence is cultivated through curiosity and working on hard, idiosyncratic problems.
- Modern society prioritizes intelligence (the genius) over wisdom (the sage), leading to a potential trade-off where one cannot easily optimize for both.
- Creative work (making new things) is inherently precarious and often leads to discontentment, unlike the traditional, wisdom-based work of administration or duty.

## Notable Claims

- Wisdom and intelligence are diverging due to the increasing specialization of knowledge. Evidence: As knowledge grows more specialized, the number of situations (points on the performance curve) increases, making the distinction between average performance and peak performance more pronounced.
- The path to intelligence requires nurturing idiosyncratic biases, while the path to wisdom requires removing them. Evidence: Wisdom relies on self-control and experience to eliminate random biases, whereas intelligence relies on curiosity and deep focus on specific, hard problems.
