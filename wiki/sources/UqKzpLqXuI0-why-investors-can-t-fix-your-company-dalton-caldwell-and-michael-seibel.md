# Why Investors Can’t Fix Your Company – Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel

## Summary

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel discuss why investors, including those at Y Combinator, cannot fix a company for a founder. They categorize common investor types—finance-focused, big-company executives, non-tech entrepreneurs, junior investors, influencers, and other founders—and explain how their backgrounds often lead to biased or misaligned advice. The core message is that founders must take personal accountability for their company's success, as investors can point out problems but cannot provide the 'silver bullet' solutions required to build a successful startup.

## Key Ideas

- Investors often give advice based on their own professional background rather than the specific needs of a pre-product-market-fit startup.
- Founders often seek 'silver bullets' or secrets from investors, but no investor can guarantee success.
- The best investor advice often involves synthesizing complex data to point out a clear problem, rather than providing a specific solution.
- Investors who are not financially incentivized to please the founder often provide the most honest and valuable feedback.
- Scaling a company is a different skill set than building from zero to one; advice from big-company veterans may not apply to early-stage startups.
- Founders must maintain personal accountability and not blindly follow any advice, including that from Y Combinator partners.

## Topics

- [[topics/investor-bias|Investor Bias]]: The tendency for investors to provide advice based on their past experiences (finance, big tech, etc.) rather than the current reality of the startup.
- [[topics/founder-accountability|Founder Accountability]]: The necessity for founders to take ownership of their company's direction rather than relying on investors to solve their problems.
- [[topics/zero-to-one-vs-scaling|Zero-to-One vs. Scaling]]: The fundamental difference in tactics required for early-stage startups versus established, large-scale companies.

## Notable Claims

- Investors with finance backgrounds often default to 'throwing money' at problems because it is their primary tool of leverage. Evidence: The hosts note that these investors often ask for financial projections before product-market fit and encourage spending on advertising without unit economics.
- Big-company executives often give bad advice to early-stage founders by suggesting they hire more executives before they have product-market fit. Evidence: The hosts argue that early-stage failure is usually due to the wrong person being in the role, not a lack of departments or executive layers.
- Junior investors often encourage founders to fundraise prematurely to boost their own KPIs and career prospects. Evidence: The hosts explain that junior investors need to show 'wins' to their firms, and marking up an investment via a new funding round is a common metric for them.
