# Inside YC's AI Playbook

## Summary

Y Combinator's Pete Koomen and Garry Tan discuss the internal transformation of YC into an 'AI-native' organization. They detail how building custom agentic infrastructure—centered on a shared database, a tool registry, and transparent, collaborative agent workflows—has empowered employees, accelerated onboarding, and created a 'shared organizational brain' that functions as a force multiplier for human intelligence.

## Key Ideas

- AI should be used as a foundational building layer for an organization, not just as a co-pilot.
- Centralizing organizational context in a single database allows agents to answer complex, cross-functional questions that were previously too time-consuming to ask.
- The 'multiplayer' agent harness is the next frontier, moving beyond single-user tools to team-wide collaboration.
- Transparency by default (making agent conversations viewable) fosters a high-trust environment and enables peer-to-peer learning.
- The 'Horseless Carriage' critique: AI software should shift control from the developer to the user, avoiding 'safetyism' that hides prompts and limits functionality.
- Just-in-time software: Future applications will be small, dynamic, and generated on-the-fly by agents rather than being monolithic, rigid codebases.
- The current era of AI development mirrors the early personal computer revolution, with a choice between centralized 'mainframe' control and decentralized, user-empowered 'personal AI'.

## Notable Claims

- Recording all organizational artifacts creates a 'shared brain' that allows employees to learn from the collective instinct of the team. Evidence: YC's internal use of meeting transcripts and agent logs to improve skills like the 'two-sentence pitch'.
- The current AI era is a 'one-time time warp' that allows startups to leapfrog incumbents by adopting agentic workflows. Evidence: The ability to automate complex financial and operational workflows that previously required significant human labor.
- Chat is the optimal interface for AI because it is the closest digital approximation to human language and thought. Evidence: The speakers' shift from doubting chat interfaces to realizing their effectiveness for complex, multi-modal tasks.
