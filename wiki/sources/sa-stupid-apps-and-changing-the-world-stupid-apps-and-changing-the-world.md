# Stupid Apps and Changing the World

## Metadata

## Summary

Sam Altman discusses the perception of 'trivial' startups in Silicon Valley, arguing that many world-changing technologies initially appear as toys due to the human inability to intuitively grasp hyperexponential growth.

## Key Ideas

- Many transformative technologies are dismissed as trivial or toys in their early stages.
- Human intuition fails to comprehend hyperexponential growth, which is characteristic of successful network-based products.
- There are two primary paths to changing the world: building something niche that users love (the 'toy' approach) or pursuing hyperambitious, capital-intensive projects.
- Founders should avoid claiming they are changing the world until they have actually achieved it.
- Market predictions are notoriously unreliable and should be ignored in favor of user engagement.

## Notable Claims

- Most people lack the intuition to understand hyperexponential growth. Evidence: The author notes that even exponential growth is difficult for humans to comprehend, and network value grows as a function of the square of the number of nodes.
- Market predictions are often historically inaccurate and costly. Evidence: Cites historical failures in predicting the market size for computers and cell phones.
