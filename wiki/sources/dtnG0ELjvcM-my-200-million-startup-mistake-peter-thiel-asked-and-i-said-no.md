# My $200 million startup mistake: Peter Thiel asked and I said no

## Summary

Garry Tan recounts his early career experience where he turned down an offer from Peter Thiel to join Palantir, a decision he estimates cost him $200 million in equity. He uses this anecdote to discuss the trade-offs between the stability of big tech companies and the high-risk, high-reward potential of early-stage startups.

## Key Ideas

- Big tech companies capture significantly more value from employees than they pay in salary.
- Equity ownership is the primary mechanism for capturing the true value created by software engineers and product builders.
- Working at large corporations can teach valuable skills like politics and negotiation, but may hinder the ability to build new things.
- Early-stage startups offer direct access to customers, fostering faster learning and problem-solving cycles.
- Young professionals should be wary of staying in big tech too long to avoid becoming 'soft' and losing the drive to innovate.
