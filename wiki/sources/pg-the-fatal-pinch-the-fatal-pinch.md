# The Fatal Pinch

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-the-fatal-pinch`

## Summary

Paul Graham defines the 'fatal pinch' as the critical period when a startup has limited runway, stagnant growth, and an over-reliance on future funding, which is often unlikely to materialize. He provides a framework for founders to recognize this state and take immediate, drastic action to reach profitability through cost-cutting, pivoting sales strategies, or engaging in 'consultingish' work.

## Key Ideas

- The fatal pinch occurs when a startup is burning cash with no growth and mistakenly assumes they can easily raise more money.
- Founders often delude themselves about investor interest; the default outcome for a struggling startup is failure.
- The best way to avoid the fatal pinch is to operate as if no further investment will ever be available.
- If in the fatal pinch, assume the probability of raising more money is zero.
- Survival strategies include firing staff, cutting salaries, or shifting focus to immediate revenue generation, including custom 'consultingish' work.
- Consulting work can be a bridge to survival, but founders must be careful not to slide into pure service-based business models.

## Notable Claims

- The probability of raising more money when in the 'fatal pinch' is zero. Evidence: Investors have higher standards for companies that have already raised money and the company is now perceived as a potential failure.
- Consulting work can save a failing product startup. Evidence: Startups often have superior technical talent compared to their customers, allowing them to charge for custom solutions while maintaining IP.
