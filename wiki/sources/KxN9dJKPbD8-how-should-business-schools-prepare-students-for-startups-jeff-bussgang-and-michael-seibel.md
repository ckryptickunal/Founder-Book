# How Should Business Schools Prepare Students for Startups? – Jeff Bussgang and Michael Seibel

## Summary

Y Combinator CEO Michael Seibel and HBS lecturer Jeff Bussgang discuss the disconnect between traditional MBA programs and the practical requirements for building successful tech startups. They analyze why MBA students often struggle with YC applications and explore how business schools can better prepare students for the realities of the startup ecosystem.

## Key Ideas

- MBA students often lack technical co-founders and rely on outsourcing development, which is a major red flag for investors.
- There is a tendency for MBA students to prioritize research and planning over building a Minimum Viable Product (MVP).
- Business schools often operate in silos, preventing the cross-pollination between business students and technical talent.
- The 'pedagogical' goal of business schools often coddles students, whereas the startup world requires high-stakes commitment and rapid iteration.
- For students not at top-tier schools, gaining experience at an early-stage startup is often more valuable than an MBA for a career in tech.
- There is a 'startup canon' of literature (e.g., Paul Graham, Steve Blank) that students should study if their formal curriculum is lacking.

## Notable Claims

- Business schools are doing a poor job of preparing students to start tech companies. Evidence: High volume of YC applications from MBAs that lack core startup tenets like technical co-founders or MVP focus.
- The purpose of a university is pedagogical, not necessarily to create billion-dollar startups. Evidence: Universities prioritize student learning and experimentation over the high-stakes, 'sink-or-swim' environment of venture-backed startups.
- If you are not at a top-five business school, an MBA may not be the best path for a tech career. Evidence: The network and credentialing benefits are strongest at top schools; otherwise, direct startup experience is more valuable.
