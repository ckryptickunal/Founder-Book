## Summary

Chris Dixon explains that the most successful startups often begin as 'good ideas that look like bad ideas.' Because obvious good ideas are already being pursued by large incumbents, entrepreneurs must find 'secrets'—contrarian insights derived from deep technical knowledge, specific problem-domain expertise, or personal experience—to build products that initially seem like toys, niche hobbies, or social norm violations.

## Key Ideas

- Good ideas that look like good ideas are already being worked on by large companies and governments.
- Startups succeed by finding 'secrets'—things you believe that most other people do not.
- Technical experts vote on the future with their time (hobbies/side projects), while business people vote with their dollars.
- Disruptive technologies often start as 'toys' that lack the features or quality required by incumbents, but improve rapidly over time.
- Many industries are 'bundles' of functions that get unbundled layer by layer by startups.
- The best startup ideas come from direct experience rather than abstract analysis or market trends.

## Topics

- [[topics/contrarian-investing|Contrarian Investing]]: The strategy of identifying high-potential opportunities that are currently dismissed by the mainstream.
- [[topics/the-innovator-s-dilemma|The Innovator's Dilemma]]: The tendency for incumbents to ignore low-quality, 'toy' technologies that eventually disrupt their markets.
- [[topics/unbundling|Unbundling]]: The process where startups pick off individual functions of a larger, established industry or organization.
- [[topics/social-engineering|Social Engineering]]: Security threats that exploit human behavior rather than technical vulnerabilities.

## Notable Claims

- Large incumbents often dismiss disruptive technologies as 'toys' because they do not meet current customer needs. Evidence: Western Union rejecting the telephone and incumbents dismissing early Skype.
- The best startup ideas are found by 'diffing' your direct experience against conventional wisdom. Evidence: Dixon's own experience with SiteAdvisor and the failure of incumbent security firms to address social engineering.
- University education will be unbundled over the next 10-20 years. Evidence: The layer-by-layer disruption of the newspaper industry as a historical parallel.
