## Summary

Michael Seibel, partner at Y Combinator, explains the core philosophy of the Minimum Viable Product (MVP). He emphasizes that an MVP should be a simple, fast-to-build tool designed to test if you can deliver value to initial users, rather than a perfect version of a long-term vision. He advocates for launching quickly, iterating based on real user feedback, and avoiding the trap of over-engineering or falling in love with the initial product.

## Key Ideas

- Launch something bad quickly; the goal is to get users interacting with your product as soon as possible.
- Hold the problem and the customer tightly, but hold the solution loosely.
- Distinguish between iterating (improving the solution to solve the problem) and pivoting (changing the problem or customer).
- Time-box your development specifications to ensure you launch within weeks, not months.
- Don't confuse a 'press launch' with a 'customer launch'; the latter is the only one that matters for early-stage startups.
- Write down your specifications to prevent scope creep and maintain accountability.

## Notable Claims

- Most founders' journeys end before a single user has interacted with their product. Evidence: Observation from Y Combinator's experience with thousands of startups.
- The initial version of a product is rarely what it eventually becomes. Evidence: Historical examples of Airbnb, Twitch, and Stripe.
- Spending time on a pitch deck is less valuable than building something for a customer. Evidence: Direct user feedback is the only way to validate if a solution actually solves a problem.
