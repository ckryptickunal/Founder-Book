## Summary

Mark Zuckerberg discusses the early days of Facebook, the importance of real identity and social connections, and his philosophy on management and startup strategy at Y Combinator's 2013 Startup School.

## Key Ideas

- Building products for oneself is a natural starting point for innovation.
- Real identity and social connections are the core value drivers for social networks.
- The most successful founders are often those who care the most about their mission, even when it seems irrational.
- Management is a skill learned through iteration and hiring people you can learn from.
- Focus on the one or two things that actually matter rather than getting distracted by complexity.
- Do not let mistakes demoralize you; focus on learning quickly and moving forward.

## Topics

- [[topics/startup-strategy|Startup Strategy]]: The importance of focus, mission-driven work, and ignoring non-essential competition.
- [[topics/management|Management]]: Learning to lead through trial and error, hiring, and developing heuristics.
- [[topics/social-graph|Social Graph]]: The theory that people want to connect with others and that real identity is the foundation of online communities.
- [[topics/internet-org|Internet.org]]: Facebook's initiative to connect the unconnected parts of the world.

## Notable Claims

- The best ideas often seem irrational or lack a clear business model at the start. Evidence: Zuckerberg cites the early days of Facebook and the current mission of Internet.org as examples.
- Real identity is superior to anonymity for building a lasting community. Evidence: Zuckerberg argues that real identity fosters more civilized and productive interactions.
- Clones can be a significant nuisance for growing startups. Evidence: Zuckerberg notes that international clones like Vkontakte in Russia have made it difficult for Facebook to gain market share.

## Quotes

> The most important thing is actually learning quickly from whatever mistakes you make and not giving up.
> My goal when I realized that I had a company was to get it to be a good company as quickly as possible.
> I think a lot of the reason why great stuff gets built is because it's kind of irrational at the time.
