# Founders of Science Exchange, Goldbely, and The Flex Company Discuss Fundraising

## Summary

Three Y Combinator alumni—Elizabeth Iorns (Science Exchange), Vanessa Torrivilla (Goldbelly), and Erica (The Flex Company)—discuss their experiences with fundraising across different company stages, emphasizing the importance of storytelling, metrics, and finding the right investor fit.

## Key Ideas

- Fundraising is similar to hiring; it is crucial to find investors who align with your mission and who you genuinely want to work with.
- The first check is often the hardest to secure; subsequent rounds become easier once you have established metrics and product-market fit.
- Storytelling for investors should focus on the business opportunity, market size, and economic impact rather than just the founder's personal passion.
- Preparation is key: know your financial models, KPIs, and unit economics cold before approaching investors for Series A.
- Founders should practice pitching to peers and lower-priority investors to refine their message and identify what resonates.
- Maintaining company operations during a fundraise requires a clear division of labor, often with one co-founder dedicated to fundraising while others focus on growth and operations.
- Burnout is a significant risk; maintaining a healthy work-life balance is essential for making better long-term decisions for the company.

## Notable Claims

- The first check is the hardest to close. Evidence: All three founders agreed that the seed stage was significantly more difficult than later rounds due to the lack of established metrics.
- Investors respond better to economic-based pitches than passion-based ones. Evidence: Elizabeth Iorns noted that shifting from a 'cool science' pitch to a 'market size and ROI' pitch significantly improved their fundraising success.
- Being profitable before a Series A provides leverage. Evidence: Vanessa Torrivilla mentioned that having worked out unit economics and achieving profitability helped speed up their Series A process.
