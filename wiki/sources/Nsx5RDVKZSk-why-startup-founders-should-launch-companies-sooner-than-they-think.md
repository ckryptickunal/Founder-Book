# Why Startup Founders Should Launch Companies Sooner Than They Think

## Metadata

## Summary

YC partners discuss why startup founders should launch their products earlier than they feel comfortable, debunking the myth that a launch is a singular, high-stakes event. They emphasize that launching is a tool for learning, diagnosing problems, and finding early adopters rather than a polished marketing spectacle.

## Key Ideas

- Founders often delay launching due to fear of failure or the misconception that they only get one chance to make a first impression.
- Big company launch models (like Apple) are inappropriate for startups; startups should prioritize speed and learning over perfection.
- A launch is not a one-time event; it is a continuous process of releasing, gathering feedback, and iterating.
- Early-stage sales are about filtering for the small group of users who have a 'hair on fire' problem, not convincing the masses.
- If a launch fails to gain traction, it is an analytical problem to solve (e.g., wrong messaging, wrong target audience) rather than a personal failure.
- The goal of an early launch should be learning, which removes the pressure of needing immediate commercial success.

## Notable Claims

- Most people do not care about a startup's initial launch. Evidence: Founders often cannot name the launch dates of major companies like Google or Uber, proving that initial launches are rarely remembered by the public.
- If you launch and nobody uses your product, it is not a failure but a diagnostic opportunity. Evidence: It allows founders to isolate variables like messaging, target audience, or product-market fit to adjust their hypothesis.
