# Google was saved by antitrust, but now pulls up the ladder on startups (w Jeremy Stoppelman of Yelp)

## Summary

Jeremy Stoppelman, founder of Yelp, discusses how Google transitioned from a startup-friendly search engine to a dominant monopoly that suppresses competition through self-preferencing, drawing parallels to the Microsoft antitrust era and advocating for legislative action to protect future startups.

## Key Ideas

- Google initially grew by indexing and sending traffic to high-quality startup content.
- Google shifted to a 'walled garden' strategy, prioritizing its own products (like Google Maps) over competitors like Yelp.
- Antitrust enforcement against Microsoft in the late 90s inadvertently created the market conditions that allowed Google to thrive.
- Google's current dominance and self-preferencing practices mirror the anti-competitive behavior Microsoft was once punished for.
- The American Innovation and Choice Online Act (S.2992) and the Open App Markets Act (S.2710) are critical for restoring a fair marketplace.
- Big Tech companies are 'pulling up the ladder' to prevent new startups from competing.

## Topics

- [[topics/antitrust-law|Antitrust Law]]: The application of the Sherman Act to prevent monopolies and ensure fair competition in the tech sector.
- [[topics/self-preferencing|Self-Preferencing]]: The practice of a platform owner favoring its own products or services over those of competitors.
- [[topics/startup-ecosystem|Startup Ecosystem]]: The environment required for new companies to innovate and grow without being stifled by incumbent giants.

## Notable Claims

- Google explicitly wrote code to demote Yelp in search results in favor of Google's own products. Evidence: Leaked FTC documents referenced by Jeremy Stoppelman.
- Google's rise was facilitated by the U.S. vs. Microsoft antitrust case. Evidence: The Microsoft case forced changes that allowed Firefox to gain market share, which Google then used for distribution.

## Quotes

> The number one beneficiary of Anti-Trust action over the last 20 years turns out to be pulling up the ladder on the next startups.
> What started as a Level Playing Field became less and less level and Google could preference their own products because they owned the field.
