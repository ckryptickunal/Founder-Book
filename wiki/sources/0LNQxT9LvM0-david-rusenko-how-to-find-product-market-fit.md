## Summary

David Rusenko, founder of Weebly, shares his practical experience and strategies for achieving product-market fit. He emphasizes that finding product-market fit is a difficult, non-theoretical process that requires listening to customer pain points, rapid iteration, and focusing on key metrics like returning usage and NPS rather than vanity metrics like signups.

## Key Ideas

- Product-market fit is the hardest problem for a startup and is found between the idea and traction phases.
- Listen to customer problems, not their proposed solutions.
- Optimize for learning rather than just incremental improvement when searching for product-market fit.
- Do not scale the team or worry about monetization until product-market fit is achieved.
- Use UX testing sessions (even with just 3-5 people) to identify critical usability issues.
- A 'Minimum Remarkable Product' is better than a 'Minimum Viable Product'—launch when your product is better than existing alternatives at solving a specific job.
- Founders should be 'calculated risk takers' who are optimistic and determined.

## Topics

- [[topics/product-market-fit|Product-Market Fit]]: The process of finding a market that wants your product, characterized by customers pulling the product toward them.
- [[topics/customer-development|Customer Development]]: The practice of interviewing customers to understand pain points and testing prototypes to validate hypotheses.
- [[topics/metrics|Metrics]]: Tracking returning usage, NPS, and renewal rates as indicators of success, while avoiding vanity metrics like signups.
- [[topics/startup-scaling|Startup Scaling]]: The strategy of keeping teams small until product-market fit is found, then scaling aggressively.

## Notable Claims

- Most startups fail because they don't find product-market fit. Evidence: Rusenko notes it is the top problem and is incredibly challenging, often taking years.
- You should not scale your team until you have product-market fit. Evidence: Scaling too early creates a fragile organization that cannot pivot effectively.
- Market research is ineffective for creating new markets. Evidence: Market research is designed for existing markets; creating a new market requires finding a hidden need.
