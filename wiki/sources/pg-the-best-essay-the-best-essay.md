## Summary

Paul Graham explores the nature of the 'best essay,' concluding that it is not a static, timeless piece of writing, but rather a process of discovery. He argues that great essays are driven by curiosity, the pursuit of novel and general insights, and a rigorous, recursive process of writing, questioning, and rewriting.

## Key Ideas

- The best essay is one that describes the most important, surprising discovery possible at the time.
- Writing is a tool for discovering ideas, not just a way to record them.
- The process of writing involves a recursive cycle: starting with a question, committing to a response, identifying gaps or errors, and refining until the truth is reached.
- Breadth of knowledge and depth of experience are essential for generating high-quality initial questions.
- Timelessness in essays is often a byproduct of being 'ineffective'—meaning the ideas haven't been fully assimilated into culture yet.
- Rewriting is the most critical phase of essay production; one should be quick to cut subtrees that do not yield novel or general insights.

## Notable Claims

- The best essay at any given time is one that describes the most important scientific or technological discovery possible. Evidence: Darwin's 1844 essay on natural selection is cited as the best essay of its time because it introduced a paradigm-shifting discovery.
- Writing is a way of converting ideas from vague to bad, which is a necessary step toward making them good. Evidence: The author explains that committing to a specific string of words allows the writer to see errors and refine them through rigorous rereading.
- Essays that are 'timeless' in an evergreen sense are often those that fail to be assimilated into the shared culture. Evidence: If an essay's ideas are successfully adopted (like Bertrand Russell's views on 'trial marriage'), they eventually become common knowledge and lose their ability to surprise future readers.

## Quotes

> Writing essays, at its best, is a way of discovering ideas.
> Writing converts your ideas from vague to bad. But that's a step forward, because once you can see the brokenness, you can fix it.
> The prize for being strict with what you've written is not just refinement. When you take a roughly correct answer and try to make it exactly right, sometimes you find that you can't, and that the reason is that you were depending on a false assumption.
