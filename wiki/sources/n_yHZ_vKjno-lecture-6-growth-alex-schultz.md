## Summary

Alex Schultz, VP of Growth at Facebook, discusses the fundamental principles of growth for startups. He emphasizes that retention is the most critical metric for growth, argues that startups should not have dedicated growth teams but rather focus on product-market fit, and outlines strategies for scaling through 'magic moments,' data-driven experimentation, and effective communication channels.

## Key Ideas

- Retention is the single most important metric for growth; if a product's retention curve does not flatten out, it lacks product-market fit.
- Startups should not have a dedicated growth team; the entire company, led by the CEO, should be the growth team.
- Identify and optimize for the 'magic moment'—the specific experience that hooks a user and keeps them coming back.
- Focus on the marginal user rather than the power user when designing growth tactics.
- Define a 'North Star' metric that aligns the entire company and reflects the core value of the product.
- Growth is a result of hard work, rapid experimentation, and violent execution rather than just clever tactics.

## Topics

- [[topics/retention|Retention]]: The primary indicator of product-market fit; measured by the percentage of users who remain active over time.
- [[topics/growth-hacking|Growth Hacking]]: The practice of using various channels and tactics to scale a product, which Schultz argues should be secondary to product quality.
- [[topics/north-star-metric|North Star Metric]]: A single, key metric that defines the success of a company and aligns all internal efforts.
- [[topics/virality|Virality]]: The mechanism by which a product spreads; analyzed through payload, frequency, and conversion rate.
- [[topics/seo|SEO]]: Search Engine Optimization; requires keyword research, high-authority links, and effective internal link distribution.

## Notable Claims

- Retention is the single most important thing for growth. Evidence: If a retention curve slopes down to the X-axis, the business is not viable regardless of growth tactics.
- Startups should not have a growth team. Evidence: The entire company should be the growth team, with the CEO setting the North Star.
- The same rules apply to every online marketing channel. Evidence: Frequency and conversion rates are inversely related; hitting users with the same message repeatedly leads to creative exhaustion.
- A good plan, violently executed today is better than a perfect plan tomorrow. Evidence: Facebook's success was driven by rapid experimentation and working harder than competitors.
