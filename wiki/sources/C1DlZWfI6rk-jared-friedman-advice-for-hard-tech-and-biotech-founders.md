## Summary

Jared Friedman, a partner at Y Combinator, provides a strategic framework for founders building hard-tech and biotech companies. He defines hard-tech as ventures requiring significant time and capital with inherent technical uncertainty, arguing that while these companies are harder to build, they are often easier to fund and recruit for than 'simple' software startups due to their ambitious nature.

## Key Ideas

- Hard-tech companies are defined by high technical risk and significant capital requirements, rather than just physical products.
- Ambitious, 'hard' problems often attract better talent and investor interest than mundane software ideas.
- Founders should avoid the 'all-or-nothing' fundraising trap by breaking capital requirements into smaller, milestone-driven rounds.
- Proving demand before building is critical; techniques include pre-sales, Letters of Intent (LOIs), and building low-cost MVP prototypes.
- Hard-tech startups can use 'hacks'—such as service-based revenue or using existing approved devices—to generate early data and credibility.

## Notable Claims

- It is often easier to start a hard-tech company than an easy one. Evidence: Ambitious missions attract top-tier talent and investor interest more effectively than generic software apps.
- Hard-tech companies have a 10x higher acceptance rate at YC than other applications. Evidence: Internal YC data suggests high-ambition founders are more likely to be accepted.
- Letters of Intent (LOI) are effective for validating enterprise demand. Evidence: They force customers to move beyond polite interest and reveal the actual decision-making process.
