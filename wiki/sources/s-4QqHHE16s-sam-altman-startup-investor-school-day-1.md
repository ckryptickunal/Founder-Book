## Summary

Sam Altman, President of Y Combinator, outlines his philosophy on angel investing, emphasizing the power law, the importance of founder quality, and the necessity of independent thinking over following market trends.

## Key Ideas

- Angel investing is a business of home runs, not singles; the power law dictates that one's best investment will outperform all others combined.
- The most successful investments often sound like bad ideas initially but are actually good ideas.
- Founders should be selected based on traits like obsession, focus, frugality, and love, rather than just current market size.
- Reputation is critical; founders perform reference checks on investors, and being helpful is a competitive advantage.
- Avoid the 'schooling effect' where investors blindly follow the herd; independent conviction is essential.
- A startup's growth rate and the founder's rate of improvement are more predictive of success than current revenue or market size.
- The best way to find great companies is through word-of-mouth referrals from other founders.

## Topics

- [[topics/power-law|Power Law]]: The principle that a small number of investments generate the vast majority of returns in a portfolio.
- [[topics/founder-selection|Founder Selection]]: Identifying traits like intelligence, communication skills, and execution speed in early-stage founders.
- [[topics/market-trends|Market Trends]]: Differentiating between real trends (high daily usage) and fake trends (hype without adoption).
- [[topics/investor-reputation|Investor Reputation]]: The importance of being helpful and ethical to secure access to the best deals.

## Notable Claims

- The number one mistake in angel investing is being swayed by what other investors think. Evidence: Altman notes that 80% of investors outsource their decision-making, leading to a 'schooling effect' where capital chases trends rather than value.
- It is easier to start a hard company than an easy one. Evidence: Ambitious people and talent are more attracted to solving difficult, interesting problems than to generic, crowded markets.
- Value investing is not a winning strategy for angel investors. Evidence: Altman's best investments were often those that felt expensive or were 'homeruns' rather than 'bargains'.
