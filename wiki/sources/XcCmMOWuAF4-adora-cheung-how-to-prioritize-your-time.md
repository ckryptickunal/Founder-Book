## Summary

Adora Cheung, a partner at Y Combinator, explains how startup founders should prioritize their time to maximize growth. She emphasizes distinguishing between 'real' progress (moving primary KPIs like revenue or active users) and 'fake' progress (vanity metrics, conferences, or networking), and provides a framework for ranking tasks based on impact and complexity.

## Key Ideas

- Time is the most precious resource for a startup; it must be spent on high-impact activities.
- Real startup progress is defined by growth in a primary KPI, typically revenue or active users.
- Founders should avoid 'fake' progress, such as attending conferences or optimizing vanity metrics.
- Prioritize tasks by evaluating their impact on the weekly goal and their complexity.
- Use a 'Maker's Schedule' to minimize context-switching costs between deep work like coding and high-interaction work like talking to users.
- Moving fast and learning from mistakes is superior to indecisiveness and over-analyzing.
- Regularly review weekly updates to identify patterns of low-value work or stagnation.

## Notable Claims

- Talking to users and building/iterating the product are the only two activities that constitute real startup progress. Evidence: These activities directly convert users to customers and inform the product roadmap, which drives primary KPIs.
- Humans have a natural instinct to choose low-value work because it is easier and provides a sense of accomplishment. Evidence: The psychological desire to check items off a list often leads founders to prioritize easy, low-impact tasks over difficult, high-impact ones.
- Context switching between coding and meetings is a major productivity killer. Evidence: The 'Maker's Schedule' concept highlights the high cost of restarting deep work after interruptions.
