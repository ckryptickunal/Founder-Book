## Summary

Karri Saarinen, co-founder and CEO of Linear, joins Y Combinator's Design Review to discuss how early-stage startups should approach brand design and website development. The core advice emphasizes authenticity, simplicity, and tailoring messaging to specific target audiences rather than mimicking mature companies.

## Key Ideas

- Startups should avoid trying to look like mature, large-scale companies too early, as it sets false expectations.
- The homepage should be a simple 'front page' that filters for the right users rather than explaining every feature.
- Use specific, jargon-heavy language that resonates with your ideal early adopters, even if it is not broad enough for a general audience.
- Design should evolve with the company; the initial website should be built quickly to focus effort on the product.
- Avoid distracting animations or 'chaotic' design elements that hinder readability or obscure the core value proposition.
- For non-technical audiences, focus on building trust and social proof rather than feature-heavy technical displays.

## Topics

- [[topics/brand-authenticity|Brand Authenticity]]: The importance of a brand reflecting the current stage and reality of the company.
- [[topics/landing-page-strategy|Landing Page Strategy]]: Balancing simplicity with enough information to capture interest without overwhelming the user.
- [[topics/targeted-messaging|Targeted Messaging]]: Using specific language to attract ideal customers while filtering out non-target users.
- [[topics/design-for-trust|Design for Trust]]: Using visual cues and social proof to establish credibility, especially for non-technical or enterprise audiences.

## Notable Claims

- Early-stage websites should be built in days, not months, to prioritize product development. Evidence: Linear's first website was created in a single day.
- Excessive animation on a landing page can distract users from the core value proposition. Evidence: Observation of Sprites AI and Dropback websites where motion obscured text.
- Enterprise buyers are not typically converted by random website visits. Evidence: Enterprise sales cycles usually require direct outreach, events, and trust-building materials.

## Quotes

> You should understand what stage of a company you are and what kind of people are you trying to find.
> The homepage should be for the people who don't really know about you. So it should give you some kind of enough information that you know that is this interesting or not.
> You're not designing in a vacuum of your page. You're designing in the context of what users are used to.
