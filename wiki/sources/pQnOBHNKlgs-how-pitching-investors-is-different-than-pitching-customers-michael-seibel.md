## Summary

Michael Seibel, partner at Y Combinator, explains the critical distinction between pitching to customers versus investors. He argues that because customers care about solving specific problems and investors care about building large, scalable businesses, founders must tailor their messaging, language, and focus accordingly to be successful.

## Key Ideas

- Customers prioritize problem-solving and functionality, while investors prioritize market size, business model, and team capability.
- Jargon is a tool for building credibility with customers but acts as a barrier to understanding for investors.
- Marketing fluff and flowery language should be avoided in investor pitches in favor of simple, concrete descriptions of what the product does.
- Founders should maintain separate pitches for their website/sales calls and their investor pitch decks.
- An effective investor pitch must address six core areas: what the product does, current traction, total addressable market, monetization, unique market insights, and team capability.

- [[topics/customer-sales|Customer Sales]]: The process of communicating product value to end-users, focusing on specific problem resolution and functionality.
- [[topics/communication-clarity|Communication Clarity]]: The importance of removing jargon and marketing fluff to ensure the core value proposition is understood by non-experts.

## Notable Claims

- Using the same pitch for both customers and investors is a common mistake for founders. Evidence: The motivations of the two groups are fundamentally different; customers want a solution to a problem, while investors want to know if a big business can be built.
- Jargon hurts investor pitches. Evidence: Investors may not have the specific problem the product solves and need to understand the business model, not just the technical industry terminology.

## Quotes

> The investor is trying to understand can you build a big business. The customer is trying to understand does your product solve their specific problem.
> Marketing language is unfortunately fluff that an investor has to filter out to understand what you really are trying to do.
> If your front page of your website and your pitch deck are saying the same thing... you might be doing it wrong.
