# How Blake Scholl Built The First Independent Supersonic Jet

## Summary

Blake Scholl, founder and CEO of Boom Supersonic, discusses the development of the XB-1, the first independently developed supersonic jet, and the company's mission to bring back supersonic passenger travel with the upcoming Overture airliner. Scholl explains how he transitioned from a software background to aerospace by applying first-principles thinking to overcome the economic failures of the Concorde.

## Key Ideas

- Supersonic passenger travel is economically viable today if efficiency is prioritized over the high-cost, low-efficiency model of the Concorde.
- Small, high-caliber teams can achieve breakthroughs in deep tech that large, legacy organizations struggle to innovate on.
- Founder motivation and passion are more critical than existing domain expertise, as skills can be learned if the mission is sufficiently compelling.
- The 'bystander effect' prevents many people from pursuing ambitious ideas, as they assume that if an idea were viable, someone else would have already done it.
- Prototyping (like the XB-1) is essential for learning and de-risking before scaling to a full-sized commercial product (Overture).

## Notable Claims

- The Concorde failed due to economics, not technology. Evidence: High ticket prices, low seating capacity, and poor fuel efficiency made it unsustainable.
- Overture will be as affordable as current business class travel. Evidence: Improved aerodynamic efficiency, modern materials, and sustainable fuel usage compared to the Concorde.
- Small teams can outperform large aerospace incumbents. Evidence: The XB-1 was built by a core team of approximately 50 people.
