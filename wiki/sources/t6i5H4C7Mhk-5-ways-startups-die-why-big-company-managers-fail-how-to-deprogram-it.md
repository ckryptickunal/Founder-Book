# 5 Ways Startups Die: Why Big Company Managers Fail (How to deprogram it)

## Summary

Garry Tan explains how corporate bureaucracy and 'corporatism'—characterized by risk aversion, credit-seeking, and buzzword-chasing—can kill startups. He advises founders transitioning from big companies to shift their focus from process and optics to tangible results, customer value, and accountability.

## Key Ideas

- Corporate environments often reward optics and 'looking busy' over actual productivity.
- The 'Not Invented Here' syndrome is driven by employees wanting to build internal tools to secure promotions rather than using superior existing solutions.
- Startups lack the 'air cover' of large companies; if the product fails, the company dies.
- Decision-makers in enterprise sales are often motivated by personal career advancement rather than just company ROI.
- Risk-averse cultures (CYA - Cover Your Ass) lead to disasters like the Challenger explosion due to poor data presentation and fear of accountability.
- True innovation requires working on things that are right but not yet popular, rather than following current industry buzzwords.

## Topics

- [[topics/corporatism|Corporatism]]: The spread of bureaucratic, risk-averse, and optics-driven behaviors from large corporations into startups.
- [[topics/not-invented-here-syndrome|Not Invented Here Syndrome]]: The tendency of employees to build internal solutions instead of adopting better external ones to gain personal credit and job security.
- [[topics/accountability-culture|Accountability Culture]]: The balance between holding people responsible for results and creating a safe environment for calculated risk-taking.
- [[topics/enterprise-sales-strategy|Enterprise Sales Strategy]]: Understanding that enterprise buyers are motivated by personal career advancement and promotion potential.

## Notable Claims

- Corporate bureaucracy costs approximately three trillion dollars per year in productivity. Evidence: Cited a 2016 Harvard Business Review study.
- People value their own created solutions significantly more than objectively better external ones. Evidence: Referenced experiments by Professor Dan Ariely.
- The Challenger space shuttle disaster was exacerbated by poor data presentation that obscured critical failure patterns. Evidence: Referenced Edward Tufte's analysis of the Morton Thiokol PowerPoint slides.
