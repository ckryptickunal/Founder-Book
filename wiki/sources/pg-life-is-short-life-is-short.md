# Life is Short

## Metadata

## Summary

Paul Graham explores the finiteness of life, arguing that the limited nature of time—quantified through experiences like raising children—should compel individuals to ruthlessly eliminate 'bullshit,' prioritize meaningful activities, and savor the present moment.

## Key Ideas

- Life is objectively short, and this can be measured by discrete events rather than just continuous time.
- The concept of 'bullshit' refers to time-wasting activities, whether forced by circumstances or self-imposed through bad habits.
- Online arguments and social posturing are common traps that steal time and energy.
- One should prioritize 'things that matter'—like relationships and personal passions—over prestige or money.
- To combat the shortness of life, one should cultivate impatience for doing what matters and practice savoring the present.
- Regret often stems from failing to spend time with loved ones while they are still present.

## Notable Claims

- Life is objectively short, and this is best understood by converting time into discrete, limited experiences. Evidence: The author uses the example of having only 52 weekends with a two-year-old or a limited number of Christmases with a child.
- Arguing online is a form of 'bullshit' that wastes life. Evidence: The author notes that defending oneself against online attacks is an instinctual but counterproductive behavior that consumes limited time.
- The most effective way to avoid regret is to cultivate impatience for the things you truly want to do. Evidence: The author suggests that instead of dwelling on death, one should act immediately on personal goals like writing a book or visiting family.

## Quotes

> If you find yourself thinking that life is too short for something, you should try to eliminate it if you can.
> The things that matter aren't necessarily the ones people would call 'important.' Having coffee with a friend matters.
> Relentlessly prune bullshit, don't wait to do things that matter, and savor the time you have.
