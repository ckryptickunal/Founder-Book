# Lecture 16 - How to Run a User Interview (Emmett Shear)

## Summary

Emmett Shear, CEO of Twitch, explains the critical importance of conducting user interviews to identify real problems rather than just building features. He emphasizes that startups should focus on understanding user behavior and pain points, particularly by talking to non-users and competitors' users, rather than relying on internal assumptions or data analytics alone.

## Key Ideas

- Talk to users to identify problems, not to validate features or ask for feature requests.
- The 'horseless carriage' effect: users often ask for faster horses rather than the actual solution to their underlying problem.
- Record interviews to share insights with the team and avoid disruptive note-taking.
- Focus on three groups: current users, users of competitors, and non-users (the largest group).
- Avoid asking 'is this feature good?' as users will often be polite and say yes, even if they won't use it.
- The most important feedback often comes from people who are not currently using your product.
- The target user group shifts over time; continue interviewing as the company scales.

## Notable Claims

- Most startups fail because they do not talk to users. Evidence: Shear notes that he and his co-founders failed to talk to users in their first startup, Kiko Calendar.
- Recording interviews is more effective than taking notes. Evidence: It allows the team to hear the user's tone and context directly, which is more persuasive than written summaries.
- Asking users if they like a feature is a trap. Evidence: Users are often polite and will say yes, but this does not predict actual adoption or willingness to switch products.

## Quotes

> Who you talk to is as important as what questions you ask.
> If you're not five-dollars-excited about it, you're probably not very excited about it.
> The most interesting things you learn in interviews come from the 'tell me more'.
> Don't show them your product. It's sort of like telling them about a feature, you want to learn about what's already in their heads.
