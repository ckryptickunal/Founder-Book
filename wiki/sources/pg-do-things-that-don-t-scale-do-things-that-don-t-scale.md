# Do Things that Don't Scale

## Metadata

- Video ID: `pg-do-things-that-don-t-scale`
- Channel: Paul Graham Essays
- Published: July 2013
- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pg-do-things-that-don-t-scale

## Summary

Paul Graham argues that successful startups rarely take off on their own; instead, founders must manually recruit early users and provide an 'insanely great' experience to gain traction. He emphasizes that early-stage startups are fragile and that founders should embrace unscalable, laborious tasks—such as manual onboarding, consulting-style support, and even manual service delivery—to learn what to build and to establish a culture of customer obsession.

## Key Ideas

- Startups require a 'crank' to get going; they rarely grow automatically at the start.
- Founders must manually recruit users rather than waiting for them to arrive.
- Early-stage growth should be measured by weekly percentage growth rates.
- Startups should focus on a narrow, specific market to build a 'contained fire' before expanding.
- Delighting early users is a competitive advantage that large companies cannot replicate.
- Perfectionism is often an excuse for procrastination; launch early and iterate based on feedback.
- Startup ideas should be viewed as vectors: the product plus the unscalable effort required to launch it.
- Avoid the 'Big Launch' mentality; focus on user happiness over initial press coverage.

## Notable Claims

- Startups should not rely on 'Big Launches' for success. Evidence: Famous launches like the Segway and Google Wave were often flops, while successful companies grew through gradual, manual user acquisition.
- Founders must do sales and marketing themselves initially. Evidence: Hiring someone else to do sales early on is ineffective; the founder needs the direct feedback loop.
- Delighting customers scales better than expected. Evidence: The effort spent on early users permeates the company culture and creates a foundation for future growth.

## Quotes

> Startups take off because the founders make them take off.
> The question to ask about an early stage startup is not 'is this company taking over the world?' but 'how big could this company get if the founders did the right things?'
> You should take extraordinary measures not just to acquire users, but also to make them happy.
> Perfectionism is often an excuse for procrastination.
